## 1. Taxonomy of Declarative Memory

**Declarative (explicit) memory**: the conscious, intentional recollection of factual information, previous experiences, and concepts. It depends on three core processes -- **acquisition**, **consolidation**, and **retrieval** -- and divides into **episodic** and **semantic** memory. [EXAM]

### 1.1 Declarative vs. Nondeclarative Memory

- **Declarative (explicit) memory**: conscious recollection of facts and events. Depends on the **medial temporal lobe** (especially the **hippocampus**). Spared in nondeclarative tasks. [EXAM]
- **Nondeclarative (implicit) memory**: includes priming, conditioning, skill learning. **Priming**: facilitated processing of previously encountered stimuli. **Skill learning**: learning cognitive, motor, or perceptual skills through practice. **Conditioning**: learning associations between stimuli or between actions and consequences (e.g., **Pavlov's dog**).

### 1.2 Episodic Memory [EXAM]

- Memories of **events** experienced at a specific **place** and **time**
- Introduced as a concept by psychologist **Endel Tulving**
- **Recollection**: remembering contextual details -- what, where, when [EXAM]
- **Familiarity**: a sense of having experienced something before without recalling specific contextual details [EXAM]
- Familiarity is classified as episodic, but its encoding mechanisms resemble **semantic memory** [PROF EMPHASIS]

### 1.3 Semantic Memory [EXAM]

- Memory for **facts** and **general knowledge** about the world
- **Semanticisation**: the process by which episodic memories transform into semantic memories over time [EXAM]
  - Day 1: "Yesterday at school I learned that Paris is the capital of France" (episodic)
  - Over time: "Paris is the capital of France" (semantic) -- the episodic context fades [PROF EMPHASIS]

### 1.4 Autobiographical Memory

- The **overlap** between episodic and semantic memory
- Contains both personal event details (episodic) and personal factual knowledge (semantic)

## 2. A Simple Neurological Model of Encoding, Storage, and Retrieval [EXAM]

### 2.1 Encoding Phase

- Different aspects of an event are stored in the **same cortical regions** involved in perceiving the event:
  - Visual information → **visual cortex**
  - Auditory information → **auditory cortex**
  - Motor information → **motor cortex**
- Memory traces consist of **alterations in synapses** (strengthened connections between neurons)
- The **hippocampus** stores a **summary representation (index)** with pointers to the distributed cortical traces [EXAM]
  - This index is not the memory itself but a pointer to where the information can be found -- like a book index

### 2.2 Storage Phase (Including Consolidation)

- Some memory traces are stabilised through **consolidation**
- Recent memories depend heavily on hippocampal indices for reactivation
- Remote memories develop **direct connections among cortical traces**, becoming less dependent on the hippocampus

### 2.3 Retrieval Phase

- A **retrieval cue** accesses the hippocampal index, leading to simultaneous reactivation of cortical traces
- For **remote memories**, retrieval can bypass the hippocampus via direct cortical connections

## 3. Medial Temporal Lobe and Hippocampal Function

### 3.1 Key Brain Regions [EXAM]

- **Hippocampus**: hub for binding; stores indices/pointers to cortical memory traces
- **Medial temporal lobe** (perirhinal, entorhinal, parahippocampal cortices): forms memory representations around the hippocampus
- **Prefrontal cortex (PFC)**: organisation of memories; contributes to encoding and retrieval
- **Sensory cortical areas**: store modality-specific traces (visual, auditory, etc.)

### 3.2 Theories of Hippocampal Function [EXAM]

| Theory | Key Claim | Evidence |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Relational memory theory** | Hippocampus mediates memory for **new associations** in general | Remembering nonspatial associations activates the hippocampus |
| **Episodic memory theory** (Tulving, Moscovitch) | Hippocampus is critical for **episodic memory but not semantic** memory | Hippocampal damage leads primarily to episodic impairment |

- Both theories are supported -- HC is associated with **recollection** rather than **familiarity** [EXAM]

### 3.3 Recollection vs. Familiarity: Neural Evidence [EXAM]

- **fMRI studies**: "Remember" (R) responses elicit greater **hippocampal activity** than "Know" (K) responses
- **Rat lesion studies**: Selective hippocampal lesions impair **recollection estimates** but not **familiarity estimates** (Fortin et al., 2004)
- Converging evidence links the hippocampus to recollection, supporting both relational and episodic memory theories

### 3.4 Differences Between Medial Temporal Lobe Subregions [EXAM]

- **Hippocampus**: processes information relatively slowly; relational and spatial; signals associations between items (recollection)
- **Perirhinal cortex**: processes information rapidly; item-based; signals novelty of individual items (familiarity)
  - Single-cell recordings show perirhinal neurons respond more strongly to **first presentations** than to repeated presentations

## 4. Key Patient Cases [EXAM]

### 4.1 Patient H.M.

- Bilateral **medial temporal lobe** removal led to severe **anterograde amnesia**
- Could not form new declarative memories (episodic or semantic)
- Nondeclarative memory (priming, skill learning) was spared
- Demonstrates the critical role of the medial temporal lobe in declarative memory [EXAM]

### 4.2 Patient K.C. [EXAM]

- Motorcycle accident causing damage to multiple brain regions including the **hippocampus**
- **Could not remember events** before or after the accident (anterograde + retrograde amnesia for episodic memory)
- **Semantic memory acquired before the accident was intact**: good vocabulary, knowledge of math, history, geography
- Could learn simple semantic associations with many repetitions
- Demonstrates that medial temporal lobe damage can impair episodic memory while **sparing retrograde semantic memory** [EXAM]

### 4.3 Beth, Jon, and Kate -- Developmental Amnesia [EXAM]

- Children with **hypoxia-related selective hippocampal damage** (hippocampus has higher metabolic rate)
- **Severely impaired** in episodic memory
- **Normal** speech, language, literacy, factual knowledge -- could attend mainstream schools
- Supports the idea that the hippocampus is specifically associated with **episodic but not semantic** memory
- Semantic memory may be mediated by **medial temporal lobe cortices** (perirhinal, entorhinal, parahippocampal) [EXAM]
- Supports the view that **episodic memory develops after semantic memory** [EXAM]

### 4.4 Semantic Dementia (Patient A.M.)

- Progressive disorder with **left anterior temporal atrophy**
- Severely impaired in **nonverbal semantic knowledge** (pyramid and palm trees test)
- Performed normally on **nonverbal episodic memory** tasks
- **Double dissociation with hippocampal amnesia**: hippocampus is more critical for episodic memory; **left anterior temporal cortex** is more critical for semantic memory [EXAM]

## 5. Contributions of the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) to Encoding and Retrieval [EXAM]

### 5.1 PFC and Episodic Memory Encoding

- PFC is known for many functions that relate to memory directly or indirectly [PROF EMPHASIS]
- **Subsequent memory paradigm**: compares brain activity for information that is **subsequently remembered** vs. **subsequently forgotten** [EXAM]
  - Four-step experimental design to isolate neural regions for episodic encoding
  - Key region: **inferior frontal gyrus** (also medial temporal lobe, dorsal parietal lobe)

### 5.2 PFC and Episodic Memory Retrieval

- **Memory search**: retrieval cues narrow attention, leading to recovery of stored memory traces
- **Monitoring processes** evaluate recovered information and reject inappropriate memories
- **Retrieval mode**: a hypothetical state that maintains continuous attention and focus on the past

## 6. Contributions of the Posterior Parietal Cortex (PPC) to Encoding and Retrieval [EXAM]

### 6.1 During Retrieval

- **Dorsal parietal cortex**: associated with **familiarity** and **low-confidence recognition**; involved in **top-down attention**
- **Ventral parietal cortex**: associated with **recollection** and **high-confidence recognition**; involved in **bottom-up attention** [EXAM]
- **Attention to Memory (AToM) model**: ventral parietal cortex mediates bottom-up attention to recovered memories [EXAM]

### 6.2 Ventral Parietal Lesion Evidence

- Patients produced **fewer episodic details** when describing autobiographical memories
- BUT could generate the missing details when **specific questions** were posed
- Produced fewer "remember" responses, but performed normally on **source memory tasks**
- Conclusion: episodic details were **available but not spontaneously attended** [EXAM]

### 6.3 The Encoding-Retrieval Flip [EXAM]

- **Dorsal parietal regions**: greater activity for subsequently **remembered** items (positive SMEs) during encoding
- **Ventral parietal regions**: greater activity for subsequently **forgotten** items (negative SMEs) during encoding
- The same ventral parietal regions that show **retrieval success** show **encoding failure**
- Explanation (AToM model):
  - During **retrieval**: bottom-up attention captures attention by recovered memories → success
  - During **encoding**: bottom-up attention reflects **distraction** from items meant to be encoded → failure [EXAM]

## 7. Memory Consolidation [EXAM]

### 7.1 Synaptic vs. System Consolidation [EXAM]

| Feature | Synaptic Consolidation | System Consolidation |
|---------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Timescale** | Fast -- more than 90% within ~90 minutes | Slow -- average 80-90% in ~28 days; can take years |
| **Mechanism** | Stabilisation of synaptic changes | Change in which brain regions are involved in recall |
| **Location** | At the synapse | Medial temporal lobe to cortex |

### 7.2 Ribot's Law

- Memory loss following brain damage affects **recent memories more than remote memories** -- a **temporal gradient** [EXAM]
- Described by French psychologist **Theodule-Armand Ribot** (1882)

### 7.3 Standard Consolidation Theory [EXAM]

- For **recent** memories: cortical traces are disconnected; reactivation requires the **hippocampal index**
- For **remote** memories: repeated reactivation establishes **direct connections among cortical traces**; cortical traces can be reactivated independently of the medial temporal lobe
- "Transfer" of memories is not a physical transfer; what is transferred is the **ability to enact the reactivation process** independently [PROF EMPHASIS]

### 7.4 Multiple Trace Theory (Nadel & Moscovitch, 1997) [EXAM]

- **Episodic memory always depends on the hippocampus**, regardless of age of the memory [EXAM]
- Each **reactivation creates a new memory trace** in the hippocampus
- For **semantic memories**: process is similar to standard theory -- hippocampal connections become unnecessary after cortical-cortical connections are established
- **Older memories are more resistant** to partial hippocampal damage because they have **more traces** [EXAM]
- **Complete hippocampal loss** results in **total retrograde amnesia for episodic memory** [PROF EMPHASIS]

### 7.5 Memory Replay and Sleep [EXAM]

- Consolidation does not only happen before **active recall** [PROF EMPHASIS]
- **Memory replay**: activation of memory pathways during **rest periods** -- sleep, resting, naps, or active rest
- Memory reactivation during sleep promotes consolidation (Rasch et al. 2007 -- odor cues during slow-wave sleep prompt declarative memory consolidation)

## 8. Priming: Perceptual vs. Conceptual [EXAM]

### 8.1 Perceptual Priming

- Relies on **different brain systems** than declarative memory (especially episodic memory)
- Depends on **sensory regions of the cortex** (e.g., visual areas) [EXAM]
- Key evidence -- Double dissociation:
  - Patient **M.S.** (right occipital lobe damage): **no priming** on implicit test, but **normal** performance on explicit test
  - **Amnesia patients** (medial temporal lobe damage): **showed priming** but **impaired** on explicit test
- Conclusion: perceptual priming depends on **sensory cortical regions**, not the medial temporal lobe [EXAM]

### 8.2 Conceptual Priming

- Depends on **different brain regions** than perceptual priming [EXAM]
- **Preserved in amnesia** patients with medial temporal lobe damage
- **Impaired in Alzheimer's disease**: neural deterioration extends beyond medial temporal lobe to **lateral temporal** and **prefrontal cortices**
- **Conceptual priming** has been linked to the **anterior portion of the left inferior frontal gyrus** -- an area strongly associated with **semantic processing** [EXAM]

---

## 9. Cortical Storage of Semantic and Episodic Memory

- Semantic knowledge is distributed across cortical regions
- Left anterior temporal cortex is critical for semantic memory (evidence from semantic dementia)
- Recent episodic memories reactivate the cortical regions originally involved in encoding

## Key Takeaways [EXAM]

1. **Declarative memory** divides into **episodic** (events) and **semantic** (facts) memory, both depending on the medial temporal lobe but with distinct neural substrates -- the hippocampus is more critical for episodic memory, while anterior temporal cortex is more critical for semantic memory
2. The **hippocampus** acts as an **index/pointer system**, not as the storage site itself -- it binds distributed cortical traces together and enables their simultaneous reactivation during retrieval
3. **Recollection** (contextual detail) is hippocampus-dependent, while **familiarity** (sense of knowing) is more associated with the **perirhinal cortex** -- a key double dissociation
4. **Standard consolidation theory** proposes that remote memories become hippocampus-independent through the formation of direct cortical-cortical connections, while **multiple trace theory** argues that episodic memories always require the hippocampus, with older memories being more resilient due to having more hippocampal traces
5. **Memory replay** during sleep and rest is a critical consolidation mechanism beyond active recall
6. The **PFC** contributes to encoding (inferior frontal gyrus, subsequent memory paradigm) and retrieval (monitoring, retrieval mode), while the **posterior parietal cortex** shows a dorsal-ventral dissociation with an "encoding-retrieval flip" explained by the **AToM model**
7. **Perceptual priming** depends on sensory cortices and is spared in amnesia; **conceptual priming** depends on prefrontal cortices and is impaired in Alzheimer's but spared in amnesia -- a critical double dissociation
8. Key patient cases to know: **H.M.** (bilateral MTL removal → severe amnesia), **K.C.** (hippocampal damage → episodic impairment with spared semantic memory), **Beth/Jon/Kate** (developmental amnesia → episodic impairment with normal semantic learning), and **semantic dementia patients** (anterior temporal atrophy → semantic impairment with spared episodic memory)
