## 3. Mock Exam Walkthrough -- Key Concepts

### 3.1 Event-Related Potentials (ERPs) [EXAM]

- **Mock exam question**: What are ERPs?
- **Correct answer**: ERPs are **raw EEG signals averaged across multiple trials** [EXAM]
- Key distinctions:
  - ERPs = raw EEG averaged across **trials** (time-domain averaging)
  - NOT EEG spectra (that would be frequency-domain analysis)
  - NOT averaged across recording sites (that would lose spatial information)
- **Why this works**: The signal of interest is **time-locked** to the event, while background noise is random. Averaging cancels out noise and reveals the consistent neural response

### 3.2 Causation vs. Correlation in Neuroscience Methods [EXAM]

This was the most extensively discussed mock exam question and represents a core exam theme.

**Mock exam scenario**: A researcher hypothesises that the **lateral prefrontal cortex** is **causally** involved in **self-control during food choices**.

**Question**: Which method is most appropriate?

| Option | Method | Verdict |
|--------|--------|---------|
| **A (CORRECT)** | **TMS** -- testing participants twice, once with real TMS and once with **sham TMS** of the lateral prefrontal cortex | Establishes a **causal** link by actively manipulating brain activity |
| B | **fMRI** -- testing participants before and after depleting self-control | Only shows **correlation**, not causation |
| C | **fMRI** -- comparing brain-damaged vs. healthy participants | Lesion studies have interpretation problems; not a controlled experimental manipulation |
| D | **fMRI** -- comparing strong vs. weak self-control groups | Only shows **correlation** between brain activity and behavior |

**Critical distinction** [PROF EMPHASIS]:
- **fMRI** can show a **correlation** between self-control and lateral prefrontal cortex activity -- not necessarily a causal link
- **TMS** establishes **causation** by actively up-regulating or down-regulating activity in that particular area

### Key terms from this question:

- **TMS (Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation)**: Non-invasive extracranial brain stimulation that can temporarily **up-regulate or down-regulate** activity in targeted brain areas [EXAM]
- **Sham TMS**: Placebo condition -- "Like a placebo drug." [EXAM]
- **Lateral prefrontal cortex**: Associated with **self-control** and executive functions [PROF EMPHASIS]

## 4. Research Methods -- Comprehensive Overview [EXAM]

### Category 1: Brain Perturbation Methods (Can Test Causation)

| Method | What It Does | Causal? | Key Details |
|--------|-------------|---------|-------------|
| **Clinical lesion studies** | Observe cognitive changes after brain damage | Suggestive but not experimentally controlled | Must interpret with care; need consistency across patients |
| **Pharmacological perturbation** | Agonists activate receptors; antagonists block them | Yes (chemical) | "An agonist will activate a particular receptor. An antagonist will block that receptor." [PROF EMPHASIS] |
| **Intracranial brain stimulation** | Direct electrical stimulation inside the brain | Yes (invasive) | Used in both humans (clinical) and animal models |
| **TMS (extracranial stimulation)** | Magnetic pulses disrupt or enhance cortical activity from outside the skull | **Yes** (non-invasive) | Key method for establishing causation in human studies |
| **Optogenetics** | Light-activated proteins control specific neurons | Yes | Primarily in animal models |

### Category 2: Measuring Brain Activity (Observing Correlations)

| Method | What It Measures | Temporal Resolution | Spatial Resolution |
|--------|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Single-neuron recording** | Individual neuron firing | Excellent (ms) | Excellent (but tiny area) |
| **EEG** | Electrical activity at scalp | Excellent (ms) | Poor |
| **ERPs** | Averaged EEG time-locked to events | Excellent (ms) | Poor |
| **MEG** | Magnetic fields from neural activity | Excellent (ms) | Better than EEG |
| **PET** | Metabolic activity via radioactive tracers | Poor | Moderate |
| **fMRI** | Blood oxygenation changes (BOLD signal) | Moderate (seconds) | Good |

### The Inverse Problem [EXAM]

Applies to **both EEG and MEG**: A distribution of scalp-recorded electrical (or magnetic) activity **could have been produced by any configuration of generator(s)**. You cannot uniquely determine the source from the surface recording alone.

## 5. Lesion Studies -- Strengths and Limitations [EXAM]

**Strengths**:
- Provide extremely valuable information about what happens when the system is "actually broken" [PROF EMPHASIS]
- Can reveal which brain regions are **necessary** for specific functions
- Remain "highly relevant" despite their limitations

**Limitations** [PROF EMPHASIS]:
- Individual cases can be misleading -- need **consistency across patients**
- Brain damage is rarely clean -- typically affects multiple areas and connections
- Cannot establish what a region does, only what goes wrong without it

## 6. Historical Progression of the Field [EXAM]

| Era | Core Idea | Key Figures | Limitations |
|-----|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Natural Philosophy / Early Psychology** (19th c.) | Behavioral observation, psychophysical methods | Helmholtz, Wundt, James | Lacked rigorous experimental control |
| **Behaviourism** (early 20th c.) | Only study stimulus to response; reject internal mental states | Skinner, Watson | Could not account for complex mental phenomena |
| **Cognitive Science** (mid 20th c.) | Internal representations are legitimate; build cognitive models | Chomsky, Miller | Models had no brain basis -- purely abstract psychological constructs |
| **Cognitive Neuroscience** (late 20th c.) | Ground cognitive models in neurobiology | Interdisciplinary | Current paradigm |

### The Pivotal Transition: Chomsky's Critique [EXAM]

- **Chomsky's 1959 review** of Skinner's *Verbal Behavior*
- Argued that behaviourism could never explain the **structural and generative properties** of mental phenomena like language
- This revived the legitimacy of studying **internal mental processes**
- Marked the critical shift from behaviourism to cognitive science
- The professor presented this as **the key turning point** in the history of the field

---

## 7. Convergence and Complementarity [EXAM]

- **Convergence**: Using **multiple different methods** to study the same cognitive function. When results from different approaches point to the same conclusion, confidence increases significantly
- **Complementarity**: Different methods have different **strengths and weaknesses** (e.g., EEG has excellent temporal but poor spatial resolution, while fMRI has the reverse). Using them together provides a more complete picture
- **Meta-analysis**: A formal statistical technique for combining results across multiple studies

## Key Takeaways [EXAM]

1. **Exam scope**: History/Methodology (Ch. 1, Module 1), Motor Control (Ch. 5), and Memory (Ch. 8-9). Emotional neuroscience is NOT on the midterm [EXAM]
2. **"Best answer" format**: Multiple options may be partially correct; you must choose the MOST appropriate one. Read boldfaced terms carefully [PROF EMPHASIS]
3. **Causation vs. correlation is the most heavily tested methodological distinction**: TMS and stimulation methods establish **causation**; fMRI, EEG, and other measurement methods establish **correlation** only. When you see "causally" in a question, think perturbation methods [EXAM]
4. **ERPs are raw EEG signals averaged across multiple trials** -- know this definition precisely [EXAM]
5. **Sham TMS** functions as a placebo control and is essential for experimental validity in TMS studies [EXAM]
6. **The Inverse Problem** (EEG and MEG): You cannot uniquely determine the neural source from surface recordings alone. Multiple source configurations can produce identical scalp patterns [EXAM]
7. **Historical progression**: Know the Behaviourism to Cognitive Science transition, especially **Chomsky's 1959 critique of Skinner's *Verbal Behavior*** as the pivotal moment [EXAM]
8. **Lesion studies** are highly relevant but must be interpreted with care; individual cases can be misleading, and you need consistency across patients [PROF EMPHASIS]
