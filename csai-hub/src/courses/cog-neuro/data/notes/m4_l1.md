## 1. What is Emotion?

The professor opened by warning that everyone has an intuitive sense of "emotion," so an explicit scientific definition is required first. [PROF EMPHASIS]

**Emotion** is formally defined as: *sets of physiological responses, action tendencies, and subjective feelings that adaptively engage humans and other animals to react to events of biological and/or individual significance.* [EXAM]

This breaks down into **three components** (the prof drew them as three overlapping circles):

| Component | Description | Example (fear) |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Physiological response** | Autonomic / bodily change | Sweating, heart rate up, pupil dilation |
| **Action tendency** | Behavioural disposition | Fight or flight |
| **Subjective feeling** | Conscious experience | "I feel afraid" |

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "These three things together is how you then decide to react to an event." The integration of body + behaviour + feeling is what defines a healthy emotional response. In schizophrenia, for instance, these components are dissociated (high autonomic arousal but reduced motor output).

Functionally, emotions are **dispositions toward behaviours that help an organism deal with biologically significant events** — they redistribute blood flow, release stress hormones, increase sensory vigilance, and prepare reflexes. They are typically triggered by environmental events but can also be evoked by **memories or thoughts**.

## 2. Classification of Emotions: Basic vs Complex

The literature splits emotions into two broad categories. [EXAM]

| Feature | **Basic emotions** | **Complex emotions** |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Origin | Innate | Learned |
| Cultural reach | Pan-cultural | Socially / culturally shaped |
| Evolution | Evolutionarily old | Evolutionarily new |
| Species | Shared with other animals | Most evident in humans |
| Expression | Specific physiological + facial patterns | Combinations of basic-emotion patterns |
| Example | Fear, happiness | Pride, shame, guilt |

The **six basic emotions** (the canonical set, though taxonomies range from 4 to 9): **anger, sadness, happiness, fear, disgust, surprise**. [EXAM]

> [PROF EMPHASIS] The prof noted babies already express basic emotions before they can think "this is what I feel" — the expression is innate. Complex emotions like **pride** depend on cultural context (e.g. some East Asian cultures discourage personal pride) and are harder to recognize because their facial signatures are less defined.

Even basic emotions take nuanced forms in humans: **moral disgust** (curled upper lip, response to violations of integrity) is anatomically distinguishable from **physical disgust** (wrinkled nose, response to bad tastes/odors).

## 3. Dimensional and Component-Process Theories

### Dimensional theories
Each emotion is a **point in a continuous space** defined by two or more dimensions — most often **arousal** (intensity, regardless of pleasantness) and **valence** (positive vs negative). [EXAM]

| Model | Key feature | Limitation |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| **Vector model** | Two axes meeting at a neutral endpoint forming a "boomerang"; arousal and valence are tightly coupled (low arousal → automatically neutral valence) | Assumes valence and arousal scale together |
| **Circumplex model** (Russell) | Orthogonal arousal × valence axes; emotions arranged around a circle's circumference; four quadrants | More nuanced — allows e.g. low arousal + high valence (calm/content) |

Both models share the underlying assumption that an emotion is a **static point** that can be pinpointed on the graph.

### Component-process theories
A different framework: emotions are **fluid, not fixed states**, and arise from the interaction of multiple **appraisal processes**. [EXAM]

Appraisal criteria (from book Table 10.1) include:
- **Novelty**
- **Pleasantness**
- **Goal significance** (outcome certainty, conduciveness, urgency)
- **Coping potential** (agency, control, power, adjustment)

Example: **fear** has *very high urgency* and *very low power* — you have a concrete goal (escape threat) and limited control. **Joy** has low urgency and high control.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] The prof flagged that component-process theories are **"not studied with neuroscience methods much"** because the fluid, multi-criteria nature is hard to operationalize empirically. [EXAM]

## 4. Early Neurobiological Theories of Emotion

The book describes a historical progression of theories — useful as exam fodder for distinguishing them. [EXAM]

| Theory | Sequence | Core claim |
|--------|----------|------------|
| **James-Lange** | Stimulus → bodily reaction → feeling | Bodily feedback *causes* the emotion ("we are afraid because we run from the bear") |
| **Cannon-Bard** (diencephalic) | Stimulus → thalamus → simultaneously cortex (feeling) + hypothalamus (body) | Parallel processing; autonomic responses too undifferentiated to specify emotion |
| **Schachter-Singer / two-factor** | Bodily arousal + cognitive label → emotion | Same arousal can be labelled differently depending on context |
| **Papez circuit / limbic system** (MacLean) | Emotion arises from medial-wall forebrain ring (cingulate, hippocampus, hypothalamus, later amygdala + OFC) | The "limbic system" — concept criticized for lacking strict anatomical/functional coherence |

Key historical evidence:
- **Klüver-Bucy syndrome**: bilateral temporal-lobe ablation in monkeys → loss of fear, hyperorality, hypersexuality, visual agnosia. Originally blamed on hippocampus, later shown to depend on **amygdala** disconnection from frontal cortex.
- **Walter Hess**: electrical stimulation of the **hypothalamus** elicits "sham rage" in cats — supports Cannon-Bard's diencephalic emphasis.
- The limbic-system theory survives loosely because the regions MacLean later added — **amygdala, OFC, vmPFC, rostral/ventral ACC** — really *are* central to emotion.

## 5. How Emotions Are Mediated in the Brain — Vertical Integration Models

> [PROF EMPHASIS] In the 70s and 80s neuroscience focused on **neocortex** (because EEG and lesion methods couldn't reach deeper structures easily). Modern imaging revealed that **deeper limbic regions are equally important**, and the dominant framework is now **vertical integration models**.

**Vertical integration models** [EXAM] = models that integrate emotion processing across many *levels* of the nervous system: limbic, cortical, brainstem, autonomic (so the body is part of the model, not just the brain).

### The fear-conditioning vertical integration model
The best-characterized example. Built from rodent fear-conditioning studies and extended to humans.

**Central hub: the amygdala** (with subnuclei: lateral, basal, central, intercalated cells). [EXAM]

Fear output is measured by autonomic indices: [EXAM]
- **Skin conductance response (SCR)** — sweating measure of sympathetic arousal
- **Fear-potentiated startle** — exaggerated eyeblink to a sudden stimulus when in a fear state
- **Pupil dilation**

Acquisition pathway: **sensory input → thalamus** (relay station, fast subcortical route bypasses cortex; LeDoux's "low road") → **amygdala (lateral → basal → central)** → **hypothalamus / brainstem** → fear output (autonomic + behavioural).

Cortical inputs (sensory neocortex, **prefrontal cortex**) provide a slower, more discriminative "high road." The **hippocampus** sits as a separate "island" — it's mainly involved in **contextual fear conditioning** (fear tied to a place), not the moment-to-moment fear expression.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "The amygdala is the most densely interconnected structure in the primate forebrain" — book. This is why it can exert such broad influence over cognition, perception, and motor systems.

### Fear modification and extinction [EXAM]
Fear must be **flexible** — your response to a stimulus has to update when the stimulus is no longer threatening. Failure to do this is called **emotional perseveration** and is a hallmark of anxiety disorders / PTSD.

**Fear extinction** = reduction of fear response by uncoupling the conditioned stimulus (CS) from the unconditioned stimulus (US). The amygdala alone *cannot* extinguish fear — it requires a loop through cortex, specifically the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)**, which inhibits amygdala output via intercalated cells. [EXAM]

**Contextual fear conditioning** also engages the **hippocampus** (place + fear binding).

PTSD example from the book: PTSD patients show *exaggerated* amygdala activity and *failure* to engage vmPFC during extinction → persistent CS+ reactivity even when the stimulus is "safe."

## 6. Emotion's Influence on Other Cognitive Functions

Emotion broadly modulates **perception, attention, memory, and decision making** — evolutionarily sensible because it lets the organism prioritize biologically significant input.

### Somatic marker hypothesis (decision making) [EXAM]
Damasio's hypothesis. The **vmPFC** indexes links between **factual knowledge of an event** and the **bioregulatory states** (autonomic, endocrine, somatic) that accompanied it. When a similar situation re-arises, vmPFC triggers reactivation of the **somatosensory pattern** representing the appropriate emotion via:
- a **direct body loop** — actual visceral response is re-evoked
- an **"as-if" loop** — pattern is simulated in **insula** and somatosensory cortex without overt body changes

These **somatic markers** act as a heuristic that constrains decision options to those marked "good" or "bad" without elaborate logical weighing.

**Iowa Gambling Task** evidence: [EXAM]
- Four decks: A & B disadvantageous (high short-term reward, occasional huge loss), C & D advantageous (smaller reward, smaller loss → higher long-term gain).
- Healthy controls: shift toward C & D over ~100 trials and develop **anticipatory SCRs** when reaching for risky decks.
- vmPFC-damaged patients (e.g. Damasio's patient **Elliot**): keep choosing A & B and produce **no anticipatory SCRs** — they "know" the rules factually but cannot use somatic feedback to guide choice.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] The prof noted that some healthy people ARE risk-takers but still produce SCRs — this signals "I'm aware this is risky, I'm doing it anyway." The vmPFC patient is fundamentally different: no awareness signal at all.

### Perception and attention
Emotion **prioritizes salient sensory information** so the system isn't overwhelmed. The amygdala plays the central role:

- **Amygdala receives input from late stages of ventral visual stream**, but **provides feedback to all stages** including primary visual cortex (V1). [EXAM]
- This bidirectional loop biases perception toward emotional content (e.g. fearful faces are detected even when masked at 17 ms or rendered invisible by binocular rivalry).
- Amygdala-damaged patients (e.g. **patient S.M.**) show no fear-specific enhancement of fusiform-gyrus activity for fearful faces and don't show the emotion-attentional-blink benefit.

The amygdala has **no direct connection to dorsal frontoparietal attention control regions**, so it influences attention indirectly via:
- Release of **acetylcholine from the nucleus basalis of Meynert** → broad cortical attentional enhancement
- Projections to **ACC, insula, vmPFC, OFC**, which in turn target **dlPFC, parietal lobe, PCC**

> [PROF EMPHASIS] First response to threat is **automatic** (amygdala-driven), but further evaluation is **slower, more cognitive, more deliberate** — relying on this indirect attentional network.

### Memory consolidation
Emotion enhances memory — sometimes for the whole event, sometimes for specific salient details.

**Flashbulb memory** [EXAM] = vivid, highly detailed recollection of a brief emotionally significant event ("scars on the mind," coined by William James). The prof emphasized: *vividness ≠ accuracy* — people often feel certain about flashbulb memories that turn out to be wrong.

**Memory modulation hypothesis** (McGaugh): [EXAM]
The amygdala enhances consolidation in the **medial temporal lobe (MTL)** including hippocampus, plus **dlPFC and vlPFC**, by two routes:
1. **Direct axonal projections** — fast, electrical
2. **Hormone release** — slow, neuromodulatory: **(nor)epinephrine, cortisol, propranolol** (β-adrenergic effects)

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "Why are hormones mainly involved in *consolidation*? Because hormones are slow." The slow time-course of hormonal action matches the post-encoding consolidation window — that's why emotional events strengthen memories *after* the fact, not at the moment of encoding.

## 7. Emotion Regulation

Two well-known strategies the prof contrasted:

| Strategy | Definition | Example |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Situation selection** | Antecedent change of behaviour to *avoid* the emotional encounter | Fear of flying → always travel by train |
| **Cognitive reappraisal** | Reinterpret meaning of the elicitor to alter emotional impact *after* it appears | "This news is alarming, but reasons A, B, C make it manageable" |

### Cognitive reappraisal — the neural data [EXAM]
Compare **active decrease** (cognitive reappraisal of negative pictures) vs **passive viewing** of the same negative pictures:

- **Increased activity** in the **dorsal frontoparietal attentional network** (dlPFC, parietal regions) when actively decreasing
- **Reduced amygdala activity** during reappraisal vs passive viewing — the prefrontal network *down-regulates* the amygdala
- **Reduced subjective negative-affect ratings** in the decrease condition (1–7 scale)

So reappraisal works at all three levels of the original emotion definition: it changes neural activity (PFC up, amygdala down), bodily/autonomic state, AND subjective feeling.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "It might be a strategy people use a lot now scrolling through news feeds — to actively decrease the emotional impact." Practical real-world example flagged by the prof.

## 8. Understanding Others — Mentalizing, Mirror Neurons, Theory of Mind

The social-cognition portion of the lecture. The prof said this is **"how you understand actions and emotions of others."**

### Theory of mind / mentalizing
**Mentalizing** (or **theory of mind, ToM**) [EXAM] = inferring the mental states of others, attributing their actions to their **beliefs, goals, desires, feelings**. Two proposed mechanisms:
- **Simulation** — mentally representing another's actions/feelings as if they were your own
- **Theory-building** — building scripts of how others typically behave

**Intentional stance** (Daniel Dennett) [EXAM]: assuming that another agent will behave consistently with *their* current mental state, which may differ from your own.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] The prof's worked example: same event X happens. You assign mental state M1, person next to you assigns M2 (different background → different meaning). Logical action from M1 = A; logical action from M2 = B. Taking the intentional stance means: *you still think A is right for you, but predict the other person will do B because of their M2.* You don't switch to thinking A is wrong — you accept the divergence.

This ability **develops around age 4–5**. The classic test is **false-belief tasks** (location-change or unexpected-contents). Young children and autistic individuals tend to fail explicit false-belief tasks. **Metarepresentation** ("I know my friend thinks the food is at A when it's really at B") is a higher-level skill that requires further cognitive maturation.

### Mirror neurons [EXAM]
Originally found in the **inferior frontal gyrus (IFG, area F5)** of macaque premotor cortex (Rizzolatti et al., 1990s). These neurons fire both when the monkey *performs* a goal-directed action AND when it *passively observes* another individual perform that same action.

Properties:
- Fire more strongly for **goal-directed biological actions** than for mimes or mechanical actions
- A subset are **audio-visual** (e.g. respond both to seeing AND hearing a bell rung)
- Found also in **rostral inferior parietal cortex (PFG, AIP)** → frontoparietal mirror system

In humans, the **putative mirror system** (fMRI evidence — caveat: spatial resolution can't confirm same-neuron activity) spans:
- **Inferior frontal gyrus** (ventral premotor)
- **Middle temporal gyrus**
- Primary motor cortex, somatosensory cortices

> [PROF EMPHASIS] Caution flagged in the book: "evidence for human mirror neurons is suggestive but does not correspond directly" to monkey data — mirror activity in humans is also seen during *mime*, undermining strict simulation accounts.

### Perspective taking — the mentalizing network [EXAM]
Going beyond mirroring requires distinguishing **first- from third-person perspective** and accounting for self-projection into past/future. The mentalizing network (engaged across stories, animations, false-belief tasks, social transgressions):

- **Medial PFC (mPFC)** — especially **paracingulate cortex**; more involved in inferences about *enduring traits*
- **Temporoparietal junction (TPJ)** — transient context-based inferences; also implicated in body location and out-of-body experiences
- **Superior temporal sulcus (STS)** — biological motion, gaze
- **Temporal polar cortex / temporal pole**
- **Inferior parietal cortex**
- **Precuneus / posterior cingulate**

### Theory of mind in apes (the chimpanzee paradigm)
Subordinate vs dominant chimp share a chamber with two food caches. The dominant chimp can see one cache but not the other (occluded). [EXAM]

- If the subordinate uses **only its own perspective** (no ToM): chooses between the two caches at chance.
- If the subordinate **takes the dominant's perspective**: preferentially goes for the cache the dominant *cannot see* (because group hierarchy says dominant eats first, and the subordinate exploits the dominant's ignorance).

Chimps reliably show this preference → evidence for at least implicit/rudimentary mentalizing.

## 9. Empathy, Sympathy, and Decety's 4-Component Model

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Empathy** | Capacity to **resonate with** another's emotional state — you share the feeling |
| **Sympathy** | **Understanding** another's emotional state without sharing it — concern, pity |
| **Personal distress** | Excessive arousal at another's distress, leading to self-focused coping rather than helping |

### Decety's 4-component model of empathy [EXAM]
A full empathic response requires the coordinated operation of four processes:

| Component | Brain regions |
|-----------|---------------|
| **1. Emotion sharing** | Somatosensory cortex, **insula**, ACC, limbic system (more automatic, midbrain-leaning) |
| **2. Self-awareness** (self vs other distinction) | Inferior parietal cortex, PFC, insula |
| **3. Mental flexibility** (perspective taking) | Medial and dorsolateral PFC |
| **4. Emotion regulation** | ACC, lateral and ventromedial PFC |

> [PROF EMPHASIS] The prof emphasized: **PFC is a hub appearing in three of the four components**. Empathy is *not* one brain region — it is a distributed network, and the four components are theoretically separable.

A student asked about overlap between mental flexibility and emotion regulation. The prof admitted the book's box-and-arrow figures are "suboptimal" and the distinction is fuzzy: both are active cognitive processes, while emotion sharing is more automatic and limbic.

## 10. Cutting-Edge Article — Retroactive Emotional Memory Enhancement

The cutting-edge paper (assigned reading on campus). **Question**: if an event seems mundane at first but later acquires emotional meaning, does that retroactively enhance memory for the original mundane event?

**Short answer**: **Yes.** [EXAM]

### Paradigm
1. **Initial learning**: participants learn associations between **neutral face–object pairs**.
2. **Emotional learning** (later): each face from initial learning is paired with either an **aversive screaming voice** OR a **neutral voice** — but the original *object* is no longer shown.
3. **Surprise associative memory test** for the original face-object pairs.

### Findings
- Memory for the initial face-object associations is **retroactively enhanced** for faces that were later paired with the screaming voice.
- Mechanism: **potentiated trial-specific reactivation** of the original neural traces in the **hippocampus** and stimulus-relevant neocortex during emotional learning.
- This induces **rapid hippocampal-neocortical functional reorganization**, characterized by:
  - Enhanced **hippocampal-neocortical coupling modulated by the amygdala** during emotional learning
  - A shift of **hippocampal connectivity** from stimulus-relevant neocortex toward distributed **transmodal prefrontal-parietal areas** at post-learning rest

### Significance
**Maladaptive generalization** [EXAM] — the same retroactive mechanism is implicated in **PTSD** and **phobias**, where a previously neutral context becomes loaded with fear after a traumatic event, contaminating earlier memories.

## Key Takeaways

- **Emotion** = physiological response + action tendency + subjective feeling — these three components must integrate for a healthy emotional state.
- **Basic** (innate, pan-cultural, 6 canonical: anger, sadness, happiness, fear, disgust, surprise) vs **complex** emotions (learned, culturally shaped, e.g. pride).
- **Dimensional theories** (vector vs circumplex) place emotions in arousal × valence space; **component-process theories** treat emotions as fluid combinations of appraisals — but are not well-studied with neuroscience methods.
- **Vertical integration models** like fear conditioning center on the **amygdala** with autonomic outputs (SCR, startle, pupil dilation); **vmPFC** drives fear extinction; **hippocampus** handles contextual fear.
- **Somatic marker hypothesis** + **Iowa Gambling Task**: vmPFC binds factual knowledge to bodily states; vmPFC patients show no anticipatory SCR and choose poorly.
- Emotion's effect on perception/attention is mediated by amygdala-driven indirect routes (acetylcholine via **nucleus basalis of Meynert**; ACC, insula, OFC → dlPFC, parietal).
- **Memory modulation hypothesis**: amygdala enhances consolidation in MTL + dlPFC/vlPFC by direct projections (fast) AND hormones — (nor)epinephrine, cortisol, propranolol (slow).
- **Cognitive reappraisal** activates frontoparietal attentional network, down-regulates amygdala, and reduces subjective negative affect — it works at all three levels of emotion.
- **Mirror neurons** (IFG, premotor) + **mentalizing network** (mPFC, TPJ, STS, temporal pole, precuneus) underpin theory of mind; **intentional stance** = predicting others act consistent with their (potentially different) mental state. Develops at ~age 4–5.
- **Decety's 4-component empathy model**: emotion sharing + self-awareness + mental flexibility + emotion regulation; PFC appears in three of four components.
- **Cutting-edge paper**: emotion retroactively enhances memory for previously mundane events via hippocampal-neocortical reorganization modulated by the amygdala — the neural basis of **maladaptive generalization** in PTSD and phobias.
