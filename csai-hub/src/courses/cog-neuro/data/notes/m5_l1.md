## 1. What Are Executive Functions? — The Umbrella Concept

**Executive function** is an **umbrella term** for processes that **modulate the activity of other cognitive functions** in a flexible, goal-directed manner. The professor explicitly warned that the textbook definition can sound vague, but the core idea is concrete: executive functions perform a **supervisory or regulatory role** over the rest of cognition. [EXAM]

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "Executive function is what modulates activity of all sorts of other functions… in that sense, it's a bit of a more indirect part of cognition."

### What this looks like in practice
- **Mentally simulating an opponent's moves** in a chess game (slow, deliberate, conscious)
- **Initiating a motor action** (rapid, often unconscious — motor control is itself a cognitive function, but the *decision* to act is executive)
- Switching social register between a lecture hall and a pizza night with friends

### Why the prefrontal cortex is special here [EXAM]

People with **prefrontal cortex (PFC) damage** retain **normal cognitive functions** — they can identify objects and sounds, hold conversations, and perform motor tasks with dexterity. What they lose is the ability to **select the right action for the current context**.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "People with prefrontal damage have normal cognitive function… but difficulty selecting the right action for the current context." This dissociation is the conceptual centerpiece of the module.

A key teaching point from the professor: there is **no single homunculus** in the brain. Executive functions arise from a **distributed network** including the PFC, **parietal cortex**, **basal ganglia**, **thalamus**, and **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)**.

## 2. The Two Foundational Concepts: Rules and Control [EXAM]

Nearly every definition of executive function rests on two concepts the prof flagged as the foundation of the entire taxonomy:

| Concept | What it does | Example |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| **Rules** | Abstract, flexible guides for human behavior | "Drive on the right-hand side of the road" — generalises across contexts |
| **Control** | Engages the *appropriate* rule for the *current* context | Switching to left-hand driving when travelling from the US to the UK |

So the test for "is this an executive function?" is: **Does it guide behaviour?** AND **Is it engaged in selecting which rules/activities to perform in which context?** [EXAM]

### The book's taxonomy of executive function [EXAM]

The book groups executive functions into **three branches**:
1. **Establishing and modifying rules** (the green section the prof spent most of the lecture on)
   - **Initiating** new rules
   - **Inhibiting** inappropriate rules
   - **Shifting** among rules
   - **Relating** rules to each other
2. **Contextual control** — **monitoring** the environment to decide when other executive functions need to engage
3. **Working memory** — capacity-limited stores supporting all of the above (**maintenance** + **manipulation**)

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "A lot of the lecture will be on this green chunk… all of this green has to do with various ways in how you can establish and modify rules in a context **for yourself**, not for people around you."

## 3. Prefrontal Cortex Anatomy and the Wider Network

### Subdivisions of the PFC [EXAM]

The PFC is roughly **35% of the human brain** (book Box 13A). The prof stressed that, unlike many cognitive functions, executive function is **not** localised to a single PFC subregion — every subdivision contributes.

| Subregion | Location | Notable role |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| **Dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC)** | Upper, lateral surface | Rule-selective neurons; oddball/inhibition; shifting |
| **Ventrolateral PFC (VLPFC)** | Lower, lateral surface | Stop-signal task (right hemisphere); deduction |
| **Ventromedial PFC (vmPFC) / Orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** | Below, midline | Social rules, reversal learning, affective input |
| **Dorsomedial PFC / Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** | Medial midline | Conflict monitoring, error detection (ERN) |
| **Frontopolar cortex (FPC)** | Most anterior tip | Relating rules, higher-order goals, exploration |

### Other regions in the network [EXAM]
- **Posterior parietal cortex** — strong bidirectional link with DLPFC; **LIP neurons** encode expected value of possible actions
- **Basal ganglia** — caudate nucleus, putamen, ventral striatum, pallidum. The prof flagged this twice: "**very importantly, basal ganglia**" — but stressed they are **deep in the brain**, so EEG can't easily reach them; usually studied with fMRI or single-unit recordings.
- **Thalamus** — the **mediodorsal nucleus** is the **relay station** that loops basal-ganglia output back to cortex, making the cortico-striatal-thalamo-cortical circuit a **closed loop**, not a one-way street.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "These connections… are also for a large part orchestrated, if you will, by the thalamus." The prof emphasised the thalamus is often left out of figures but is central to the loop.

### The three parallel basal-ganglia loops (book Figure 13.7) [EXAM]

All three loops share the **same architecture**: **Cortex → Striatum → Pallidum → Thalamus → back to Cortex**.

| Loop | Cortical entry | Function |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Motor loop** | Primary/premotor/supplementary motor cortex → putamen | Motor control |
| **Prefrontal loop** | DLPFC → anterior caudate | Cognitive executive control |
| **Affective loop** | ACC, OFC → ventral striatum | Reward and emotional regulation |

## 4. Damage to the Prefrontal Cortex — Two Syndromes [EXAM]

The prof highlighted that *where* in the PFC damage occurs determines the symptom profile, even though all PFC damage broadly impairs executive function.

| Feature | Frontal Dysexecutive Syndrome | Frontal Disinhibition Syndrome |
|---------|-------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Lesion site** | **Lateral PFC** | **Ventral and medial PFC** |
| **Behaviour** | Don't start anything new; start things and don't finish them; can't plan into the future | Constant, **unproductive movement**; failure to read social cues |
| **Affect** | Apathy, quietness, blunted | Euphoric/manic, abnormal humor |
| **Insight** | Confabulate implausible explanations for life problems | Lack insight; may be self-destructive |
| **Lab tests** | Impaired on response selection, working memory | Often normal in lab; chaotic in real life |

### Phineas Gage [EXAM]

The classic case of disinhibition syndrome — the **tamping iron** drove through his **orbitofrontal and medial PFC**. The traditional narrative (responsible → profane, reckless, impulsive) is now thought to be **exaggerated**: Gage stayed employed for years, travelled to South America, and reportedly was a good storyteller.

### The unifying summary [EXAM]

Despite the diverse symptoms — from inability to plan to social misbehavior — the deficits **share a common core**: *impairments in the forming, updating, and implementing of rules for appropriate or effective behavior.* The prof and book both emphasise this exact phrasing.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "Even though the social aspect that I just described seems wildly different from the inability to plan your actions… all of them can essentially be summarised as executive function."

## 5. Initiating New Rules — How Rules Are Encoded in the Brain

This is the first sub-branch of the green taxonomy. The exam-relevant question: **how is a rule encoded in the brain?**

### Single-unit monkey study (Wallis et al., 2001) [EXAM]

- Monkeys were trained on a **matching / non-matching task** with cues (juice reward OR auditory tone) signalling which rule applied.
- A first stimulus (Sample) was followed by a second; on **matching trials** monkeys responded if Test 1 matched Sample; on **non-matching** trials, they did the opposite.
- **Single-unit recordings** in lateral PFC showed neurons whose firing was **selective for the rule itself** — independent of which cue was used (juice vs. tone) and independent of the specific stimuli (Object A vs. Object B).

### Conclusion [EXAM]
- **PFC encodes abstract rules** (rule-selective populations)
- **Basal ganglia link specific stimuli to specific responses** (the concrete S-R mappings)
- **Parietal cortex** also contributes — LIP neurons encode expected value of possible actions

> [PROF EMPHASIS] The prof noted the design seems "a leap" from real life, but it's a controlled artificial baseline for studying abstract rule encoding. Single-unit recordings are typically done in **monkeys, not humans**, because they require implanted electrodes.

## 6. Inhibiting Inappropriate Rules

Inhibition is "as important, if not maybe more important than initiating new rules" — selecting wrong behaviour for a context is socially and functionally devastating. The book lists **four forms of inhibition**:

1. **Halting behaviours that are well-trained or previously valid**
2. **Preventing irrelevant information from interfering** with ongoing processing
3. **Restraining socially inappropriate behaviour**
4. **Removing irrelevant information from working memory**

### The Oddball task [EXAM]

This is **the practical for Module 5**, so the prof flagged it explicitly. [EXAM]

- Stream of stimuli, mostly **A** (standard, e.g., squares) requiring a standard response
- Rare **B** (oddball, e.g., circles, ~5–10%) requiring inhibition / different response
- Generates the **P300** — a positive ERP deflection beginning ~300 ms post-target [EXAM]
- The P300 is so robust it survives messy data and few trials — "really uh, unlike many other things in cognitive neuroscience, you can pick it up usually with very little stimuli."
- Source: distributed — parietal cortex, **medial PFC**, **dorsolateral PFC**, with hippocampal contribution. fMRI confirms DLPFC activation.

### Comparison of inhibition tasks [EXAM]

| Task | What you do | Twist | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|-------|
| **Oddball** | Standard response to A; alternate/inhibit for rare B | Reactive inhibition | Generates P300; basis of M5 practical |
| **Go/No-Go** | Respond ("Go") to most stimuli; withhold for rare "No-Go" | Pre-emptive inhibition | Schizophrenia patients impaired on No-Go |
| **Stop-signal** | Respond to stimulus; if a stop signal appears ~300 ms later, **withhold** | Inhibit a response **already initiated** | Sensitive marker for **ADHD**; depends on right posterior VLPFC + basal ganglia |

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "ADHD really, uh, struggles a lot with that type of task." The prof flagged stop-signal as the more cognitively demanding inhibition task because the response is already **on its way** when the stop signal arrives. **Parkinson's disease** (basal ganglia damage) also impairs stop-signal performance.

## 7. Shifting Among Rules — The Wisconsin Card Sorting Test

Real life is not rigid: rules change all the time and you must adapt **flexibly**.

### Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST) [EXAM]

- A deck of cards differs in **colour**, **shape**, and **number** of symbols
- Subject sorts cards by one rule (e.g., colour); only feedback is **correct/incorrect**
- After a number of correct trials, the experimenter **silently changes the rule** (e.g., to shape)
- Healthy adults notice the negative feedback and shift to the new rule within a few trials

### Perseveration in PFC patients [EXAM]

First reported by **Brenda Milner (1963)**: patients with PFC damage **continue to use the previously valid rule** despite repeated negative feedback. This is **perseveration** — the hallmark of impaired prefrontal function.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "People that prefrontal cortex damage… ultimately learn the first rule of sorting by colour… no matter how many trials the researcher then with a shift to another rule says incorrect, incorrect, incorrect — they can't adapt flexibly."

Important nuance from the book: perseveration is **not** an inability to learn new rules per se — if the previous rule is *unavailable* (e.g., all cards are the same colour), patients learn the new rule normally. The deficit is shifting **away from a still-applicable previous rule**.

### Reversal learning and the OFC

The **orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** is critical for shifting in tasks involving **reward and punishment**. OFC-lesioned animals learn stimulus–reward contingencies normally, but cannot relearn when the contingency is reversed.

## 8. Relating Rules — The Frontopolar Cortex

The final green branch: combining multiple rules **at the same time** to generate higher-order behaviour.

### Progressive Matrices Test [EXAM]

The prof's example: two pairs of stimuli (top and bottom). Question: "**Do the two pairs differ on the same dimension?**"
- Top pair: same texture, different shape → differs on **shape**
- Bottom pair: same shape, different texture → differs on **texture**
- → The pairs differ on different dimensions, so the answer is **No**

This requires extracting one rule from each pair, then **relating** the rules — a classic test for **frontal lobe function** (Raven's Progressive Matrices is the clinical tool).

### Frontopolar cortex (FPC) [EXAM]

- Most **anterior** tip of the PFC
- Activates specifically for **relating rules**, **higher-order integration**, and **tracking goals/subgoals**
- FPC damage especially impairs complex Progressive Matrices items, while simple ones remain intact
- Also implicated in **information-seeking (exploratory) behaviour** vs. exploitative reward-seeking
- **Tower of London / Tower of Hanoi** puzzles also tap this kind of mental simulation; left anterior frontal lobe damage particularly disrupts performance on complex multi-move puzzles [EXAM]

## 9. Hierarchical and Rostro-Caudal Models of PFC Organization [EXAM]

The prof noted these theories all have empirical support — none is definitively "the" answer, but each captures something real.

### The general claim
There is a **rostro-caudal organization** of the frontal lobes by **increasing abstraction**:
- **Posterior** PFC → **simple functions** (matching behaviour to stimuli)
- **Anterior** PFC → **complex functions** (higher-order goals)

### Two specific theories [EXAM]

| Theory | Champion | Core claim |
|--------|----------|------------|
| **Temporal abstraction** | **Etienne Koechlin** | Posterior = current-time rules; middle = changing-context rules; anterior = long-term goal rules |
| **Policy abstraction (hierarchy)** | **David Badre** & **Mark D'Esposito** | Anterior regions support **higher-order policies** that determine which simple posterior rule applies; **anterior shapes posterior** |

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "There's definitely research needed to know which of those distinctions is actually concretely mapping out to how the prefrontal cortex functions." The exam may ask you to *contrast* these without claiming one is winning.

A useful concrete example: the prof's own — short-term goal ("get a coffee after lecture") vs. long-term goal ("where I want to be three years from now") engage different temporal-abstraction levels and therefore different rostro-caudal regions.

## 10. Conflict Monitoring — The Anterior Cingulate Cortex

The final piece of the network the prof said he had to include: the **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)**.

### The ACC's role [EXAM]
- **Detects conflict** between competing responses
- **Processes feedback** (especially error feedback)
- **Allocates resources** for cognitive control to other regions (notably DLPFC)

### Stroop task [EXAM]

- Read the word OR name the ink colour
- **Congruent**: word "BLUE" printed in blue ink — no conflict
- **Incongruent**: word "YELLOW" printed in green ink — strong conflict because reading is faster than colour-naming
- **Neutral**: word "TRUCK" printed in red — partial control (no semantic colour conflict)

ACC activates more on **incongruent** trials. High ACC activity on trial *N* predicts higher DLPFC activity on trial *N+1* — i.e., ACC signals conflict, DLPFC implements the resulting control adjustment.

### Error-related negativity (ERN) [EXAM]

- A **negative-polarity scalp electrical potential** measured with EEG, localised (originally) to ACC
- Two flavours:
  1. **Response ERN** — follows a motor movement the participant realises was incorrect
  2. **Feedback ERN** — follows external feedback that an action did not produce the desired outcome
- ERN amplitude correlates with slower response time on the **next** trial → reflects engagement of control

> [PROF EMPHASIS] The prof flagged ERN as "an important term in the context of conflict resolution" — strong exam-cue.

### Caveat (book detail) [EXAM]
Recent **lesion studies** (Fellows & Farah, 2005) show that patients with ACC damage perform **normally** on Stroop conflict tasks. So while neuroimaging consistently shows ACC activation, ACC may not be strictly **necessary**. Newer accounts recast ACC activity as detecting **mismatch between expectations and outcomes** — a learning signal rather than a pure conflict detector.

## 11. Working Memory — The Engine Underneath Everything

Every executive function task discussed (oddball, WCST, stop-signal, Stroop, Progressive Matrices) depends on **working memory** — the capacity-limited, online maintenance and manipulation of currently relevant information.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] "None of these tasks are possible without working memory involvement… how can you select the appropriate rule for a certain context without pulling information from working memory?"

### Baddeley model [EXAM]

Three **capacity-limited buffers** + a **central executive** that allocates resources among them.

| Component | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| **Phonological loop** | Auditory / language information |
| **Visuospatial sketchpad** | Visual & spatial information |
| **Episodic buffer** | Multimodal information pulled from episodic memory |
| **Central executive** | Control system; allocates processing resources to the three buffers |

The prof's example: a heavily visual task allocates more processing to the **visuospatial sketchpad**, less to the phonological loop.

### Cowan model (alternative) [EXAM]

- **No discrete buffers**. Long-term memory representations remain **active** as long as they are **rehearsed**.
- The **central executive** acts as a **spotlight of attention** focused on a small subset of long-term memory.
- One big bucket; the difference is *what is currently illuminated*, not *which container holds it*.

| Comparison | Baddeley | Cowan |
|------------|----------|-------|
| Architecture | Modality-specific buffers | Unitary LTM activation |
| Capacity limit | In each buffer | In attentional spotlight |
| Central executive | Allocates resources | Selects/illuminates |

> [PROF EMPHASIS] Both models posit a **central executive** — that consensus is the takeaway. The disagreement is about whether storage is modular or unitary.

## 12. Cutting-Edge Paper — Meditation & 7T fMRI

The prof's chosen cutting-edge paper for this module. Key facts to know: [EXAM]

### Setup
- **7-Tesla fMRI** — *Tesla* is the strength of the MRI magnet; 7T is unusually strong, giving very high spatial resolution
- Compares **focused meditation** vs. **passive rest** (whole-brain, stringent statistical correction)

### Key finding
- **Reduced activity in the Default Mode Network (DMN)** during meditation compared to passive rest
- **No** brain region showed **increased** activity vs. rest

### The Default Mode Network (DMN) [EXAM]
- Also called the **task-negative network**
- Core nodes: **Posterior cingulate cortex (PCC)** + **medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC)**
- Most active during rest / mind-wandering / self-referential processing; suppressed by goal-directed cognition

### Interpretation
- DMN deactivation during meditation = **attenuation of mind-wandering and spontaneous thought**
- **Thalamic nuclei and occipital cortex** also deactivate → suggested role in **perceptual decoupling** (turning inward, away from sensory input)
- Robust **even after controlling for covariates** (alertness, effort) — though the prof noted the latter dampens signal slightly, reflecting moment-to-moment variation in meditation depth

### Why it matters for this module
Meditation is essentially a **trained executive function skill** — sustaining attention, mitigating distraction. So DMN deactivation provides a neural correlate for executive control over distractibility.

> [PROF EMPHASIS] The prof flagged controlling for covariates (alertness, effort) as **good practice for theses and internships** — a methodological exam cue that surfaced again here.

## Key Takeaways

- **Executive function = umbrella term** for processes that **modulate other cognitive functions**; built on **rules** + **control**. [EXAM]
- The **PFC is central but not alone** — parietal cortex, **basal ganglia**, **thalamus**, and **ACC** form a distributed network with cortico-striatal-thalamo-cortical loops.
- **PFC damage** dissociates into **dysexecutive syndrome** (lateral) and **disinhibition syndrome** (ventromedial — Phineas Gage). The unifying core deficit is impaired **forming, updating, and implementing of rules**.
- The "green" taxonomy of rule processing has four parts: **initiating** (rule-selective neurons in lateral PFC), **inhibiting** (oddball/P300, go-no-go, stop-signal), **shifting** (WCST → perseveration), and **relating** (Progressive Matrices → frontopolar cortex).
- **Two hierarchical theories** organise PFC rostro-caudally: **Koechlin's temporal abstraction** vs. **Badre & D'Esposito's policy abstraction**. Both agree posterior = simple, anterior = complex.
- **ACC + ERN** are the conflict-monitoring system; activated by Stroop incongruency. Recent lesion studies challenge whether ACC is strictly *necessary*.
- **Working memory** underwrites all executive function — **Baddeley** (modular buffers + central executive) vs. **Cowan** (unitary LTM activation + attentional spotlight).
- The **cutting-edge 7T meditation paper** shows DMN deactivation during meditation — a neural signature of **perceptual decoupling** and trained executive control over mind-wandering.
- **Practical for M5 = oddball task / P300** — you'll be measuring this in your own brain. [EXAM]
