# Module 2 Lecture 1: Motor Control

## Overview

Motor control is one of the most well-understood areas in cognitive neuroscience. This lecture covers how the nervous system organises and executes movements — from the hierarchical organisation of motor systems, through the roles of the basal ganglia and cerebellum, to how movements are planned and initiated. Understanding motor control also provides a window into cognitive processing more broadly, as motor structures have been co-opted for cognitive functions.

---

## 1. Motor Control is Hierarchical [EXAM]

The motor system is organised in a **hierarchical** fashion, with different levels controlling different aspects of movement.

### 1.1 Upper Motor Neurons (Top of Hierarchy)

- **Location:** **Primary motor cortex** and cortical **premotor areas**
- **Function:** Issue **motor commands** (motor programs) to local circuit neurons and lower motor neurons
- Upper motor neurons specify **what to do AND how to do it** [PROF EMPHASIS]
  - Evidence: cortical motor spikes **precede** the initiation of actual muscle movement (temporal precedence)
  - The **spike rate** (firing strength) of cortical motor neurons also dictates the **force** of the action
  - Example: lifting your arm fast vs. poking a machine hard — the upper motor neurons encode both the action and its force parameters
- **Low-current stimulation** of primary motor cortex can **directly evoke movements**, demonstrating relatively direct access to lower motor circuits [EXAM]

### 1.2 Lower Motor Neurons (Bottom of Hierarchy)

- **Location:** **Spinal cord** and **brainstem**
- **Function:** The **final common pathway** for motor commands — they are the effectors
- Activity in lower motor neurons correlates with **reflexes** (the basic building units of limb movement)
- **Local circuit neurons** project to lower motor neurons and contribute to their local coordination

### 1.3 Control Systems Interfacing with Upper Motor Neurons

Two major control systems modulate upper motor neuron activity:

| Structure | Primary Function |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Basal ganglia** | Initiation/stopping of movements, motor skill learning, goal-directedness |
| **Cerebellum** | Movement coordination and online error correction |

### 1.4 The Signature Writing Analogy [PROF EMPHASIS]

- Motor programs are like "signature writing" — the upper motor system issues these programs, and lower motor neurons carry them out
- This illustrates the hierarchical relationship: planning at the top, execution at the bottom

## 2. Cortical Motor Areas and Movement Planning [EXAM]

### 2.1 Primary Motor Cortex (M1)

- Located anterior to the central sulcus
- Heavily involved in **executing movements** — direct control over limbs
- Contains a **functional topography** (motor homunculus/somatotopic map)
  - Can be demonstrated non-invasively using **transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)** [EXAM]
  - Also confirmed by **functional neuroimaging**
- Direct electrical stimulation evokes specific movements — evidence for hierarchical control

### 2.2 Premotor Cortex and Supplementary Motor Area (SMA)

- Heavily involved in **planning**, **movement intention**, and **control** [EXAM]
- **Premotor areas** provide **abstract goal-directed planning information** to the primary motor cortex
- Primary motor cortex then translates this into **movement intentions** (motor programs)
- These motor programs are then translated by **downstream local circuits** into **muscle contraction patterns**

### 2.3 Population Coding [EXAM]

- Movement direction is encoded by **populations of neurons**, not individual cells
- Neurons "vote" together through **majority voting** to democratically determine the next movement
- This is the concept of **population coding** — the collective activity of many neurons specifies movement direction
- Key researcher: **Georgopoulos et al. (1986)** — demonstrated neuronal population coding of movement direction

### 2.4 Cortical Pathways for Motor Control

- Axons of upper motor neurons in primary motor cortex descend through the **medullary pyramids**
- **Majority of axons cross (decussate)** at the caudal end of the medulla → terminate in the spinal cord → control **distal muscles** (away from body) [EXAM]
- **Small minority remain uncrossed** → control **midline proximal musculature** (towards body)
- This explains why damage to one hemisphere affects the **contralateral** side of the body

## 3. Initiation of Movement by the Basal Ganglia [EXAM]

### 3.1 Structure

The basal ganglia consist of **three principal nuclei**:

1. **Caudate nucleus**
2. **Putamen** (caudate + putamen = **striatum**) [EXAM]
3. **Globus pallidus**

Also important for basal ganglia function:
- **Subthalamic nucleus**
- **Substantia nigra pars compacta** (dopaminergic neurons)
- Substantia nigra pars reticulata (involved in vision circuits)

These are **subcortical structures**.

### 3.2 Function: Gating Mechanism [EXAM]

- Basal ganglia receive inputs from **nearly all cortical areas**
- Act as a **gating mechanism**: **inhibiting potential movements until the right time** [PROF EMPHASIS]
- Functions include:
  - **Movement initiation and stopping**
  - **Motor skill learning**
  - **Goal-directedness**
  - **Producing sequences of movements**
  - **Subjective sense of time**
- Activity is **reward-dependent** — whether you do something is modulated by reward, emotion, and current state [PROF EMPHASIS]
- Reward modulation: neurons in the caudate nucleus show graded changes in firing rate corresponding to **reward size**

### 3.3 Direct and Indirect Pathways

- **Direct pathway:** Disinhibits desired movements (releases the "brake")
- **Indirect pathway:** Maintains inhibition of unwanted movements
- The balance between these pathways determines which movements are produced

### 3.4 Central Pattern Generators (CPGs) [EXAM]

- **Local circuits** in the spinal cord and brainstem that produce **rhythmic movements** (walking, swimming)
- Evidence from the classic **decerebrate cat experiment** [PROF EMPHASIS]:
  - After spinal cord transection, cat hind limbs can still walk on a treadmill
  - Reciprocal bursts of action potentials in flexors and extensors during swing/stance phases are preserved
  - Demonstrates that rhythmic behaviour can be generated without higher brain input
- Upper motor neurons **modulate** the rhythmic behaviour; basal ganglia **initiate and stop** it
- CPGs originally evolved for basic rhythmic functions but serve more complex cognitive motor behaviours (e.g., **spoken language** relies on similar sequencing mechanisms) [EXAM]

### 3.5 Clinical Disorders of the Basal Ganglia [EXAM]

| Disorder | Cause | Symptoms |
|----------|-------|----------|
| **Parkinson's disease** | Death of dopaminergic neurons in **substantia nigra pars compacta** | Difficulty initiating movement, tremor at rest, slow shuffling gait; movements improve with sensory cues (e.g., marks on the floor) |
| **Huntington's disease** | Hereditary atrophy of the **caudate nucleus** | **Choreiform** (dancelike) uncontrollable movements, psychotic thought patterns, eventual dementia |
| **Hemiballismus** | Unilateral damage to the **subthalamic nucleus** | Choreiform movements of contralateral limbs |

- **Parkinson's treatment:** **L-dopa** (synthetic dopamine precursor) — restores basal ganglia's ability to release tonic inhibition from thalamus, enhancing motor cortex excitability. Chronic use can cause cognitive problems. [EXAM]
- These disorders provide evidence that basal ganglia **gate the production of goal-directed movements**
- Motor sequence learning is **impaired** in Parkinson's and Huntington's patients but **preserved** in amnesia patients with medial temporal lobe damage [EXAM]

## 4. Basal Ganglia and Cognition [EXAM]

### 4.1 Nonmotor Pathways

Anatomical studies reveal **nonmotor pathways** through the basal ganglia:

| Pathway | Cortical Origin | Function |
|---------|----------------|----------|
| **Sensory-motor network** | Sensory-motor cortices | Movement disinhibition |
| **Limbic (emotional) network** | Orbital and ventral prefrontal cortex → limbic striatum | Emotional processing |
| **Associative (cognitive) network** | Prefrontal and parietal association cortices → associative striatum | Cognitive processing |

- All three networks are enhanced by projections from **midbrain dopamine neurons** [EXAM]
- The same principles governing **movement disinhibition** in the motor pathway apply to emotional and cognitive processing [PROF EMPHASIS]
- Each channel forms a **feedback loop**: cortex → basal ganglia → thalamus → back to cortex

### 4.2 Evidence for Cognitive Role

- **Joystick-cursor experiment:** Strongest **putamen** activation when subjects had to **learn** joystick-cursor movement with 90° offset, compared to moving without feedback, watching cursor move, or resting [EXAM]
- Animals with basal ganglia lesions can perform movements but **cannot learn new actions** to acquire rewards
- Parkinson's patients show impairments in **probabilistic classification tasks**
- Huntington's disease involves **psychotic thought processes** — evidence for cognitive role of caudate

## 5. Online Error Correction by the Cerebellum [EXAM]

### 5.1 Structure

- A large, **foliated structure** that sits atop the **pons** in the brainstem
- Also called the "**small brain**" (or colloquially the "reptile brain")
- Contains **exquisitely organised circuits of specialised neurons**

Subdivisions of the cerebellar cortex:

| Division | Input Source |
|----------|-------------|
| **Spinocerebellum** | Spinal cord (proprioceptive information) |
| **Cerebrocerebellum** | Cerebral cortex via pons |
| **Vestibulocerebellum** (flocculus + nodulus) | Vestibular nuclei |

Key output structure: **Dentate nucleus** (deep cerebellar nucleus)

### 5.2 Function: Error Correction [EXAM]

- Computes the **net error** between ongoing **motor commands** (from motor cortex) and **actual movements** being produced [PROF EMPHASIS]
- Functions as a **relay system** — evolution figured out that relaying information through the cerebellar cortex allows adjustment of this net error
- Error signals are relayed back to **frontal and parietal cortices** via projections from the dentate nucleus → through the **thalamus** → back to **primary motor cortex**
- This net error can be used to **update circuitry** (similar to artificial neural networks) [PROF EMPHASIS]

### 5.3 Cerebellar Inputs and Outputs

**Inputs:**
- **Frontal motor and parietal cortex** → project through the **pons** (brainstem) → cerebellar cortex (higher-order motor programs)
- Lower brain systems also project to cerebellar cortex → monitor how actual actions are going

**Outputs:**
- Deep cerebellar nuclei → **thalamus** → back to **primary motor cortex**

### 5.4 Brainstem Contributions

Brainstem circuits coordinate lower-level reflex circuits to:
- Make **anticipatory postural adjustments** to accommodate movements
- **Correct posture** for ongoing changes in balance
- Coordinate more complex **multijoint movements**

### 5.5 Intention Tremor and Cerebellar Ataxia [EXAM]

- Damage to the cerebellum → **inability to perform smooth movements**
- **Intention tremor:** irregular, jerky movements when reaching (e.g., nose-to-finger test) [EXAM]
- **Cerebellar ataxia:** loss of movement control producing irregular movement
- **Alcohol intoxication** also disrupts cerebellar function → impaired balance and coordination [PROF EMPHASIS]
- Clinical test: patients asked to point rapidly from clinician's finger to their own nose — cerebellar patients produce irregular, jerky movements vs. smooth movements in normal subjects

### 5.6 Cerebellum and Motor Learning

- **Tracking experiment:** When subjects track a target using a mouse with a **rotated cursor**, cerebellar activity is **larger for rotated** vs. unrotated cursor [EXAM]
- As tracking error decreases (learning), **cerebellar activity also decreases**
- Cerebellar activation **peaks during initial learning** and declines with improvement
- Damage to the cerebellum **disrupts the ability to learn new motor skills**

### 5.7 Cerebellum Controls Ipsilateral Musculature

- Unlike upper motor neurons of cortex and basal ganglia, cerebellar circuits mediate movements of the **ipsilateral** (same-side) musculature [EXAM]
- This is a key difference from cortical motor control (which is contralateral)

## 6. Cerebellar Contributions to Cognition [EXAM]

### 6.1 Beyond Motor Function

While traditionally considered a strictly motor structure, the cerebellum has been implicated in:
- **Nonmotor learning**
- **Attention**
- **Timing**
- **Verbal working memory**
- **Speech**

### 6.2 Evidence

- **Neuroimaging:** Cerebellar activation in nonmotor learning, attention, timing, and verbal working memory tasks
- **Neurophysiological studies:** Patients with cerebellar damage show deficits in speech, learning rates, timing, and working memory
- **Autism:** Problems in attention orientation in autistic individuals linked to **cerebellar abnormalities** [EXAM]
- Prefrontal cortex **massively projects** to the cerebellum (not just motor/premotor cortex)

### 6.3 The Co-option Hypothesis [EXAM]

- The cerebellum may be an example of **neural circuitry originally adapted to one purpose being co-opted for another** [PROF EMPHASIS]
- **Motor model:** Cerebellum computes predicted sensory consequences of movement → error between predicted and actual consequences is fed back to update future predictions
- **Cognitive model (Ramnani, 2006):** The same error-correction circuitry applied to cognitive processing — computes predicted **cognitive consequences** of cognitive operations
- Prefrontal inputs to cerebellum are **more developed in humans** than non-human primates, paralleling differences in cognitive complexity
- During learning: **prefrontal circuits show reduced activation** while **cerebellar circuits become more active** — cerebellum provides fast, accurate, automatic "simulations"

## 7. Planning Movements [EXAM]

### 7.1 Hierarchical Organisation of Planning

1. **Premotor areas** provide abstract goal-directed planning information
2. **Primary motor cortex** (and **superior colliculus** for visual movement) translates this into movement intentions
3. **Downstream local circuits** translate motor programs into muscle contraction patterns

### 7.2 The Readiness Potential (Bereitschaftspotential) [EXAM]

- **EEG recordings** confirm hierarchical organisation of movement planning
- Voluntary movements generate a **readiness potential several seconds before** the actual movement [EXAM]
- **Enhanced readiness potential** when subjects judge the **time of urge to move** (red line) rather than the movement itself (blue line)
- Neuroimaging localises the readiness potential to **premotor areas** and **supplementary motor areas**
- This has **massive implications for consciousness** [PROF EMPHASIS] — motor planning occurs before conscious awareness of the urge to move

### 7.3 Anosognosia [EXAM]

- **Definition:** "Loss of awareness" — patients are **unaware of or deny their inability to move**
- Occurs with damage to **premotor and primary motor cortex**
- Provides evidence that premotor cortex is involved in **motor planning and intentional awareness**
- Implies that the planning system generates a sense of intention, even when execution is impossible

## 8. Motor Sequence Learning [EXAM]

### 8.1 Two Phases of Learning

| Phase | Characteristics | Brain Regions |
|-------|----------------|---------------|
| **Early learning** | Rapid improvement within a single session | Premotor cortex, pre-SMA, SMA, caudate (basal ganglia), cerebellum, parietal cortex |
| **Advanced learning** | Slow improvement over multiple sessions | Reduced global activity in most regions; increased activity in specific subregions of **primary motor cortex** and **cerebellum** |

Reference: **Steele and Penhune (2010)**

### 8.2 Serial Reaction Time (SRT) Task

- Subjects respond to stimuli appearing in a repeating sequence
- **Implicit learning** detected by interposing a **random block** — reaction times are significantly slower during random trials
- Motor sequence learning is:
  - **Preserved** in amnesia patients (medial temporal lobe damage)
  - **Impaired** in patients with basal ganglia disorders (Parkinson's, Huntington's) [EXAM]

### 8.3 Key Brain Regions in Motor Learning

- **Motor cortical regions:** premotor cortex, pre-SMA, SMA
- **Basal ganglia (caudate):** sequence initiation/stopping
- **Cerebellum:** error correction during learning
- **Posterior parietal cortex:** spatial coordinates for guiding movement (damage → **optic ataxia**)

## Key Takeaways [EXAM PRIORITY]

1. **Motor control is hierarchical:** Upper motor neurons (primary motor cortex) issue commands; lower motor neurons (spinal cord/brainstem) execute them. Upper motor neurons specify both **what** to do and **how** to do it.

2. **Basal ganglia gate movements:** The striatum (caudate + putamen) and globus pallidus act as a gating mechanism, **inhibiting movements until the appropriate time**. Dysfunction leads to Parkinson's (can't initiate) or Huntington's (can't inhibit).

3. **Cerebellum computes error:** It calculates the net error between intended and actual movements, enabling smooth, coordinated actions. Damage causes **intention tremor** and cerebellar ataxia.

4. **Both basal ganglia and cerebellum contribute to cognition:** The basal ganglia have limbic and associative pathways; the cerebellum's error-correction circuitry has been **co-opted for cognitive functions** (attention, timing, working memory).

5. **Population coding** in the motor cortex: Movement direction is determined by the collective "voting" of populations of neurons, not individual cells (**Georgopoulos et al., 1986**).

6. **The readiness potential** demonstrates that neural preparation for movement occurs **seconds before** the conscious urge to move — with major implications for theories of consciousness and free will.

7. **Central pattern generators** in the spinal cord can produce rhythmic movements (walking, swimming) **independently** of higher brain centres, as demonstrated by the decerebrate cat experiment.

8. **Motor learning occurs in two phases:** Early learning involves broad cortical/subcortical activation; advanced learning shows reduced global activity but increased specific activation in M1 and cerebellum.
