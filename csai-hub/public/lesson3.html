Lesson Three

How a network actually takes each step.

Backprop gives us the gradient. But what do we do with it? Plain gradient descent is the obvious answer — and the broken one. Today: the family of optimizers that patch its flaws, one idea at a time.

Section 1 · slides 1–4

From gradients to optimizers

Last lesson ended with backpropagation — a way to compute ∂L/∂W for every weight. The actual update was a one-liner:

W ← W − η · ∇L

That simple rule is the plain gradient descent optimizer. It works. But it has sharp edges — noise, local minima, narrow valleys, a single learning rate for every parameter — and each of today's optimizers is a fix for one of them.

Think of the loss as a landscape. Each weight is a coordinate; the loss at that point is the height. Training = walking downhill. Every optimizer is a different walking strategy.

Click on the surface on the right to drop a ball. It follows the gradient. Start from different places and you'll land in different valleys — welcome to the core problem.

Section 2 · slides 5–8

Batch, mini-batch, stochastic

Before choosing how to step, we choose how much data we look at per step.

  • Batch gradient descent — use all training examples. The gradient is the true average direction. Smooth path — but impossibly slow on a modern dataset.
  • Stochastic gradient descent (SGD) — use one sample at a time. Very fast per step, but the gradient is a noisy estimate. The path zigzags.
  • Mini-batch gradient descent — the compromise everyone uses. Take a small random group (say 32 or 128 samples), average their gradients. Most of SGD's speed, most of batch's stability.
Noise is not always bad. The randomness of mini-batch and SGD actually helps the path jump out of shallow local minima that full-batch would get trapped in.

Section 3 · slides 10–14

Momentum: remember where you came from

SGD has a specific failure mode: in a narrow valley, the gradient points mostly across the walls, barely along the valley. Plain SGD oscillates wall-to-wall and barely progresses.

The trick: don't use the raw gradient — use an exponentially weighted average of the past few gradients. Oscillations cancel. The consistent direction survives.

vt = β · vt−1 + (1 − β) · ∇L
W ← W − α · vt

β ≈ 0.9 is the typical value — roughly "average over the last 10 steps." A higher β means you trust the history more; a lower β keeps you reactive to the current gradient.

Rolling-ball intuition. Imagine a ball rolling down a valley. It does not stop at every wiggle in the floor because it has momentum. The derivative is acceleration; β is friction (just under 1 so it doesn't speed up forever). The accumulated velocity is vt.

Toggle β on the right — watch plain SGD slam between the walls of the ravine while momentum glides along its floor.

Section 4 · slides 15–17

Nesterov: a smarter ball

Momentum has one weakness: it is blind. It accumulates velocity and keeps going — straight past the minimum, then has to come back.

Nesterov's fix is almost comically simple: before computing the gradient, look ahead first. Use the momentum to peek at the position you'll probably be in next step, and take the gradient there.

vt = β · vt−1 + (1 − β) · ∇L(W − β·vt−1)

The gradient now knows about the momentum. If momentum is overshooting, the look-ahead gradient will already be pointing back — correcting the step in the same update, not one step later.

"A ball with a notion of where it is going." The standard ball blindly rolls; Nesterov's ball knows to slow down before the hill rises again. Less overshoot, less wasted backtracking, faster convergence.

Section 5 · slides 19–22

AdaGrad: a learning rate per parameter

So far every parameter has used the same learning rate. But different weights behave differently — some see frequent, large gradients (a weight tied to a common feature), others rarely move (a weight for a rare feature).

AdaGrad (Adaptive Gradient) keeps a running sum of squared gradients r for each parameter, and uses it to shrink that parameter's learning rate.

rt,i = rt−1,i + gt,i²
Wi ← Wi − (α / (√rt,i + ε)) · gt,i

Frequently-updated weights get their effective rate shrunk; rarely-updated ones keep a big rate. Good for sparse features, word embeddings, NLP.

The fatal flaw: r only grows. After many steps it becomes huge for every parameter, the effective rate collapses toward zero, and learning simply stops. There is no way back.

Section 6 · slides 23–26

RMSprop: forget the distant past

Hinton's fix for AdaGrad is minimal: replace the cumulative sum of squared gradients with an exponentially weighted average.

vt,i = β · vt−1,i + (1 − β) · gt,i²
Wi ← Wi − (α / (√vt,i + ε)) · gt,i

Now v is a moving average, not a sum. It can go up or down. Old gradients are forgotten; only recent magnitude matters.

What this fixes: no more collapse to zero. If a parameter's gradients suddenly become large, RMSprop damps it. If they become small, RMSprop gives it more room again. The rate adapts continuously.

Section 7 · slides 27–32

Adam: momentum + RMSprop + bias fix

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) is the practical default. It combines the best ideas you have seen:

  • Momentum — a moving average of the gradients themselves mt.
  • RMSprop — a moving average of the squared gradients vt.
  • Bias correction — early averages start biased toward zero (because they're initialized at zero). Adam divides them by 1 − βt to boost them at the start.
mt = β₁ mt−1 + (1−β₁) gt    vt = β₂ vt−1 + (1−β₂) gt²
m̂ = mt / (1 − β₁ᵗ)    v̂ = vt / (1 − β₂ᵗ)
W ← W − α · m̂ / (√v̂ + ε)

Defaults: α = 0.001, β₁ = 0.9, β₂ = 0.999. These were chosen empirically across many tasks in the original paper.

Adam is the workhorse. It's not always best — sometimes plain SGD with momentum generalizes better — but it usually converges faster and needs less tuning. It is the sane default.

Section 8 · slides 33–39

AdamW and Lion

AdamW is a tiny fix to Adam. A common trick is weight decay: nudge every weight a little toward zero on each step (to prevent overfitting). The old way folded this into the gradient — but then Adam's per-parameter scaling also scaled the decay, inconsistently. AdamW decouples weight decay, subtracting it directly after the Adam step.

W ← W − α · m̂ / (√v̂ + ε) − α · λ · W

Small change; big effect in practice. AdamW is the standard optimizer for transformers today.

Lion (EvoLved Sign Momentum) is a 2023 surprise: Google used a genetic algorithm to search for a better optimizer. What it found keeps momentum but uses only the sign of the update. No squared gradients — so it stores half the state of Adam.

mt = β mt−1 + (1 − β) gt
W ← W − α · sign(mt)
When Lion shines: large batches (gradient direction is reliable), lots of data, and tight memory. When it struggles: small batches and situations where the magnitude of the gradient matters. Because you throw away magnitude, use a much smaller learning rate.

Check yourself

Q1. Why is mini-batch gradient descent the standard in practice?

Full-batch is accurate but too slow for modern datasets. Pure SGD is fast but very noisy. Mini-batch averages a small random group — most of the speed, most of the stability, and the leftover noise actually helps escape shallow local minima.

Q2. What specific problem does momentum fix?

Momentum smooths the step by averaging past gradients. Wall-to-wall oscillations cancel; consistent directions survive. A rolling ball with inertia glides through the valley instead of bouncing.

Q3. What is the key difference between Nesterov momentum and standard momentum?

Standard momentum: gradient at current point + momentum. Nesterov: peek ahead using the momentum first, then take the gradient there. If momentum overshoots, the look-ahead gradient already points back — correcting in the same step.

Q4. Why does AdaGrad eventually stop learning?

AdaGrad sums squared gradients cumulatively. That sum never decreases. After enough steps the denominator √r explodes and the effective learning rate collapses. Nothing can recover it.

Q5. What single change does RMSprop make vs. AdaGrad?

RMSprop replaces the growing sum with an exponentially weighted average. It can go up or down, so effective rates no longer collapse — they adapt continuously.

Q6. Adam combines which ideas?

Adam keeps a moving average of gradients (momentum), a moving average of squared gradients (RMSprop), and divides both by 1 − βt to correct their bias-toward-zero at the start of training.

Q7. What does AdamW change about Adam?

Classic L2 regularization goes through the gradient, so Adam's per-parameter scaling distorts the decay. AdamW decouples weight decay: subtract α·λ·W as its own term, after the main Adam update. Small fix, big practical win for transformers.

Q8. Why does Lion use a much smaller learning rate than Adam?

Lion throws away gradient magnitude and just takes sign(m). Every per-parameter step is either +1 or −1 before the learning rate. With no self-damping, a normal α would overshoot wildly — so Lion needs a much smaller α.
