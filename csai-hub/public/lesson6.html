Section 1 · slide 2 · the problem

Why sequences need memory

A perceptron eats one vector and produces one answer. A sentence doesn't fit that shape. "The kid was…" has three words today, and tomorrow's sentence may have thirty. You can't size your input layer for both.

Worse, the meaning of word t depends on the words that came before it. A network that forgets the subject by the time it reaches the verb has no chance of agreeing them. You need state — a small vector that travels along the sequence and accumulates context.

Press ▶. A token enters at each time step. The hidden state ht is the memory that's passed forward. Watch it carry information across the sequence.

Recurrent means self-referring. The network applies the same weights at every step and reads its own previous output. One small unit, unrolled in time, becomes a long pipeline.

Quick check

Why can't a plain MLP read a variable-length sentence directly?

An MLP needs a fixed-size input vector. A sentence (or any sequence) can be short or long. You need a model that processes one element at a time while carrying state forward.

Section 2 · slide 3 · the recurrent cell

The vanilla RNN cell

One cell, one rule, repeated forever. At each time step, the cell takes the new input xt and the previous hidden state ht−1, mixes them with two weight matrices, and squashes the result with tanh:

ht = tanh(Wx xt + Wh ht−1 + b)
Same Wx, same Wh, same b at every time step. Only the inputs change.

The output at step t is normally a separate transformation of the hidden state — ot = g(ht) — perhaps a linear layer plus softmax. That part is exactly like an MLP. The new ingredient is the recurrent connection: ht−1 wraps around and feeds back in.

Move the sliders. Wx controls how much the new input pushes the state. Wh controls how much of the old state carries over. Watch the bar of ht evolve as a sine-wave input flows in.

A cell with a hidden state of size n has weight matrices of shape (n × input) and (n × n). The recurrent matrix Wh is the one that causes all the trouble — we'll see why.

Quick check

Across T time steps, how many distinct copies of Wx does the unrolled RNN have?

The whole point of recurrence is weight sharing across time. One Wx, one Wh, applied T times. That's why the RNN can handle a sequence of any length with a fixed parameter count.

Section 3 · slide 3 · BPTT

Backprop through time

Training works the same way it always does: take the loss, follow the chain rule back through every operation, accumulate a gradient for each weight. The twist is that the recurrent matrix Wh appears at every time step. So its gradient is a sum over the whole sequence.

For a loss LT at the end of the sequence, the gradient that reaches step k has to travel backward through every intermediate hidden state:

∂LT / ∂Wh   involves   ∏t = k+1T ∂ht / ∂ht−1
A product of Jacobians, one factor per time step you cross.

Olier's framing: "you have 2, 3, 4 time steps — fine. Twenty — that product becomes a problem." Each Jacobian multiplies a derivative of tanh, which is at most 1 and usually much smaller. Multiply enough of those together and the signal disappears.

Press ▶. A loss is created at the end. Watch the gradient signal propagate backward, multiplied by one Jacobian per step. The further back you go, the more multiplications stack up.

Same parameters, every step. So the gradient for those parameters is the sum of contributions from every step. Long sequence → long sum → trouble at both ends.

Section 4 · slide 4 · the vanishing gradient

Vanishing & exploding gradients

That product of Jacobians is the whole story. If each factor has magnitude below 1, the product collapses to zero exponentially fast. If each factor is above 1, the product blows up. There's almost no middle ground.

The practical effect: information from the start of a long sequence has almost no influence on the loss at the end. The network sees the recent past clearly and the distant past as noise. Olier puts it bluntly: "what happened at the beginning of the sequence doesn't really matter for changing the parameters."

Long-range dependencies are the casualty. If a sentence is "The kid who lived next to the family with the dog that barked all night was tired," the verb agrees with kid, twelve words back. A vanilla RNN can't connect them — the gradient that would teach the link has decayed to nothing.

Move the slider for the per-step factor. At 0.7 the gradient at step t = 0 is essentially gone after twenty steps. At 1.1 it explodes. At exactly 1.0 it stays constant — but that boundary is impossibly thin.

Quick check

Why does tanh make vanishing gradients more likely than amplification?

tanh'(z) = 1 − tanh²(z) is bounded by 1 and only reaches 1 at z = 0. Anywhere else it's smaller — often a lot smaller. Multiply many such numbers and the product vanishes.

Section 5 · slides 5–13 · gated units

The LSTM: a cell with gates

The fix proposed by Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) is shockingly simple in spirit. Add a separate cell state ct that runs alongside the hidden state. Make it a conveyor belt: information slides across with almost no transformation, so gradients can travel along it without vanishing. Then add gates to control what gets on the belt and what gets off.

A gate, in Olier's words, is just a knob — a sigmoid that outputs a number between 0 (closed) and 1 (open). Multiply your data by it, and you choose how much to keep. Three gates govern the LSTM:

Forget gate ft = σ(Wf·[ht−1, xt] + bf) — what to erase from the cell state.
Input gate it = σ(...) with candidate čt = tanh(...) — what new content to write.
Output gate ot = σ(...) — what part of the cell to expose as the hidden state.

The cell state update reads almost like English: forget some old, add some new:

ct = ft ⊙ ct−1  +  it ⊙ čt
ht = ot ⊙ tanh(ct)

Drag the gate sliders. Forget = 0.9 means the network keeps almost everything. Forget = 0.1 means it wipes its memory at every step. Watch the cell state respond.

The gradient highway. Because ct is mostly an addition, not a multiplication, the partial derivative ∂ct/∂ct−1 is basically the forget gate itself. If forget is close to 1, gradients travel back across hundreds of steps unscathed.

Quick check

Why does the cell state ct help long-range gradients survive?

The path ct−1 → ct is mostly element-wise addition, gated by the forget gate. No squashing, no contraction by Jacobians. Gradients can flow back along it freely.

Section 6 · slides 15–21 · GRU

The GRU: same idea, fewer parts

The Gated Recurrent Unit (Cho et al., 2014) is the LSTM's leaner cousin. It drops the separate cell state and merges the forget and input gates into one. Two gates, one state, fewer parameters — often the same accuracy.

Reset gate rt = σ(Wr·[ht−1, xt]) — how much of the past to forget when proposing new content.
Update gate zt = σ(Wz·[ht−1, xt]) — how much of the new state to mix in.
Candidate ȟt = tanh(W·[rt ⊙ ht−1, xt]).
New state ht = (1 − zt) ⊙ ht−1 + zt ⊙ ȟt.

Notice the elegant trick: zt and 1 − zt are complementary. Where the gate says 1, you take the new candidate. Where it says 0, you keep the old state. One vector decides both directions at once.

When should you pick which? GRUs are smaller and a touch faster — pick them by default. LSTMs sometimes edge ahead on very long sequences or tasks where the explicit cell state matters. In practice they're close; experiment.

Both architectures share the same trick: let the network decide what to remember. The gates learn this from data — no human writes a rule. That's what made gated RNNs the dominant sequence model from 1997 until Transformers took over.

Quick check

How many gates does a GRU use compared to an LSTM?

GRU collapses forget + input into one update gate (with its complement) and replaces output with a simpler exposure. Two gates total. LSTM keeps three plus a separate cell state.

Section 7 · slides 22–42 · Seq2Seq

Sequence to sequence

Translate "I love you" into French. The input is three English tokens; the output is two French tokens ("Je t'aime"). Different length on each side. Different vocabulary. The classic answer: stack two RNNs.

Encoder: read the input one token at a time. Throw away the per-step outputs. Keep only the final hidden state — call it the context vector. Treat it as a summary of the whole input.

Decoder: a second RNN, started from the encoder's context vector. Feed it a <START> token. It produces a token. Feed that token back as the next input. Repeat until it emits <END>.

Because words can't be fed to a neural network directly, each token first passes through an embedding — a learned vector representation. Two words with similar meaning get similar vectors. Word2Vec was the classical recipe; today the embedding is just a trainable lookup table at the bottom of the network.

Press ▶. Three English tokens flow into the encoder. The final hidden state — the gold ball — becomes the decoder's seed. Two French tokens emerge one by one, each fed back as the next input.

During training, the decoder is sometimes fed the true previous token instead of its own prediction — a trick called teacher forcing. Otherwise early mistakes propagate and the model never converges.

Quick check

What does the context vector represent?

The encoder runs through every input token and accumulates information into its hidden state. The last hidden state is taken to summarise the full sequence and is handed to the decoder.

Section 8 · slides 40–43 · the bottleneck

The bottleneck — and why we need attention

Look back at Seq2Seq. Every word of the input gets compressed into one fixed-size vector before the decoder starts. For a five-word sentence, this is fine. For a fifty-word paragraph, it isn't. The first words leave faint traces; the last words dominate.

Olier's metaphor: each new step is half new input, half old summary. The next step is half newer input, half (half-of-the-old-summary). After a few hops, anything from the start of the sequence is a tiny fraction of a tiny fraction. Long-range information dies in compression.

The bottleneck problem. The decoder only sees the final encoder state. Everything else — every intermediate h1, h2, …, hT−1 — is discarded. Useful information about which input word matters for which output word is gone.

What if the decoder could look back at all of the encoder hidden states and decide, at each output step, which ones to focus on? That mechanism is called attention. It made translation work properly. And once attention existed, somebody asked: do we even still need the recurrence? The answer is no — and that answer is the Transformer.

Drag the slider. As the sequence gets longer, the bar shows how much of each input position survives in the final context vector. Watch the start of the sequence get crushed.

Recurrence gave neural networks memory. Gates made that memory robust. Seq2Seq turned it into a translator. Attention — coming next — removed the bottleneck. Then self-attention threw out recurrence altogether and the modern era began.

Quick check

Why is the single-context-vector design a problem for long sequences?

A fixed-size vector has finite capacity. As the sequence grows, every token competes for space, and the earliest tokens lose. Attention lets the decoder peek at every encoder state directly.

Check yourself

Q1. What is the role of the hidden state ht in an RNN?

The hidden state is the network's memory. It is updated at every step and read at the next step, so context flows along the sequence.

Q2. In the unrolled RNN, the matrix Wh is:

Weight sharing across time is what makes a recurrent network work for sequences of arbitrary length with a fixed parameter count.

Q3. Why does training a long-sequence RNN run into the vanishing gradient problem?

Each step backward multiplies by ∂ht/∂ht−1. Most of the time these factors are small. Multiply enough of them and the result is essentially zero.

Q4. A gate, in an LSTM or GRU, is implemented as:

A sigmoid output near 0 closes the gate; near 1 opens it. Element-wise multiplication then chooses what fraction of each component to keep.

Q5. The LSTM's cell state ct exists to:

The cell state evolves additively, gated by the forget gate. Its backward path doesn't repeatedly multiply small numbers — long-range gradients survive.

Q6. What does the LSTM's forget gate control?

ft is multiplied element-wise into ct−1. A value of 0 wipes that component; a value of 1 keeps it intact.

Q7. The GRU's update gate zt trades off between:

ht = (1 − zt) ⊙ ht−1 + zt ⊙ ȟt. z says how much new, 1 − z says how much old.

Q8. In a Seq2Seq model, the context vector handed to the decoder is:

The encoder reads the whole input sequence and builds up its hidden state. Its final state is taken to encode the entire input and is used to seed the decoder.

Q9. Why is the context vector a bottleneck?

The whole input has to be compressed into one fixed-size vector. Beyond a certain length, that capacity simply runs out — earlier tokens are crushed.

Q10. Why use word embeddings instead of one-hot vectors?

A one-hot of a 50,000-word vocabulary is a 50,000-dim sparse vector with no notion of meaning. Embeddings are 100–300 dim, dense, and carry semantic structure (king − man + woman ≈ queen).

Q11. Teacher forcing, during Seq2Seq training, means:

Without teacher forcing, an early wrong prediction snowballs through the rest of the output. Feeding the true previous token keeps training stable.

Q12. What problem does attention solve, that motivates the next lesson?

Attention removes the single-vector bottleneck. The decoder, at each step, computes a weighted sum over all encoder hidden states, focusing where it matters. Self-attention takes this further and eliminates recurrence entirely — the Transformer.
