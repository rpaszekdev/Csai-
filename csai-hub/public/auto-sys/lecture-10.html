1. The Brooks Provocation — “Doomed to Succeed”

Week 10 opens with a deliberately uncomfortable framing. Before any tool tour, Murat plants a famous line from Rodney Brooks at the centre of the lecture and refuses to move on until the implication has been chewed.

Mandatory quote Exam

“The problem with simulations is that they are doomed to succeed.” — Rodney Brooks

Mandatory question: Why does Rodney complain about simulators?

The complaint is not that simulators are useless — it’s that a simulator is a closed world built by the same engineer who writes the controller. Physics, sensor noise, friction, lighting, contact dynamics, and timing are assumptions baked into the simulator. So if your controller works in sim, it has only proven that it works against your own assumptions about the world — not against the world itself.

That’s the seed of what later in robotics literature became called the reality gap: the distance between simulated success and real-world failure. Brooks’s line is the lecture’s spine — every section after it is either a reason to use simulators anyway, or a warning about how to use them honestly.

2. Why Simulate? — The Trade-off Slide

If simulators are doomed to succeed, why use them at all? Slide 07 lays out the answer as a two-column trade. On the cost side of working with real hardware:

  • Costly in case of accidents — a humanoid that falls over is a repair bill, not a respawn.
  • Safety concern for operators — a 30 kg arm running buggy code is a hazard.
  • Time and budget — physical iterations are slow, and most labs cannot afford parallel hardware.

On the benefit side of running in simulation:

  • Your computer can be your lab — no booking, no setup, no teardown.
  • Fast prototyping — reset is a keypress, not an afternoon.
  • More time for algorithm development rather than dealing with hardware quirks (broken cables, flat batteries, drift).

Definition Exam

Definition

A robotics simulator is “a simulator used to create an application for a physical robot without depending on the physical machine, thus saving cost and time. In some cases, such applications can be transferred onto a physical robot (or rebuilt) without modification.” (Wikipedia, slide 08)

Two phrases in that definition do the heavy lifting. “Without depending on the physical machine” explains the economic case. “Without modification” is the aspiration — and the same aspiration Brooks is sceptical of. Sim-to-real transfer is rarely free; this lecture is largely about the conditions under which that transfer is honest.

3. Simulation vs Computer Graphics

The next four slides set up an opposition that students often blur: a physics simulator is not a game engine, even though both produce moving 3D pictures.

Physics simulatorComputer graphics / game engine
Uses equations — the next frame is computed from physical laws (Newtonian mechanics, contact forces, friction, gravity). Gives an impression — the next frame is whatever looks right; physics is approximated, scripted, or faked when convenient.
Behaviour is governed by physics. Recall F = ma. Behaviour is governed by the storyboard. The hero never trips unless the writer wants them to.
An object you didn’t script can still move — because forces act on it. An object you didn’t animate sits still.
Why this matters

If you train a controller in a game engine, your robot is learning to satisfy a visual environment, not a physical one. Forces, mass, inertia, and contact dynamics are the bridge to the real robot. In simulations, your agent’s behaviour is governed by physics — that’s the entire reason the bridge is even there.

The slide deck links out to myphysicslab.com as a small in-browser demonstration that physics-driven motion is computed, not animated.

4. Physics-Based Simulators & Dynamical Systems

Murat now stitches two definitions together to land a precise statement of what a physics-based simulator actually is.

Definition 1 — Simulation Exam

“Simulation is the process of using a computer to approximate how a dynamic system (here a robot) evolves in time. To this end, if the computer relies on the laws of physics to predict the behaviour of the dynamic system of interest, then the simulation is called physics based.”

Definition 2 — Dynamical system Exam

“In physics, a dynamical system is described as a particle or ensemble of particles whose state varies over time and thus obeys differential equations involving time derivatives.” (Wikipedia)

Slide 15 deliberately stacks the two definitions and then asks: based on (1) and (2), what can you simulate? The trick is that the answer is hidden inside the definitions:

  • Anything whose state can be described as variables that change over time.
  • Anything whose evolution can be expressed as differential equations.
  • Anything whose behaviour is predictable from physical laws.

That’s the formal answer. The slide deck then gives a more practical answer in the next batch of slides: you can simulate the world — if you can model the right four (then six) categories of stuff.

5. What Can You Actually Simulate?

Slides 16–18 are the “ingredients list” of a physics-based robot simulator. To simulate the world you need to capture the physics of four kinds of things, then add two more for the robot itself.

The four world elements Exam

  1. Rigid bodies — the easy case. Constant shape, contact via collision detection. Most industrial robots, table legs, walls, boxes.
  2. Compliant (deformable, soft) bodies — bend, stretch, deform. Cloth, soft grippers, soft skin, flexible cables.
  3. Fluids — hardest of the four. Water, blood, mud. Often skipped or coarsely approximated in robotics simulators.
  4. Terrains — ground geometry and material. Hard floor vs gravel vs grass changes contact dynamics dramatically.

Plus two robot-side elements Exam

  1. Sensors — cameras, lidar, IMU, touch. The simulator must produce plausible sensor readings, not just correct physics.
  2. Actuators — motors, joints, soft actuators. Must convert commanded torques/velocities into the same forces a real motor would produce.
Why the list matters

The four-plus-two list is the operational answer to the question raised on slide 15. Each item is a place where the reality gap can open up. A simulator that nails rigid bodies but fakes deformables will let you train manipulation policies that work on blocks but fall apart on cloth. A simulator that fakes sensor noise will give you controllers that don’t generalise to a real camera.

Provocation question

Mandatory question

How can you model legged locomotion (walking, running, jumping) without using any animations?

What is one of the physical systems used to generate legged locomotion for humanoid robots?

The point of the provocation is to make “animation” a dirty word. If your humanoid walks because you scripted a walk-cycle, you have a game character, not a robot. To get walking out of a simulator you have to model joints, torques, contact, balance, and gravity — the same ingredients as the real world. Classical answer: spring-loaded inverted pendulum (SLIP) and related compass-gait or ZMP-based dynamic walkers, evaluated through a physics engine, not painted on top of one.

Sim-to-real

PyBullet is the only one in this list whose mission statement explicitly mentions sim-to-real transfer. That’s the formal name of the bridge Brooks complained about. The fact that a flagship simulator advertises itself by that phrase tells you the field has accepted the reality gap as a first-class research problem rather than a footnote.

Bridging sim and real

Slide 27 makes a small but important note: a simulator is not just for offline experiments — you can also link a virtual robot to an actual robot, running the controller against a digital twin while the physical body mirrors the result. That’s how platforms like the Neurorobotics Platform manage closed-loop physical experiments.

7. Which Simulator Should You Use?

Slide 28 is the one to memorise — it’s the lecture’s decision tree, written out in plain English.

Decide by deployment style

  • Web-based simulator — if you don’t want to deal with the installation hassle. Trade-off: less control, fewer features.
  • Locally installed simulator — if you have time to climb the learning curve and want full control over your environment.

Decide by support model

  • All of these are open-source, which is good for inspection but means there’s no help desk. You should solve your own problem or find a solution from the web. Forum-archaeology is part of the job.

How to phrase it on the exam

The exam-friendly answer to “which simulator?” is three sentences: (1) name the robot or task, (2) name the matching simulator, (3) justify with one line referring to integration / robot fit / paradigm fit. Don’t list all six.

8. The Toolchain Pivot — OS & Terminal

Slide 29 is a soft pivot: Murat openly admits the rest of the deck is only suggestions, not exam content. But he insists the suggestions would be great assets for your background, especially if you’re considering work in AI, ML, or Robotics. The deck then walks through the working environment of a researcher who actually ships robotics code.

Operating system

  • A GNU/Linux-based OS will facilitate development progress — most robotics tooling (ROS, Gazebo, qiBullet, iCub) is built and tested on Linux first.
  • You will solve more bugs faster because the forum answers exist. StackOverflow, ROS Answers, GitHub issues — all skewed Linux.
  • You don’t need a new computer: use dual boot, or run a virtual machine.

Terminal / konsole

  • Not every machine you’ll use has a GUI — servers and HPC clusters don’t.
  • You don’t need to click, drag and drop. With a terminal you can be super-efficient.
  • Every command is composable: pipes, redirection, scripts. You can replay the same workflow on any machine.
Why this is in a simulator lecture

It’s in the same lecture because simulators are heavyweight pieces of software. Most of the friction is not “how does the physics work?” — it’s “why won’t the GPU driver load?”, “why is the ROS topic empty?”, “why does the URDF parser segfault?”. The toolchain section is Murat front-loading the boring infrastructure problems so the simulator section doesn’t collapse on first contact with reality.

9. Python, Virtual Environments & pip

Slide 33: Python 3 is the lingua franca of machine learning and is easy to learn, read, and adapt for ML projects. It comes with a deep ecosystem of ML and data-science libraries. Murat points to codingbat.com/python and the Stanford CS231n NumPy tutorial as warm-up material.

Virtual environments Recommended

Definition

A virtual environment is “a cooperatively isolated runtime environment that allows Python users and applications to install and upgrade Python distribution packages without interfering with the behaviour of other Python applications running on the same system.” (docs.python.org)

  • Use virtualenvwrapper to create an isolated Python environment.
  • Organises all software packages in one place.
  • For team projects: eliminates development conflicts among project members — everyone runs the same dependency set.
  • Useful for reproducible research — lock the environment, ship the lockfile.

The four-verb workflow

  1. Create — mkvirtualenv myproject
  2. Workon — activate it before doing anything
  3. Install a package — pip install ... happens inside the env
  4. Exit (deactivate) — back to the system Python
Why this matters for simulators

Robotics simulator stacks are infamous for dependency hell: incompatible NumPy versions, conflicting CUDA toolkits, ROS packages that demand a specific Python. Virtual environments are not optional — they’re what stops your Week 10 PyBullet experiment from breaking your Week 06 unsupervised-learning code.

pip — the package manager

  • Python is a batteries-included language — basics like string manipulation are built in.
  • With pip, you install packages that aren’t in the standard library: NumPy, PyBullet, MuJoCo bindings, scikit-learn, matplotlib, and so on.
  • Pip resolves dependencies automatically — but it does not resolve them well across environments. That’s the virtualenv’s job.
10. Libraries, Git, IDEs, Editors

The final stretch is a quick rundown of supporting tools. None of it is exam-bearing — all of it is what real robotics researchers actually use day-to-day.

Core ML & viz libraries

  • scikit-learn — Python-based machine learning. Classical ML algorithms (SVMs, random forests, k-means, PCA), great for sanity-check baselines on sensor data before reaching for deep models.
  • Matplotlib — the workhorse plotting library. Interactive visualisation of trajectories, sensor traces, learning curves.
  • Seaborn — built on top of matplotlib; quicker for statistical plots.

Git — version control

  • Git is a distributed version control system.
  • Use it for coordination and collaboration — especially relevant for the team project.
  • Every commit is a checkpoint; every branch is a safe place to experiment.

An integrated development environment is “a software application that provides comprehensive facilities to computer programmers for software development.” (Wikipedia)

Text editors close the deck because they’re the tool you’ll need when none of the other tools are available: lightweight, language-agnostic, extensible. If you’re ssh’d into a cluster machine running a simulation overnight, you don’t install PyCharm — you edit the config in nano and re-run.

Key Takeaways
  1. Brooks’s warning: simulators are doomed to succeed because the engineer who writes the controller also wrote the world. A working sim is evidence of self-consistency, not of reality.
  2. Why simulate anyway: safety, cost, speed, and the ability to use your laptop as a lab. The trade-off slide (07) is the canonical justification.
  3. Definition: a robotics simulator builds an application for a physical robot without depending on the physical machine; physics-based means the next state is computed from physical laws, not authored.
  4. Sim is not a game engine: physics simulators use equations, game engines give an impression. F = ma is the slogan.
  5. The 4 + 2 element list is the operational answer to “what can you simulate?”: rigid bodies, deformable bodies, fluids, terrains — plus sensors and actuators. Each is a place where the reality gap can open.
  6. Simulator decision tree: humanoid → iCub · spiking nets → Neurorobotics Platform · Pepper/NAO → qiBullet · RL → MuJoCo · ROS stack → Gazebo · sim-to-real / Python ML → PyBullet.
  7. Web-based vs locally installed: web for zero-install convenience, local for full control and the willingness to climb a learning curve.
  8. The toolchain pivot: Linux + terminal + Python 3 + virtualenv + pip + Git + a real editor. Not exam-bearing, but Murat positions it as a research-survival kit.
  9. Reproducibility: isolated environments and versioned code are how a simulator experiment becomes a result you can defend — and how you avoid breaking your other coursework.
