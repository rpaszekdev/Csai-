1. Why Emotion in Autonomous Systems?

Lecture 08 closes Module 04. The content is conceptual, not numeric — the prof deliberately steps away from formal models because the topic resists clean math. The framing comes from Lisa Feldman Barrett, a cognitive scientist whose theory of constructed emotion and predictive brain hypothesis run through the entire lecture.Prof

Mandatory quote — Barrett

"Through prediction and correction, your brain continually creates and revises your mental model of the world. It's a huge, ongoing simulation that constructs everything you perceive while determining how you act."

"An emotion is your brain's creation of what your bodily sensations mean, in relation to what is going on around you in the world."

We have already seen emotion-flavoured agents earlier in the course. Recall the Braitenberg vehicles:

  • Vehicle 2a / 2b — fear / aggression. Two sensors, two motors, + couplings; 2a orients away from the source, 2b toward it.
  • Vehicle 3 (Love) — same wiring with − couplings; 3a permanent love, 3b explorer love.
Exam — Braitenberg emotions Exam

Braitenberg vehicles do not truly feel emotion. The emotion is visible to the on-looker only — the agent does not possess the internal state. We are describing behaviour with emotion words; the vehicle does not experience fear or love.

2. Context in Emotion Perception

The cropped face on slide 8 looks like anger or pain. The same face on slide 9 — with the body and the tennis court visible — is Serena Williams winning at Wimbledon. The emotion flips from negative to positive purely on context.

Source: Context in Emotion Perception — Barrett et al.

Exam — context matters Exam

If you build an emotion-recognition model with only a cropped face, you will mis-classify systematically. Contextual information (scene, body, situation) is required. Same face in a hospital bed = negative; in Wimbledon = positive.

3. Mr. Data & the Functions of Emotion

Mr. Data (Star Trek) — an Android with a positronic brain and immense computational power, but unable to feel emotion. He struggles to interact with humans and eventually receives an "emotion chip".Prof

The prof uses Data to motivate why autonomous agents need emotion-like mechanisms. Emotion is not decorative — it is functional.

Prof anecdote Prof

Albert Heijn example: you walk in to buy protein powder. Someone screams "bomb!". Your behavioural hierarchy is rewritten in milliseconds — the protein no longer matters, evacuation does. That re-prioritisation is emotion doing functional work.

Data vs Lore — the cautionary twin

Lore is Data's twin. Same positronic brain, but Lore was programmed with emotions — and became emotionally unstable, megalomaniac, self-superior. The prof uses Lore to flag that induced emotion is not automatically good; it can destabilise an otherwise capable agent.

4. Affect / Feeling / Mood — Definitions

"Everybody knows what emotion is until someone asks them to define it." There is no universally accepted definition of emotion. The course adopts working definitions and you may pick the one that fits your project.

TermDefinitionTime-scale
Affect The underlying affective experience of an emotion or a mood — the raw "valence + arousal" felt-state. Continuous
Feeling A subjective representation of emotion. Personal, idiosyncratic. Seconds — minutes
Emotion A response to a significant internal/external event; brain's interpretation of bodily sensation in context. Seconds — minutes
Mood Diffuse affective states; longer duration, often without a clear contextual stimulus. Hours — days
Exam — keyword discrimination Exam

Mood lasts longer and may have no identifiable trigger. Feeling is a subjective experience of an emotion. Affect is the underlying experience. Emotion is the brain's response to a significant event. Distinguish all four.

Culture-specific emotions

Some emotions exist in one culture and not in another — e.g. Dutch "gezellig", German "Schadenfreude" (joy at another's misfortune), and a Japanese emotion for the regret felt after a bad haircut. The prof also chatted with ChatGPT and invented an emotion ("technology") — the joy of a student who successfully implements HCI principles in their robot.

This matters for HRI: emotions are not universal — they are partly culturally constructed, which lines up with Barrett's theory of constructed emotion.

5. Computational Emotion Models

To put emotion into a robot we have to numerically define it. Three models are introduced.

OCC model (Ortony, Clore, Ollins)

  • Hierarchically classifies 22 emotions.
  • Three branches: consequences of events, actions of agents, aspects of objects.
  • Branches can be combined to yield compound emotions.
  • Implemented on the Kismet robot — the first OCC-style HRI model.
Exam — OCC framing Exam

Expect a true/false like: "All emotions experienced by humans can be experienced by robots using the OCC model." Answer: false — OCC encodes 22; humans experience many more, including culture-specific ones.

You will not be asked to trace a specific event through the OCC tree — only to recognise the model and its limits.

Circumplex model (Russell)

  • Projects emotions onto a 2-D space: valence × arousal.
  • High valence + high arousal = delight; low valence + high arousal = distress; etc.
  • Has expressive power for HRI experiments.
  • Flaws: angry and afraid are conceptually different but sit close together in 2-D; intensity is missing; 2-D is too thin for a complex phenomenon.

PAD model (Pleasure–Arousal–Dominance)

  • Adds a third axis — dominance / submissiveness (intensity).
  • Resolves the angry-vs-afraid collision in the circumplex by giving them different dominance scores.
  • Costs: requires domain knowledge to assign valence / arousal / dominance per event.

6. Expressing Emotion on a Robot

Robots do not need a humanoid face to express emotion. Channels include:

  • Facial expression — eyebrows, mouth, eye position (e.g. Kismet — surprise / happiness / sadness / interest / disgust).
  • LEDs & colour — chest LEDs blinking fast can read as anger; iCub example with no movable face still reads as "angry" via LED eyebrows.
  • Movement & posture — slow approach vs throwing; non-verbal gestures.
  • Sound & speech pattern — beeping, tone, tempo (Keepon robot syncs to music).
  • Gaze — following the human's eyes while serving water.

Prof story — bodily maps Prof

Slide 29 (Nummenmaa et al., PNAS 2014): self-report colour maps of body regions activated by each emotion. Different emotions are topographically distinct and culturally universal. The "shame" map looks like Spider-Man — the prof saw the same picture on Reddit the night before a Lisbon talk and could not stop laughing. Whenever I see that figure, I remember the Reddit comment. Use it as a guide for which body parts a robot should activate when expressing each emotion.

Controller-wise: standard feedback control reaches a target position. Affective control adds a social channel — the robot still moves marker-to-hand, but it does so with gaze, posture and pace tuned for the human. The integration is hard; there is no standard recipe.

7. Constructed Emotion, Degeneracy, Predictive Brain

Barrett's theory of constructed emotion (slide 31) makes four moves the prof emphasises:Prof

  1. "Universal" basic emotions may not be universal. Smiling does not mean the same thing across cultures.
  2. Emotions regulate the body-budget — the brain spends ~20% of your energy and uses emotion to allocate it. You "dislike" a movie so you stop watching, conserving energy.
  3. Degeneracy — many different bodily states can produce the same emotion, and one bodily state can produce different emotions. Like dice: 1+2 = 4+(−1) = 3; the sum is constant, the constituents vary. Same happiness can come from a Star Wars film, the script, or John Williams' score.
  4. Predictive brain hypothesis supports it — the brain is constantly predicting the body's next state and constructing emotion as part of that simulation.
Prof — degeneracy as a thesis hook Prof

The prof says "degeneracy" is a high-leverage keyword for thesis projects, internships and interviews. If asked about a database / ML system in an interview, framing it through degeneracy — many constituents, same outcome — earns you points. His own lab pushes degeneracy into robots; it is hard but very interesting.

Kirtay's own work — emergent emotion on iCub

(slide 32) During his PhD with the iCub robot, the prof studied emergent emotion: the robot avoids costly decisions by short-cutting the decision process — it stops searching for the optimal action and stays at a good-enough solution. This is the function of emotion (short-cut cognition) realised computationally without a face.Prof

The current extension links emotion to energy consumption and trust: if a partner robot makes you waste energy (more retries, more failures), you trust it less for the next task. This connects emotion → trust → robot-robot interaction (next section).

8. Robot-Robot Interaction (RRI) & Trust

Where HRI handles human ↔ robot, RRI handles robot ↔ robot. The prof flags this as one of his keywords for the future of the field.Prof

  • Teaching scales — humans cannot teach every robot. One well-trained robot teaches another, that one teaches another → exponential propagation.
  • Trust emerges from repeated interaction. If two partner robots assist you and one wastes energy / makes more errors, you'll prefer the lower-error robot for the next task — calibrated trust.
  • The lab models trust via energy consumption and number of successful uses (e.g. correctly placed pick-and-place over 100 trials).

Trust types in HRI

TypeBehavioural patternRisk
Calibrated trustTrust matches the robot's actual reliabilityHealthy
Over-trustUser trusts beyond capability — ignores warningsDangerous (Tesla autopilot, surgical systems)
Under-trustUser refuses to use a competent systemWastes capability; product flop

Anthropomorphism & measurement

How do you measure anthropomorphism in HRI? Mostly questionnaires — Likert scales like "I dislike → I like the robot" or "robot is awful → robot is nice". You can also use behavioural indicators: language usage, whether the user follows social rules toward the robot, etc. Most HRI experiments collect before/after surveys.

Prof — symbiotic future Prof

The prof's community (especially Japan) believes in human-robot symbiotic societies. Without emotion-like mechanisms in the robots, you lose memories, niceness, social bonding — the entire glue that makes interaction work. Emotion is integral to that future.

Affective computing — Picard & Norman

  • Rosalind Picard — Affective Computing (MIT). The originator of the field; argues machines must have emotion-like mechanisms, not merely could.
  • Don Norman — Emotional Design (preceded Design of Everyday Things). The HCI link — the prof flags it as essential reading if you care about emotional product design.Prof
9. Social Robotics — Definition, Form, Guidelines
Exam — social robot definition Exam

"A social robot is an autonomous or semi-autonomous robot that interacts and communicates with humans by following the behavioural norms expected by the people with whom the robot is intended to interact."

A social robot has five attributes (slide 39):

  1. Form — abstract / biomorphic / zoomorphic / anthropomorphic.
  2. Modality — uni-modal vs multi-modal (single sensor/output channel vs many).
  3. Social norms — non-negotiable rules of the target society.
  4. Autonomy — autonomous or semi-autonomous (human intervenes in failure).
  5. Interactivity — must respond to human action.
Behavioural norms — definition

"Shared standards of acceptable behaviour by groups. Social norms can be informal understandings that govern members of a society, or be codified into rules and laws." (Wikipedia)

Design guidelines (Bartneck et al.) Exam

  1. Form should match abilities — "form follows function". A humanoid is expected to have robust speech recognition; a dog-form sets different expectations and may be more honest.
  2. Mimic human-human dialogue in human-robot dialogue, and manage communication failures — recognise, respond to, and where possible employ all modalities humans naturally use.
  3. Mimic human social norms, provide a consistent set of behaviours, be aware of its own social role, world knowledge, and what it does not know.

10. Failure Modes & Ethics of Affective HRI

Slide 46 lists the potential problems with social / affective HRI:

  • Addressing user expectations — humanoid form promises capability the controller cannot deliver. Mismatch breaks trust.
  • Addiction — emotionally engaging robots can capture users (esp. children, elderly).
  • Attention theft — robots compete with humans for attention.
  • Loss of interest — novelty fades; many social-robot products fail in the market for this reason.
  • Robot abuse — humans can mistreat robots; raises ethical questions (and design questions for sturdy robots).
Prof — why social robots flop Prof

The accompanying HRI textbook podcast spends time on this. Many social interactive robots fail in the market because expectations diverge from capability and novelty wears off. The prof recommends listening to it while preparing demos.

Exam — what to memorise Exam

For the exam: functions of emotion, affect / feeling / mood / emotion definitions, OCC / Circumplex / PAD at a high level, social robot definition + 5 attributes, 3 design guidelines, and the keyword HRI = social world (vs robotics = physical world). You will not be asked to define "emotion" — there is no agreed definition.

Key Takeaways
  1. HRI lives in the social world; robotics in the physical world. Both share a body, but HRI adds gaze, posture, voice, proxemics, social norms.
  2. Affect / feeling / mood / emotion are different things. Feeling = subjective. Mood = diffuse, long, often without trigger. Affect = underlying experience. Emotion = brain's interpretation of bodily sensation in context.
  3. Emotion is functional: modulates & motivates behaviour, communicates, bonds, recalls memory, short-cuts cognition, shifts behavioural hierarchies (Albert Heijn / fire-alarm example).
  4. OCC (22 categorical) · Circumplex (valence × arousal) · PAD (+ dominance) — three computational emotion models. Each has expressive power and limits; PAD resolves the angry-vs-afraid collision.
  5. Context wins. Cropped face → wrong inference (Serena Williams). An emotion-recognition model without context will systematically mis-classify.
  6. Mr. Data and Lore — emotion is necessary and dangerous. Without it: poor social interaction. Wrongly induced: instability and megalomania.
  7. Constructed emotion + degeneracy + predictive brain (Barrett) — same outcome (e.g. happiness) can arise from many constituents; the brain spends body-budget via emotion. Degeneracy is a high-value thesis keyword.
  8. Robots can express emotion without a face — LEDs (iCub eyebrows), movement, posture, sound (Keepon syncs to music), gaze. The Kismet face is one option, not a requirement.
  9. Social robot = autonomous/semi-autonomous + interactive + follows behavioural norms. Five attributes: form, modality, norms, autonomy, interactivity. Three design guidelines: form-matches-ability, manage dialogue + failures, follow social norms consistently.
  10. RRI & trust — robots can teach robots (exponential scale). Trust is calibrated from energy consumption and successful uses. Over-trust and under-trust are both failure modes; affective HRI products often flop from mis-set expectations and attention theft.
