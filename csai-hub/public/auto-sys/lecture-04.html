1. Two Quotes, Two Schools of Thought

Lecture 04 opens with a deliberately staged collision between two philosophers — the lecture's "mandatory quotes". The whole week sits in the tension between them.

QuoteSchool
"I think, therefore I am." — Descartes Classical / cognitivist: intelligence = abstract symbol manipulation. The body is a vessel; the mind could in principle be ported anywhere.
"There is more intelligence in your body than in your best wisdom." — Nietzsche Embodied: the body is a contributor to intelligence, not its container. Cognition is grounded in a physical, situated agent.
Prof stance Prof

"Body is also a contributor of the intelligence — and we will have a stance on this school of thought. Why? Because in your project you will do embodied agents. We will treat having a body as a prerequisite for being intelligent in robotics."

The five framing questions for the lecture Exam

  1. Do we need a body to be considered an intelligent agent?
  2. What is the role of the body in cognition / emotion?
  3. What is it like to be a robot?
  4. Where does the body end? Where does the external world start?
  5. Can you have a sense of self without having a body?

Painter's brush, the dinosaur scene in Jurassic Park, your phone — these were all probes from the prof to push you on Q4. Empathy, tool-use and pain-by-proxy all suggest the boundary of "self" leaks past the skin.

2. What Is Embodiment? + Morphology
Definitions · core Exam

Embodiment — the term used to indicate agents that possess a physical body. (In simulation, we say "virtually embodied" — but unless stated otherwise, "embodied" defaults to physical.)

Morphology — how the parts of that body, made of different materials, are arranged. Morphology constrains the behavior of the robot.

Concrete consequence of the morphology constraint: a human-morphology robot solves tasks on land but is helpless underwater; a fish-morphology robot is the inverse. If you want a behavior, design the morphology that affords it.

Morphology categories you must be able to label Exam

  • Human morphology — Pepper, Nao, Affetto baby robot, hyper-realistic Affetto.
  • Animal / zoomorphic — Unitree dog, fish, octopus arm. The starfish-shaped soft robot = "starfish-shaped animal morphology" (NOT "hand" — the prof said this will be marked wrong).
  • Specialized / hybrid — Osaka restaurant waiter robot resembles a plate-shaped serving morphology.
  • Plant-based / abstract — sometimes "no clear morphology" is the right answer.
Exam · the canonical question Exam

Q1: "Is ChatGPT an embodied agent?" — NO. ChatGPT alone has no physical body. Our definition requires a body. (Even the prof's note: he asked GPT itself, and it answered "no, I am not embodied.")

Q2: "Are Pepper and Nao embodied agents?" — YES. Both possess a physical body that exists in the real environment.

Twist (lecture discussion): if you connect ChatGPT as the cloud brain of a Pepper robot, the combined system can be considered an embodied agent. Standalone LLM ≠ embodied; LLM + physical body interacting with the world = embodied.

Note for the exam: the slide PDF originally typoed "Pepper and Nao = YES" with the answer column blank — the lecture confirmed the answer.

3. Two Provocations — Dead Fish & Passive Walker

Recall from Week 01: the prof asked "do we really need a brain to generate behavior?" and showed a passive walker. Week 04 returns to that thread and pushes harder.

The dead trout

  • A dead fish, placed upside-down in flowing water, still swims — apparently perfectly.
  • It has a body. It has no motor control coming from the brain. It only has environmental interaction with the water.
  • Yet swimming behavior emerges.

The passive walker (callback to W01)

  • An artificial system with no motors, no sensors, no electricity, no brain.
  • Walks down a slight incline using only its weight, its leg geometry, and gravity.
  • Same lesson as the trout: an intelligent-looking function emerges from body × environment, no controller required.
Exam · classic question Exam

"Passive walkers are brainless — so are they robots?"

YES, they are robots. Recall the 6 elements (W01): body, sensors, actuators, brain, environment, function. The passive walker satisfies them because its body performs the same function as the brain — the morphology IS the controller.

This is exactly the Nietzsche-side evidence the prof needs: "the body is doing something. And that something is called embodied intelligence or embodied cognition."

4. Classical vs Modern Approach to Intelligence

The lecture explicitly contrasts two paradigms. Know the contrast cold for the exam.

AspectClassical (cognitivist)Modern (embodied)
Locus of intelligence Brain & central processing — symbols, algorithms, abstract computation. Brain × Body × Environment — cognition emerges from system-environment interaction.
Body Optional; an output channel. Required — physical instantiation that shapes the cognition.
Environment Modeled as input data. A constitutive partner — the agent is in a world it perturbs.
Picture used in slides Person sitting still, "thinking." Person juggling — coupled to objects in real time.
Aligned with Descartes, GOFAI, AlexNet-style deep learning on disembodied datasets. Nietzsche, Pfeifer & Scheier, Bongard, modern robotics.
Three components Prof

Embodied intelligence has three components: BRAIN, BODY, ENVIRONMENT. Sometimes the body can absorb the brain's role (passive walkers), but ideally all three interact and intelligence emerges from that coupling.

Why the prof bans simulators for the demo day: "simulations are doomed to fail because we cannot simulate reality. That's why we are avoiding simulations as embodied-intelligence researchers." You may train in simulation; you must show physical robots on demo day.

5. Pfeifer & Bongard — How the Body Shapes the Way We Think

The lecture is adapted from the book How the Body Shapes the Way We Think: A New View of Intelligence by Rolf Pfeifer and Josh Bongard. The prof: "old book, content still super valid… it will change your perspective on what intelligent systems are."

The motto Prof

"Intelligence cannot merely exist in the form of an abstract algorithm but requires a physical instantiation, a body." — Pfeifer & Scheier. Repeated multiple times. "That's our motto."

Pfeifer's "Crazy Bird" thought experiment

Three creatures, identical control (wheels rotating at constant speed), different bodies → three radically different behaviors emerge. The prof asked the class three times: "does it make sense?"

  • You cannot blame the controller — the controller is the same.
  • Therefore the difference comes from the shape and the materials.
  • Identical brain + different bodies = different behavior. Identical body + different brains = different behavior. Both halves matter.

Embodied, simulated, embodied non-collocated Exam

Pfeifer's textbook gives three deployment configurations:

  1. Embodied — physical robot acting in the real environment.
  2. Simulated — virtual robot in a virtual environment (virtually embodied).
  3. Embodied non-collocated — physical robot whose brain runs elsewhere (cloud). The brain is elsewhere from the body but they interact in real time.

(The prof noted the original figure caption mixes left/right labels — read the diagram itself, not the caption.)

This is the structural justification for why a Pepper robot driven by a cloud LLM still counts as embodied: the brain doesn't have to live inside the skin.

6. The Role of Body & Movement in Cognition

Held & Hein active vs passive kittens

Two kittens raised in identical visual environments, but only one could move freely; the second was passively transported by the first.

  • Active kittens — developed normal visual perception; could climb stairs, avoid drop-offs.
  • Passive kittens — significant, permanent visual-perception deficits. Functionally blind on a visual cliff. Could not recover.
Lesson Exam

Same brain. Same visual input. Only self-generated movement differed. Conclusion: perception requires action. The brain alone cannot rescue the system. Body + environment + agency are co-constitutive.

ImageNet (AlexNet) vs Baby Joe

SystemInputs neededGeneralization
AlexNet (disembodied CNN) ~15 million high-res images, 2,200 categories, GPU clusters. Achieves classification accuracy only after massive training; brittle to viewpoint/material change.
Baby Joe (~2 yrs old) One example + a name + the ability to handle the object. "Generalizes that name in an adult-like manner." Sees an Albert Heijn cup once → recognizes a Starbucks cup as a cup weeks later.

The infant's secret is multimodal embodied interaction: rotating, mouthing, banging, looking — extracting invariant representations through the body. AlexNet has no body and no agency, so it must brute-force what an embodied agent gets cheaply.

Why robots should be embodied Prof

"Embodied (robotic) models that share some bodily and sensorimotor features with the organism they are supposed to model might be preferable to purely computational or mathematical models." (Kirtay et al., 2020.) → To study human affordances, use a humanoid with hands; you cannot study grasping without a graspable body.

7. Synthetic Methodology + The Four Bodies

Synthetic methodology — "understanding by building" Exam

If you want to understand a phenomenon, build a system that does it. To understand human walking, build a walking robot. To understand ant locomotion, build an ant-bot.

The ant slide (slide 21) is a beautifully compact lesson. Three steps:

  1. (a) Direct mapping of observed ant behavior into an internal representation.
  2. (b) Using that representation to control a walking robot — fails. Too complex, too brittle.
  3. (c) Apply a much simpler model — a Braitenberg-style architecture — and the robot suddenly walks plausibly.

The simpler model turns out to be a better explanation of the original ant behavior. Lesson: extract principles, do not 1:1 reverse-engineer biology. Simplify, then build.

You'll follow this approach in your project: don't try to clone an animal — extract the locomotion / sensing principle and instantiate it cheaply.

Tom Ziemke's four bodies Exam

Cognitive Robotics Ch. 11 (Ziemke). The four ways an agent can be said to "have a body":

BodyWhat it isRobotics relevance
Social bodyThe body as it appears to others — gestures, openness, signals.HRI: Pepper, Cutie — invites interaction.
Sensorimotor bodyThe body that interacts with the environment via sensors + motors.Most central for roboticists. Maps directly to W01's sensors-actuators-controller loop.
Living bodySelf-regulating, homeostatic body. Manages energy, hormones, temperature.Battery management, thermal regulation, "artificial homeostasis."
Lived bodyThe body as experienced from the inside — phenomenology, sense of self.Most philosophical; consciousness territory. The lecture skips this in depth.
In-class philosophical probe Prof

Zlatev's claim: "a robot with bodily structures, interaction patterns, and development similar to those of human beings could possibly recapitulate [human] ontogenesis, leading to emergence of intentionality, consciousness, and meaning."

Agree or disagree? The prof's position: not a binary. It depends on the definition of consciousness and on whether perfect functional / structural replication suffices. Open question — but practically, "if you replicate everything, the robot does what you do" is a defensible functionalist position.

8. Morphological Computation
Definition · core Exam

Morphological computation = the ability of the body to perform the same function as the brain. "Offloading computation from the brain to the body."

The two regimes Exam

  • Brain co-determines the actions of the robot — quasi-passive walkers walking on flat terrain (need a controller pulse to keep going).
  • Body co-determines the actions of the robot — pure passive walkers (no controller, downhill only).

The same passive walker continuum from W01 is now formalized: morphological computation is the name for what the passive walker is doing.

Advantages

  • The robot can perform regulations that would be difficult or impossible to perform via the brain alone (e.g. compliant, real-time soft contact).
  • Robots can produce "complex" behavior with "simple" brains — or no brain.
  • Computational cost goes down; mechanical compliance handles what kinematics couldn't.

Implications

  • The functions played by body and brain are not separated.
  • The role of the brain cannot be explained without considering the role of the body, and vice versa.
Cecilia Laschi · soft robotics Prof

"The use of soft materials and continuum deformations and stiffening make control more difficult in computational terms using traditional model-based robot control approaches. Therefore, the robots of tomorrow will benefit from the interconnection and interaction between morphological computation and soft robotics."

Cecilia Laschi (the prof's PhD advisor's PhD advisor) — note the lineage. The "Poseidona" octopus-arm drone gripper is her lab's flagship demonstration.

9. Evolving Bodies — Voxel-bots, Xenobots, Co-evolution

Three escalating examples of letting the body itself become the design variable.

(1) Voxel-bot evolution — three cell types

  • Goal: assemble cubic cells in a 10×10×10 voxel grid that can walk fastest on a planar surface.
  • Three cell types:
    • Bone — rigid, fixed shape.
    • Tissue — soft, deformable.
    • Muscle — periodically contracts and expands (red expand-then-contract; another muscle does opposite phase).
  • Sensorless (except local expansion/contraction state) and brainless — locomotion comes exclusively from morphology.
  • Genetic algorithm produces increasingly fast walkers — "more muscle is generally faster, but morphology decides the gait."

(2) Xenobots — living robots

  • AI evolves morphologies in simulation from passive (skin) and contractile (cardiac) cells.
  • Researchers then build the evolved morphology with actual living frog stem cells (no genetic modification).
  • Behaviors realized in vivo:
    • Swim through aqueous environments for days without human intervention.
    • "Go see, remember, come back, and tell" — fluoresce red after exposure to blue light.
    • Self-replicate — pile up loose stem cells into copies of themselves (Bongard's most recent experiment).
  • Bongard: a faster AI now optimizes a "blob of virtual soft material" into a useful soft robot in 30 seconds on a laptop — by zooming in on inefficiencies and tweaking neurons / lengthening legs to remove trial-and-error.

(3) Brain–body co-evolution (Half-Cheetah)

ComponentSetup
Body~11 cylindrical elements connected with hinge joints (general bauplan).
Brain Feed-forward neural net.
Sensors: contact sensors, segment velocity/orientation, joint angle.
Motors: torque applied to actuated joints.
  • Hand-designed morphology + evolved brain → baseline.
  • Co-evolved brain & body → outperforms baseline. Evolution discovers a morphology suited to the locomotion task.

Ideal co-evolution criteria

  1. Simple — a small set of elementary elements (analogous to amino acids).
  2. High expressive power — wide phenotype spectrum, speciation under natural selection.
  3. Evolvable — supports genetic variation; small genetic edits → meaningful morphological/behavioral edits.
10. Implications for AI & Robotics Design

Synthesizing what the lecture argues you should now believe as a designer of autonomous systems:

  1. Don't put intelligence only in the controller. A clever body buys you behaviors that no controller could afford.
  2. Pick morphology to fit the task and environment. A soft, octopus-style arm picks up an egg and a 260× body-weight bucket of water — same body, no morphology change required. A rigid claw can't.
  3. Use synthetic methodology. Start from the simplest body that could exhibit the principle, then add. (Braitenberg-style minimalism applies again.)
  4. Use the environment. The dead trout and passive walker work because the world is doing computation for them. Design with physics, not against it.
  5. Multimodal interaction beats data scale. Baby Joe vs AlexNet: the embodied agent generalizes from one example because it can act on the object. Build for action loops, not just inference.
  6. Embodied AI must "push the world." Bongard: an LLM-only system cannot become truly intelligent unless it perturbs the world physically. This is the stake the lecture plants for the rest of the course.
Exam-style question (likely) Exam

Given Pfeifer's three-bot demonstration (same controller, three bodies, three behaviors): explain why the behavior differs and what concept this illustrates.

Answer: The difference is not in the controller — that is identical (constant motor speed). It comes from morphology and material. Different shapes and materials produce different couplings with the environment, hence different emergent behaviors. This illustrates morphological computation and the embodied view of intelligence.

Key Takeaways
  1. Embodiment = an agent that possesses a physical body. Default reading is physical; "virtually embodied" only when stated. ChatGPT alone is NOT embodied; Pepper / Nao ARE.
  2. Morphology = how the body's parts (different materials) are arranged. Morphology constrains the agent's possible behaviors — and is therefore a design knob, not just an aesthetic.
  3. Two schools: Classical (Descartes / GOFAI / AlexNet) puts intelligence in the brain. Modern / embodied (Nietzsche / Pfeifer / Bongard) requires BRAIN × BODY × ENVIRONMENT.
  4. Dead fish + passive walker prove brainless systems can display intelligent behavior — body and environment together do the computation. Passive walkers are robots because the body performs the brain's function.
  5. Pfeifer's "Crazy Bird": identical control + three different bodies → three different behaviors. The body, not the brain, was the variable.
  6. Held & Hein kittens: same brain + same visual input — but only the kitten that moved itself developed normal vision. Perception requires action.
  7. Synthetic methodology = "understanding by building." Don't reverse-engineer biology 1:1; extract principles, build the simplest model that exhibits them (the ant example).
  8. Four bodies (Ziemke): social, sensorimotor (most relevant for us), living, lived.
  9. Morphological computation = body performs the function of the brain. Lets robots "produce complex behavior with simple brains, or no brain at all." Soft robotics + morphological computation = the prof's bet on the future of robotics.
  10. Voxel-bots, xenobots, brain-body co-evolution all point the same way: when the body is a free variable, simpler controllers solve harder tasks. Bongard's takeaway: AI must push the world, or it isn't really intelligent.
