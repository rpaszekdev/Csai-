2. The Perception–Action Cycle


      
Without sensors, robots are blind. The whole point of equipping a robot with sensors is to complete the perception–action cycle — sometimes called the perception–thinking–action cycle (the "thinking" part is implied even when the diagram omits it).


      
A sensor is a device that measures some physical quantity in the environment — and that quantity is always a number. Distance, pixel intensity, temperature — all reduce to numbers.


      

        
Exam framing Exam

        
"You are a designer of a robot and your task is to recognize / classify objects. What kind of sensors do you need?"

        
Answer template: camera (RGB), LIDAR (3D point cloud — less robust than vision), and a touch sensor measuring mechanical properties (friction, temperature). Prof "Vision is not a sensor. Camera is a sensor. Don't forget that and name it correctly."

      


      
For obstacle avoidance: ultrasonic / sonar, IR, laser / lidar, plus vision. For audio: microphone. For object material properties: touch sensor (mechanical, not just binary bumper).


      
There is no universal sensor. A nuclear-reactor robot and an underwater seabed sampler need totally different sensor stacks. Your sensor choice is dictated by task + environment.

    

  


  

    

      
      
      
      
    


    

      
3. Sensor Classification — Two Axes


      
Two orthogonal classifications. Every sensor sits in one cell of a 2×2 grid.


      
Axis 1 — Proprioceptive vs Exteroceptive

      

        
TypeMeasuresExamples

        
          

            Proprioceptive (PC)
            Internal state of the robot
            Joint position, joint angle, battery level (only if the API exposes it as a value)
          

          

            Exteroceptive (EC)
            Quantities in the environment
            Camera, microphone, tactile sensor, lidar
          

        
      


      
Axis 2 — Active vs Passive

      

        
TypeHowExamples

        
          

            Active (A)
            Emits energy into environment, measures the reflection. Affects the environment.
            Lidar, laser, sonar, IR rangefinders, most distance sensors
          

          

            Passive (P)
            Only receives energy. Does not affect the environment.
            Camera, compass, photodiodes, microphone
          

        
      


      

        
Exam · "Box A" question Exam

        
"Specify the category of the sensor that should be filled in Box A, and provide two examples of passive sensors used in this context."

        
60% of students fail this question every yearProf The trap: everyone writes "camera" and stops. The prof wants at least two different passive examples — e.g. camera + microphone, or camera + photodiode. "Please learn at least two of them and give me different options."

        
If your example is ambiguous (e.g. "touch sensor"), define it. "Binary touch (bumper)" vs "mechanical-property touch sensor" are different things — say which.

      


      
Why the active / passive split matters

      
    
        
  • Active sensors interfere with each other — two robots emitting IR or sonar in the same room can pollute each other's readings.
    • 
        
  • Passive sensors are stealthier and don't perturb the environment — important for biology / medical / surveillance contexts.
    • 
        
  • GPS: in the slide table some textbooks mark it as Active, but by our definition it is Passive — your phone receives satellite signals, it doesn't transmit anything to space. Prof noted the slide table has an error here.
    • 
      

    

  


  

    

      
      
      
      
    


    

      
4. Common Sensor Types — Deep Dive


      
Cameras (RGB)

      
    
        
  • Pepper has 2× RGB cameras (2560×1920 @ 1 fps, or 640×480 @ 30 fps). One is on the forehead, not in the eyes — placed there to see what's in front of the feet for obstacle avoidance.
    • 
        
  • Power-hungry: cameras draw far more current than a sonar or IR sensor — budget your battery.
    • 
        
  • Output is three matrices (R, G, B), not "what you see". Everything for the robot is just numbers.
    • 
        
  • Cheap → use as many as you want, USB lets you mount cameras anywhere (back of robot, top, etc.).
    • 
      


      
Depth sensor

      
    
        
  • Pepper has one depth sensor behind the left eye, 320×240 @ 20 fps.
    • 
        
  • Output is a distance-in-millimeters matrix per pixel.
    • 
        
  • Often paired with RGB → RGB-D camera (e.g. Intel RealSense). Color-coded depth: green = far, blue = close.
    • 
      


      
Distance sensors — sonar, IR, laser

      
Simple rule: transmit a wave, receive its reflection, time the round-trip → distance. The constant you exploit:

      
    
        
  • Speed of sound (~343 m/s) for sonar/ultrasonic.
    • 
        
  • Speed of light for IR / laser / lidar.
    • 
        
  • "Speed of sound << speed of light" — this is on slide 22 and explains the precision gap.
    • 
      


      

        
Exam · sound vs light Exam

        
Why is laser more precise than sonar? The light beam is a narrow pinpoint; you measure the distance to one point in space. Sound expands as a cone — you can only conclude "there is free space within this cone of N meters", not "the obstacle is at exactly point X". This is the fundamental precision limitation of acoustic distance sensing.

      


      
Lidar

      
SICK LMS and similar are expensive (~€1000+) but produce a 2D scan — every dot is an obstacle distance. In ROS, the scan vector is just numbers: 0.40 = 40 cm away, 0 = either out of range (too far) or too close (saturated). The controller polls this vector continuously and decides each step from the latest reading.


      
If you can't afford expensive sensors

      
    
        
  • Simulators — Webots, etc. — provide virtual versions of all common sensors.
    • 
        
  • Datasets — Google dataset search, Kaggle, Zenodo, the iCub multisensor dataset.
    • 
        
  • You can train in simulation, but the final demo must be on the real robot. Prof "We will not accept simulation projects."
    • 
      

    

  


  

    

      
      
    


    

      
5. Braitenberg Vehicles — Why They Matter


      
Braitenberg vehicles are toy thought-experiment robots invented by Valentino Braitenberg in his 1984 book Vehicles. They show that apparently complex behaviors — fear, aggression, love, exploration — can be modelled with extremely simple wiring between a sensor and a motor.


      

        
Exam-ready definition Exam

        
Braitenberg vehicles are autonomous — they satisfy all three conditions from week 01:

        
    
          
  1. No human operator required.
    2. 
          
  2. Operate for prolonged periods (until battery dies).
    3. 
          
  3. Work in unknown environments.
    4. 
        

        
They are the simplest form of autonomous system we cover — and a textbook example of reactive behavior.

      


      
Why Braitenberg is "perfect for CSAI"

      
    
        
  • Synthetic psychology — build the simplest mechanism that produces a behavior, then read what an onlooker would call it ("love", "fear", "aggression"). The vehicle is not actually in love; we project the label onto the behavior.
    • 
        
  • Robotics — direct sensor-to-motor coupling, no controller / brain / planner needed.
    • 
        
  • Even the vehicle's body plays a role (sensor placement, body shape, friction).
    • 
      


      

        
Prof framing Prof

        
"For roboticists we always think about deep RL, lots of GPUs, lots of training data. But with this kind of basic stuff we can still achieve very nice demos. Be inspired with the simplicity of these vehicles."

        
And from Braitenberg himself: "Much of this work is only interesting if you are yourself involved in it." Tinker — don't just read the book.

      

    

  


  

    

      
    


    

      
6. The Wiring Rule — + and −


      
Every Braitenberg vehicle is just a sensor wired to a motor. The sign of the connection is the entire programming language.


      

        
SignTypeBehavior

        
          

            +
            Excitatory
            Motor speed is directly proportional to sensor reading. Sensor reads 1 → motor turns 1×. Sensor reads 10 → motor turns 10×.
          

          

            −
            Inhibitory
            Motor speed is inversely proportional to sensor reading. High stimulus → motor slows down / nearly stops.
          

        
      


      

        
Default convention Exam

        
If nothing is drawn on the connection, assume + (excitatory) by default. The prof said this explicitly: "If there is nothing here, you should assume that there is a positive connection between sensor and motor."

      


      
That's the whole rulebook. Everything else (vehicles 1, 2a, 2b, 3a, 3b, 4) is just wiring choices on top of these two signs, plus the geometry of where you mount the sensors.

    

  


  

    

      
      
      
    


    

      
7. Vehicle 1 — One Sensor, One Motor


      
Setup: a single temperature sensor → one motor (positive connection by default). Motion is always forward — the vehicle cannot reverse.


      
Range: temperature reads, say, 1–99 (above 100 → water boils, below 0 → water freezes — both kill the vehicle, so we ignore the boundaries).


      
Behavior on a still sea

      
    
        
  • Hot water → motor spins fast → vehicle rushes through.
    • 
        
  • Cold water → motor spins slow → vehicle dawdles.
    • 
        
  • It cannot stop — no two sequential temperature readings are ever exactly equal in a real environment, so the motor always has some speed.
    • 
      


      

        
Exam answer template Exam

        
The behavior of Vehicle 1 is "heat-seeking" (or, equivalently, "cold-avoiding") — it moves faster in hot regions and slower in cold regions, never stopping. An onlooker would call this restless behavior "alive" — the vehicle "doesn't like cold places". Note: it doesn't actually seek anything; the body's geometry plus the speed differential produce the apparent seeking.

        
Prof "Behavior" ≠ "action". Behavior is the aggregate pattern an outside observer sees, not a single action.

      


      
The same vehicle works with any scalar sensor: swap temperature for light and you get a "light-seeker"; swap for humidity, sound, etc. The principle generalizes.

    

  


  

    

      
      
    


    

      
8. Vehicles 2a & 2b — Fear and Aggression


      
Two sensors, two motors, both connections positive (+). The only thing that differs is which sensor wires to which motor — uncrossed vs crossed.


      

        
VehicleWiringBehaviorOnlooker label

        
          

            2a
            Uncrossed — left sensor → left motor, right sensor → right motor (ipsilateral, +/+)
            When a stimulus appears on one side, the nearer sensor reads stronger → that side's motor speeds up → the vehicle turns away.
            Fear — flees the source
          

          

            2b
            Crossed — left sensor → right motor, right sensor → left motor (contralateral, +/+)
            Stimulus on one side speeds up the opposite motor → vehicle turns toward the source and rams it.
            Aggression — attacks the source
          

        
      


      

        
Jurassic Park analogy Prof

        
"Imagine the T-Rex is your retina-equipped Braitenberg. Whenever it sees the light, it approaches. If the light gets closer, it hits the light." → Vehicle 2b — aggression with a light sensor. The crossed wiring is what turns "I sense it" into "I charge at it".

      


      

        
Exam tip — wiring vs sign Exam

        
For 2a vs 2b: both signs are +. The only thing that flips behavior is which side of the body the wires connect to (uncrossed vs crossed). A frequent exam mistake is to say "2a is negative" — wrong; it's positive but uncrossed.

      

    

  


  

    

      
      
      
    


    

      
9. Vehicles 3a, 3b — Love & Explorer


      
Two sensors, two motors, both connections inhibitory (−). With a minus sign, more stimulus → slower motor.


      

        
VehicleWiringBehaviorOnlooker label

        
          

            3a
            Uncrossed, both −
            Approaches a source, slows as it gets closer, eventually stops next to it (motors near zero). Stays there.
            Permanent love — settles on one source
          

          

            3b
            Crossed, both −
            Approaches but doesn't fully commit — slows near the source, drifts off, looks for another. Restless.
            Explorer love — likes the nearby source but keeps an eye open for others
          

        
      


      
Subtlety: 3a never fully stops — no two sensor readings are identical, so there's always residual motion. The prof: "It's not stopping actually; it's slowing down on a super-micro scale, doing a kind of permanent attachment."


      

        
Exam · RoboCup combo Exam

        
"You hire a Braitenberg robot to compete in RoboCup. It must find the ball, approach it, and kick it. What kind of Braitenberg mechanism do you implement?"

        
Answer — combine two phases:

        
    
          
  1. Approach phase: Vehicle 3a (love, − connection) — the robot homes in on the ball and slows down as it gets close. Or Vehicle 2b (aggression, + crossed) for fast approach.
    2. 
          
  2. Kick phase: flip the sign to + once close → the robot accelerates through the ball, kicking it.
    3. 
        

        
Key insight: complex multi-stage behavior emerges from sequencing simple Braitenberg primitives, not from a complex controller.

10. Implementation — From Theory to Arduino


      
The whole Vehicle 1 behavior fits in ~20 lines of Arduino code:

      
    
        
  • Read a photoresistor (or thermistor, or humistor — whatever sensor you have).
    • 
        
  • Map the reading to a PWM duty cycle on the motor pin.
    • 
        
  • Done. RPM (revolutions per minute) scales with sensor input.
    • 
      


      

        
Project floor Prof

        
"If you do this — add two motors and show in the actual demo that it's working — you will pass the group project. There is no excuse not to do it."

        
The minimum viable autonomous robot is two sensors + two motors + Braitenberg wiring. Anything beyond that is bonus.

      


      
Articulating answers

      
When explaining a Braitenberg behavior on the exam, use numbers, not images. Bad answer: "the agent moves toward the other one and the other one moves away…". Good answer: "sensor reads 99 → motor turns at 99 RPM → at distance d the right sensor reads X, left reads Y, ratio drives turning rate Z". Prof "Be careful to articulate your answers in the numerical way."


      
Tools for the practical

Key Takeaways

    
    
      
  1. Sensor = number-producing device. Camera output isn't "what you see" — it's three matrices of pixel values.
    2. 
      
  2. Two-axis sensor classification: proprio vs extero, active vs passive. Memorize 2 examples per cell — "camera" alone won't pass the Box-A question.
    3. 
      
  3. Active sensors emit and listen (lidar, sonar, IR); passive only listen (camera, microphone, photodiode, GPS).
    4. 
      
  4. Laser >> sonar in precision — light is a pinpoint, sound expands as a cone.
    5. 
      
  5. Braitenberg vehicles satisfy all 3 autonomy conditions — no operator, runs until battery dies, works in unknown environments. Simplest autonomous system in the course.
    6. 
      
  6. + excitatory · − inhibitory. Default if no sign drawn = +.
    7. 
      
  7. Vehicle 1 (1 sensor, 1 motor, +): heat-seeking / restless / "alive". Cannot stop.
    8. 
      
  8. Vehicle 2a (2+2, uncrossed, +): fear — flees. Vehicle 2b (2+2, crossed, +): aggression — attacks (T-Rex).
    9. 
      
  9. Vehicle 3a (uncrossed, −): permanent love — settles. Vehicle 3b (crossed, −): explorer love — restless drifter.
    10. 
      
  10. Combine vehicles to get multi-stage behavior — RoboCup approach + kick = Vehicle 3a (or 2b) followed by sign-flip to +.
    11. 
      
  11. Articulate exam answers numerically. "Sensor reads X → motor turns Y RPM" beats hand-wavy diagrams.
    12. 
      
  12. Project floor: 2 sensors + 2 motors + Braitenberg wiring on the actual hardware = pass. No simulation-only projects.
