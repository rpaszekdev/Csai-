1. Turing's Child-Machine Argument
Mandatory quote Exam

"Instead of trying to produce a programme to simulate the adult mind, why not rather try to produce one which simulates the child's? If this were then subjected to an appropriate course of education, one would obtain the adult brain. […] Our hope is that there is so little mechanism in the child brain that something like it can be easily programmed."

— Alan Turing, 1950

This single paragraph is the founding charter for Cognitive Developmental Robotics (CoDeRs). Turing's bet: rather than hand-engineer the adult mind, build a learner that grows. The course he envisioned is precisely what developmental robotics tries to give a robot — online interaction, bodily feedback, and graduated experience.

Why this matters for week 09: the entire lecture is structured around cognition that develops, not cognition that is loaded. Every architectural decision in the rest of the slides flows from Turing's frame.

2. What Is Cognitive Robotics?
Course definition Exam

"Cognitive robotics is the field that combines insights and methods from AI, as well as cognitive and biological sciences, to robotics."

— Cognitive Robotics ch. 1 (course textbook). This is the working definition for the exam.

The discipline sits at a three-way intersection — it borrows representations and learning algorithms from AI, theories of mind and behavior from cognitive science, and grounding in real bodies and brains from biology / neuroscience.

How it differs from earlier weeks

  • vs Behavioral robotics (Brooks, week 03): behavior-based subsumption can produce intelligent action, but no internal world model. Cognitive robotics adds memory, anticipation, planning.
  • vs Classical AI: classical AI treats cognition as symbol manipulation in a body-less brain. Cognitive robotics insists cognition is situated, embodied, and developed.
  • vs Reinforcement learning (week 05): RL optimises a single policy. Cognitive robotics composes multiple capabilities — perception, attention, memory, planning — into a single agent.
3. The Six Attributes of Cognition
Six attributes Exam

A system is cognitive when it exhibits these six capabilities together:

  1. Perception — making sense of sensory input
  2. Attention — selecting what matters from the stream
  3. Action selection — picking the next thing to do
  4. Memory — storing and recalling past experience
  5. Learning — updating from interaction
  6. Reasoning — drawing inferences and planning

Two extras commonly added in the literature: Metacognition (thinking about one's own thinking — knowing what you don't know) and Prospection (evaluating possible futures before acting).

Two views of cognition Exam

ViewCognition is…Examples
Cognitivist Symbol manipulation. Cognition = computation over internal symbolic representations. GOFAI, classical planners, expert systems
Emergent A self-organising process arising from brain × body × environment coupling. Connectionism, dynamical systems, embodied / enactive cognition

The course leans emergent, but a real cognitive robot usually borrows from both — symbolic planning on top of embodied perception.

4. Why Cognition Is Useful for Robots

A cognitive robot achieves its goals by:

  • Perceiving the environment
  • Paying attention to the events that matter
  • Anticipating the need for some action
  • Planning what to do
  • Anticipating the outcome as it executes the action
  • Learning from the resultant interaction
  • Adapting to change
Two reasons cognition matters Exam
  1. Autonomy in everyday environments — anticipating outcomes before acting and adapting to changes / unforeseen situations.
  2. Effective interaction with humans — inferring goals and intentions of the person being interacted with, so the robot can behave helpfully. Humans like to engage with cognitive agents — cognition fosters HRI (callback to week 08).

Note the loop: cognition lets the robot survive the messy real world (point 1), and the messy real world includes humans (point 2). The two reasons aren't separate — interacting with people is the hardest "everyday environment".

5. Cognitive Architectures

A cognitive architecture is a unified blueprint that ties together memory, perception, action selection, learning, and reasoning into one running system. The key claim: rather than building each capability separately, a cognitive architecture provides the scaffolding in which capabilities interoperate.

Three families Exam

FamilyApproachExamples
Symbolic / cognitivist Cognition = symbol manipulation; explicit declarative + procedural memory; production rules. ACT-R, Soar, SS-RICS
Emergent / connectionist Cognition emerges from sub-symbolic dynamics — neural networks, self-organisation, dynamical systems. SOIMA, DAC (Distributed Adaptive Control)
Hybrid Symbolic planning on top of sub-symbolic perception & motor control. Most modern systems are hybrid. iCub cognitive architecture, CRAM, Cog
Open question Prof

"Is it possible to integrate all these abilities into a single architecture?" Slide 22 poses this directly. There is no agreed answer — every architecture so far makes trade-offs. Symbolic ones are interpretable but brittle. Emergent ones are robust but opaque. Hybrid ones inherit both problems and add an integration cost.

6. SOIMA — A Concrete Architecture

SOIMA = Self-Organised Internal Models Architecture. The reference architecture used in this lecture as a worked example of an emergent cognitive architecture (Hermosillo-Valadez 2016).

Four ingredients

  • The units of the architecture are Self-Organising Maps (SOMs) — direct callback to week 06 unsupervised learning.
  • It implements forward and inverse models in the same architecture (forward = predict next sensor state given an action; inverse = predict the action that produces a desired sensor state).
  • It can reconstruct missing modalities — given vision, fill in the expected proprioception, and vice versa. This builds on week 07 multimodal learning.
  • It performs sensorimotor mapping — connecting raw sensor patterns to motor commands without hand-coded labels.
Project tip Prof

Slide 29 explicitly says: "You can also employ these concepts for your project." If your group project needs sensorimotor learning without supervision, a SOM-based architecture is a very defensible choice.

The SOIMA design is exam-relevant because it packages four earlier-week concepts (SOMs, forward/inverse models, multimodal fusion, sensorimotor coupling) into one architecture. Expect a question that asks you to identify which capabilities a given architecture provides.

7. Developmental Robotics

Developmental robotics is the operational arm of Turing's child-machine idea: build robots that grow into their abilities by interacting with the environment, rather than receiving a fully-formed adult policy.

The premise (slide 36): the learning targets we want from autonomous robots are quite similar to the tasks human infants solve in their first years — recognising agents, controlling limbs, producing speech, predicting outcomes, building social attachments. Why not borrow the trajectory?

Four characteristics of human development Exam

"Human development is characterised by online, crossmodal, continuous, open-ended learning."

  • Online — learning happens while the child interacts with the environment, not in an offline batch mode.
  • Crossmodal — different modalities and cognitive domains are acquired in parallel and interact with each other (vision shapes audition, audition shapes language, etc.).
  • Continuous — there is no clean start/stop boundary between phases of learning.
  • Open-ended — learning is a lifelong process; new skills keep being added on top of old ones.

Most current ML systems violate all four characteristics. They train offline, on one modality, in a single phase, with a fixed goal. That gap is what developmental robotics is trying to close.

8. Why Development, Not Just Learning?

Slide 42 asks the obvious challenge: "Can't we just learn? Why do we need development?" Three answers:

Answer 1 — Body changes

An infant's body grows. Limbs lengthen, weight shifts, balance has to be re-acquired. A robot's body can also change — wear, damage, modular reconfiguration. Pure learning assumes a fixed body; development assumes the body is a moving target.

Answer 2 — Curriculum

Children don't learn calculus before they learn to count. Skills are layered: motor babbling → reaching → grasping → tool use. Each stage scaffolds the next. Trying to learn the top of the stack without the bottom is intractable. Developmental robotics calls this curriculum learning — and modern ML has rediscovered the idea (curriculum learning, growing networks).

Answer 3 — Open-endedness

A "learning" agent has a fixed objective. A "developing" agent changes its own objectives as it grows — it discovers new things to be curious about, new goals to pursue. Intrinsic motivation (curiosity, novelty-seeking) is the operational mechanism.

Cross-species comparison Prof

The lecture explicitly compares what we can learn from children (slide 38), chimps (slide 39), and what we want from cognitive robots (slide 40). The point isn't to copy biology 1:1 — it's to extract principles (synthetic methodology, week 04 callback) that scale to artificial agents.

9. Cognitive Robotics Beyond Research Labs

Why do we study cognitive robotics? Two parts on the slide, with a half-joking third:

  • Scientific — building cognitive architectures is a way to test theories of cognition. If your model of attention can't make a robot pick the right object on a table, the model is incomplete.
  • Engineering — robots that need to operate in households, hospitals, and unstructured workplaces need cognitive abilities; pre-programmed scripts don't generalise.
  • Slide 31's bonus: "+ Cognitive robotics is fun!" — the prof's own line, half a joke and half not. Working at the intersection of three rich fields is rare and energising.

Where it shows up outside the lab

  • Assistive robots — caregiving, rehabilitation. Requires reading intent, planning safe actions, learning user preferences.
  • Educational robots — peer-tutors and language partners. Requires memory of past interactions, social cues, theory of mind.
  • Autonomous vehicles — anticipating other agents' intentions is a cognitive task masquerading as perception.
  • Industrial collaboration (cobots) — picking up where the human left off requires inference, not just pre-programming.
10. Building a Cognitive Robot — Synthetic Methodology Revisited

Slide 18 is a deliberate callback to week 04 (Embodiment) §7 — Synthetic Methodology. The recipe stays the same here, applied to cognition:

  1. Pick a cognitive ability you want to model (attention, memory, action selection).
  2. Identify principles from cognitive / biological science — not literal mechanism.
  3. Build the simplest robot that exhibits the principle.
  4. Test on real interaction, then iterate.

The mistake to avoid: do not try to copy the human brain at the wiring level. The biology is there to inspire; the engineering is yours. SOIMA does this — it doesn't copy the cortex, it borrows the principle of self-organising sensorimotor maps.

How weeks 1-9 connect Exam

The exam loves "putting it together" questions. Cognitive robotics is the integration point:

  • Week 02 sensors / actuators → physical perception & action layer
  • Week 03 controllers → action-perception cycle, hybrid architectures

  • Week 07 multimodal → fusing vision + touch + audition into a coherent percept
  • Week 08 social HRI → cognition that includes other minds

Key Takeaways
  1. Turing's child-machine quote is the founding charter for cognitive (developmental) robotics. Build the learner, not the adult policy.
  2. Course definition: "Cognitive robotics is the field that combines insights and methods from AI, as well as cognitive and biological sciences, to robotics."
  3. Six attributes of cognition: perception, attention, action selection, memory, learning, reasoning. Plus metacognition + prospection. Likely a fill-in-the-blank.
  4. Two views: cognitivist (symbol manipulation) vs emergent (brain × body × environment). Course leans emergent; modern systems are usually hybrid.
  5. Three architecture families: symbolic (ACT-R, Soar), emergent (SOIMA, DAC), hybrid (iCub). Know one example from each.
  6. SOIMA packages four earlier-week concepts (SOMs, forward/inverse models, multimodal fusion, sensorimotor mapping) into one architecture.
  7. Two reasons cognition matters: autonomy in everyday environments + effective interaction with humans.
  8. Four characteristics of human development: online, crossmodal, continuous, open-ended. Modern ML violates all four — the gap is what dev robotics tries to close.
  9. Why development, not just learning: bodies change, skills scaffold (curriculum), goals open-end via intrinsic motivation.
