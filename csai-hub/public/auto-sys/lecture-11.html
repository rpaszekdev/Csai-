3. Sample Q1 — Braitenberg Vehicle 1
Sample exam Q1 Exam

Describe the behavior of Braitenberg Vehicle 1 in an open-ended environment where temperature varies. Recall that this vehicle has one temperature sensor directly connected to the actuator.

Assumptions: temperature cannot be negative; two consecutive readings will not be the same.

Worked answer

Vehicle 1 has a single sensor directly wired to a single motor. The actuator's velocity is therefore directly proportional to the sensor reading.

  • In cold areas the temperature reading is low → the motor receives a small signal → the vehicle slows down.
  • In hot areas the reading is high → the motor receives a larger signal → the vehicle speeds up.
  • Because temperature is non-negative and consecutive readings are never identical, the vehicle never stops — there is always some forward motion.

Key concept being tested: the direct sensor-to-actuator coupling defining Vehicle 1. No controller, no memory, no thresholding — pure reactive behavior emerging from a hard-wired connection.

4. Sample Q2 & Q3 — True/False (Sensors, Embodiment)
Sample exam Q2 Exam

Mark each sentence True or False:

(a) A proprioceptive sensor measures the quantities related to the internal state of the agent.

(b) Cameras located on a robot's forehead are types of proprioceptive sensors.

Worked answer — Q2

  • (a) True. By definition, proprioceptive sensors measure the robot's own internal state — joint angles, motor torque, battery level, encoder counts.
  • (b) False. A camera measures quantities external to the robot (the scene). It is therefore exteroceptive, not proprioceptive — even when mounted on the forehead.

Trap: the location of the sensor on the robot's body is a distractor. Categorisation depends on what the sensor measures, not where it lives.

Sample exam Q3 Exam

You see two robots: Affetto (an android child face, left) and a soft walking robot (right). Mark each as True or False:

(a) Both robots are examples of embodied agents.

(b) The soft walking robot has the morphology of a human hand.

Worked answer — Q3

  • (a) True. Both have a physical body situated in the environment — they are physically embodied agents. Affetto is rigid; the walker is soft. Both qualify.
  • (b) False. The soft walker has the morphology of a leg / walker, not a hand. Morphology = shape and structure, and the question hinges on knowing what each form looks like.

Concept tested: "embodied agent" applies broadly (rigid and soft); morphology describes the bodily form, not the material.

5. Sample Q4 & Q5 — Morphological Computation
Sample exam Q4 Exam

Why are passive walkers considered a good example of morphological computation? Write a few sentences.

Worked answer — Q4

A passive walker is a legged robot that walks down a slight incline using only gravity and the geometry of its legs — it has no controller, no motors, no sensors. The walking gait emerges from the body alone: the mass distribution, joint placement, and pendulum dynamics of the legs are the computation.

This is the textbook illustration of morphological computation: the body is performing work that would otherwise need an algorithm. It demonstrates that embodiment can substitute for explicit control — a core thesis of the embodiment lectures (Pfeifer & Bongard).

Sample exam Q5 Exam

Why can industrial robots that perform precise pick-and-place tasks not be considered agents performing morphological computation? Write a few sentences.

Worked answer — Q5

Industrial pick-and-place arms are rigid, fully actuated, and operate under centralised computer control. Every joint angle, gripper force and trajectory is explicitly computed by the controller; the body offloads nothing. The morphology is shaped for repeatability and stiffness, not to exploit dynamics.

In morphological computation, the body's physics replace control (passive compliance, pendulum gait, soft-fingered grasping). In an industrial arm, the body is just a passive executor of commands — there is no offloading. Therefore the agent does not perform morphological computation.

Trap Exam

Don't confuse "the robot has a body" with "the robot uses morphological computation". Every physical robot has a body. The question is whether the body's dynamics do work that would otherwise need a controller.

6. Sample Q6 & Q7 — Sensors, Hybrid Paradigm
Sample exam Q6 Exam

In the lectures we used a figure to classify sensor types. Write the sensor type of Box A (highlighted in red) and define passive sensors.

Worked answer — Q6

Box A in the standard course taxonomy refers to passive sensors (the alternative branch is active sensors). The categorisation distinguishes how the sensor obtains its measurement.

Passive sensor (definition): a sensor that measures energy already present in the environment without emitting any signal of its own. It "listens" rather than "probes".

  • Examples: cameras (capture ambient light), microphones (capture ambient sound), thermometers, passive IR motion sensors.
  • Contrast with active sensors (lidar, sonar, structured-light depth) which emit a signal and measure its reflection.
Sample exam Q7 Exam

The hybrid deliberate/reactive paradigm can be visualised in the figure above. Write the functions represented by boxes A and B.

Worked answer — Q7

The three primitives of the classical robot-architecture taxonomy are Sense, Plan, and Act. The hybrid paradigm uses all three but reorganises the flow: a deliberative layer plans long-horizon, a reactive layer maps Sense → Act in tight loops, and the planner only intervenes when the reactive layer cannot handle the situation.

  • Box A = Act — the actuator-side primitive that closes the perception-action loop.
  • Box B = Plan — the deliberative primitive that sits above pure reaction.

Concept tested: the three architectures from the controllers lecture — reactive (Sense → Act), deliberative (Sense → Plan → Act), and hybrid (both, with arbitration).

7. Sample Q8 & Q9 — LfD & Reinforcement Learning
Sample exam Q8 Exam

An operator controls a robot via a haptic device. What is the type of learning from demonstration (LfD) addressed in this experiment?

Worked answer — Q8

The answer is Teleoperation. In LfD, the three canonical demonstration channels are:

  • Kinesthetic teaching — the human physically moves the robot's joints (zero-force / gravity-comp mode).
  • Teleoperation — the human controls the robot remotely through a master device (joystick, haptic, motion-tracker). The robot mirrors the operator's commands while sensors record the trajectory.
  • Observation / shadowing — the human performs the task with their own body and the robot watches via cameras.

A haptic device is the archetypal teleoperation interface: it transmits motion commands to the robot and force feedback back to the operator. Hence the answer is teleoperation.

Sample exam Q9 Exam

Fill in the blanks:

(A) In RL, the 1) ___________ is the term used to represent the numerical feedback that indicates the success or desirability of an action and/or state.

(B) During the RL experiment the agent updates its 2) ___________ based on observed rewards and the estimated future rewards.

Worked answer — Q9

  • 1) reward — the scalar feedback signal from the environment.
  • 2) Q-value (also accepted: Q-function, Q-table) — the action-value estimate the agent improves at each step using the Bellman update.

Concept tested: the vocabulary of Q-learning. Reward is the signal; Q-value is the estimate the agent maintains and improves. Don't confuse the two.

8. Sample Q10–Q12 — Controllers & Hardware
Sample exam Q10 Exam

What is the role of the controller in autonomous systems? Frame your answer in the context of the action-perception cycle.

Worked answer — Q10

The controller is the brain of the robot. It regulates the state of the actuators based on the current and previous states of the sensors.

In the action-perception cycle:

  1. Perception — sensors sample the environment and the robot's own internal state.
  2. Decision — the controller maps that sensor history (current + past) to a motor command.
  3. Action — actuators execute the command, changing the world (or the robot's pose) — which the next perception step picks up.

The controller is what closes the loop: without it, sensors and actuators would not be coordinated. Whether reactive, deliberative, or learned, the controller's defining role is this sense → decide → act mapping over time.

10. Common Mistakes & Traps

Patterns the sample questions deliberately exploit. Internalise these before walking into the exam.

  1. Sensor location ≠ sensor type. A camera on the forehead is still exteroceptive. The category depends on what is measured, never on placement.
  2. "Embodied" includes soft robots. Don't restrict the term to rigid humanoid arms — soft walkers and grippers are equally embodied.
  3. Morphology = shape, not material. A soft robot can have the morphology of a leg, an arm, or a tentacle. Don't conflate softness with hand-likeness.
  4. Morphological computation requires offloading. Having a body is not enough — the body's physics must replace control. Industrial arms have bodies but do not perform morphological computation.
  5. Reward ≠ Q-value. Reward is the signal from the environment. Q-value is the agent's estimate. Mixing these in fill-in-the-blanks is a free point lost.
  6. Hybrid ≠ reactive ≠ deliberative. Know the three paradigms and what flow each implies (Sense→Act vs Sense→Plan→Act vs both with arbitration).
  7. Teleoperation vs kinesthetic vs observation. Three flavours of LfD. A haptic master is teleoperation; physically grabbing the robot's arm is kinesthetic; watching a human is observation.
  8. Compound T/F is graded per part. Q2 has parts (a) and (b); each is independent. Don't assume both halves share an answer.
  9. "Open-ended" wants concepts, not novels. A few precise sentences with the right vocabulary scores higher than three paragraphs of vague prose.
  10. Vehicle 1 has no controller. The Braitenberg sequence: V1 = single sensor → single motor. V2/V3 add crossings, inhibition, and produce more complex behavior. Know which vehicle does what.
11. Final Tips — Strategy on Exam Day
  • Read the assumptions. Q1 spelled out two assumptions ("temperature non-negative", "consecutive readings differ"). Use them in your answer — they're load-bearing.
  • Name the concept first, then explain. "This is morphological computation: the body…" beats a long preamble.
  • Use course vocabulary verbatim. Reward · Q-value · proprioceptive · exteroceptive · passive · active · embodied · morphological computation · teleoperation · action-perception cycle. If you write "the body computes things by itself" instead of "morphological computation", you're throwing points away.
  • Answer T/F decisively. Don't hedge. If the question grades both parts, mark each unambiguously.
  • For "describe behavior" questions, narrate the loop. "Sensor reads X → controller maps to Y → actuator does Z → environment changes → next reading."
  • Hardware questions: pick what you actually built. Q11/Q12 explicitly accept any two components. Choose ones you can defend.
  • Time budget. ~40 questions in a fixed window — roughly 1.5–2 minutes each. Don't over-invest in a single open-ended question.
  • Register for the exam. Slide 10 says it twice. Unregistered = no exam = no resit pathway.
Final Exam-Prep Tips
  1. Twelve sample questions = the exam's DNA. Master the worked answer to each — they are the closest thing to a leaked exam you will get.
  2. Weeks 02–05 + practicals dominate. Sensors, Braitenberg, controllers, embodiment, RL vocabulary, and your Arduino kit are the safest revision targets.
  3. Combi-exam means three answer styles. Practice writing in all three formats: open-ended, fill-in-the-blank, true/false (graded per sub-part).
  4. Memorise the term pairs. Reward / Q-value · proprioceptive / exteroceptive · passive / active sensor · reactive / deliberative / hybrid · kinesthetic / teleoperation / observation.
  5. Definitions before nuance. "Autonomous = without intervention + prolonged + partially unknown env" is a one-line answer that unlocks dozens of points across questions.
  6. Use the assumptions printed in the question. They are not decoration — they constrain the correct answer (see Q1).
  7. Action-perception cycle is the framing for any controller question. Sense → Decide → Act → world changes → next sense.
  8. Morphological computation needs offloading. Body alone ≠ morphological computation. Body replacing control = morphological computation.
  9. Pass requires both halves. Exam > 5.5 AND project deliverables submitted. Prepping the exam doesn't replace handing in the report.
  10. Register, double-check date, sleep. Slide 10's logistics line is also exam advice — show up calm, registered, on time.
