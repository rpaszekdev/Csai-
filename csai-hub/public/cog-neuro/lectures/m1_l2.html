1. The Two Major Categories of Methods Exam



Cognitive neuroscience methods fall into two broad categories:






Category
Logic
Examples






Brain Perturbation
"If we break/disrupt X, what happens to behaviour?"
Brain lesions, pharmacological manipulation, TMS/tDCS, optogenetics, intracranial stimulation




Neuromonitoring (Neuroimaging)
"What does the brain do during task Y?"
EEG, ERPs, MEG, fMRI, PET, CT, MRI, DTI, single-unit recording, fNIRS






Prof emphasis Prof
The professor structured the entire lecture around this distinction. Perturbation methods manipulate the brain and observe the behavioural result; neuromonitoring methods manipulate the cognitive process (e.g., having people read sentences) and observe what the brain does.


Key Insight: No Method Is Sufficient Alone Exam


Every method has limitations. The field addresses this through:


    

  • Convergence: If EEG, fMRI, and single-cell recordings all support the same theory, the argument is much stronger Exam
    • 

  • Complementarity: Different methods provide different types of information (e.g., EEG gives millisecond timing, fMRI gives millimetre localisation) Exam
    • 



Prof emphasis Prof
"If we can show something with single cell recordings, EEG and fMRI, which are all sort of giving us a different insight... we strengthen our argument."




















2. Brain Perturbation Methods



2.1 Brain Lesions: Stroke, Trauma, or Disease


    

  • Study how a healthy brain starts to malfunction cognitively after damage
    • 

  • Naturally occurring lesions (stroke, trauma, disease) — uncontrolled location and extent
    • 

  • Directed lesions (in experimental animals) — controlled and selective
    • 



Major Limitations Exam:
- Damage never occurs cleanly — "if you've got ten people with damage because they had a stroke," the damage varies across patients
- Need to combine information across subjects to find the overlapping damaged region (lesion overlap analysis)
- Do not identify a network — only that a region is essential for a task
- No temporal resolution
- Effects of recovery are unknown or complex
- Relatively few available subjects; often heterogeneous groups


Advantage: Can strongly implicate a region as being essential (causal) for a task — unlike neuroimaging which shows correlation only


2.2 Pharmacological Perturbations Exam


Drugs that affect neurotransmitter systems (e.g., the dopamine system):






Type
Definition
Example






Agonists
Drugs that activate receptors like neurotransmitters
Nicotine (mimics acetylcholine)




Antagonists
Drugs that block receptors
Various dopamine blockers






    

  • Can manipulate how information flow would normally work — e.g., massively excite all neurones or block responsiveness to specific neurotransmitters
    • 



2.3 Intracranial Brain Stimulation


    

  • Direct electrical stimulation of brain tissue
    • 

  • Mostly limited to animals and rare clinical circumstances (e.g., epilepsy surgery)
    • 

  • Can provide rather specific neural perturbation
    • 



2.4 Extracranial Brain Stimulation Exam


Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)


    

  • Through a set of coils, a strong but transient and rapidly changing magnetic field is generated over the scalp Exam
    • 

  • Results in an extraneous flow of current that interacts with local neural processing
    • 

  • Creates "virtual lesions" — temporary, reversible disruption of brain function Exam
    • 

  • TMS can impair OR improve performance on tasks involving the stimulated area
    • 



Advantages:
- Advantages of lesion studies, but transient and noninvasive (non-surgical)
- With single-pulse TMS, can get some temporal resolution for assessing when a brain area is critical


Drawbacks Exam:
- Affects a relatively large area of superficial brain regions (up to 1.5 cm into the brain)
- Limited to superficial cortex only
- Can cause uncomfortable/painful muscle twitching (especially frontal and temporal lobes)
- Some safety risk (e.g., could trigger seizure in predisposed individuals)
- Sham TMS = acting as if applying TMS but not doing it (placebo control) Exam


Prof emphasis Prof
"TMS might not be invasive, but we're still injecting a magnetic field into your otherwise well working brain... I would be very careful to apply it to my own brain."


Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS)


    

  • A constant, low-amplitude electrical current applied directly to the scalp
    • 

  • Simple device: two electrodes, a battery, current adjustment
    • 

  • Anodal (positive) stimulation → thought to increase cortical excitability Exam
    • 

  • Cathodal stimulation → thought to decrease excitability Exam
    • 

  • Compared against sham (no current) condition
    • 

  • Effects can last for minutes after stimulation ends
    • 

  • Used for both research and clinical applications (depression, chronic pain)
    • 



Disadvantages: Spatial coarseness; limited mechanistic understanding


Key Physics Principle Exam


Prof emphasis Prof
"If we have a current, an electrical current, we always have a perpendicular magnetic field." Any brain imaging method based on electricity has a magnetic counterpart — this is why EEG has MEG, and TMS uses magnetic fields to induce electrical currents.


2.5 Optogenetics Exam


    

  • Combination of genetics and laser light to (de)activate neural circuits
    • 

  • Genetically manipulate neurones in mice so they can be turned on/off using light
    • 

  • Highly specific: can target particular neural circuit types
    • 

  • Very invasive — limited to animal models
    • 





















3. Neuromonitoring Methods (Neuroimaging)



Prof emphasis Prof
Neuromonitoring "flips around the circuit" compared to perturbation: Rather than manipulating the brain and looking at behaviour, we manipulate the cognitive process and look at brain activity.


3.1 Direct Electrophysiological Recording from Neurons Exam


The only method that can directly measure action potentials.






Type
Method
What it measures






Extracellular recording
Electrodes in the extracellular space
Action potentials of one or more neurons




Intracellular recording
Electrode penetrates an individual neuron
Action potentials AND post-synaptic potentials






    

  • Typically done in monkeys (or other experimental animals)
    • 

  • Metal electrodes are inserted directly into brain structures
    • 

  • Researchers also increasingly study local field potentials (non-spike dendritic fluctuations)
    • 

  • Very invasive — almost completely limited to animals
    • 

  • High spatial AND temporal resolution at the single-neuron level
    • 

  • Limitation: typically records from only one brain area, so does not identify network interactions
    • 



3.2 Electroencephalography (EEG) Exam


What EEG measures — THE classic trap question Exam:


Prof emphasis Prof
Summed post-synaptic dendritic field potentials of pyramidal cells — NEVER action potentials!
Prof Emphasised on Slide 32 of both lectures.


    

  • Noninvasive recording of the brain's electrical signals from the scalp
    • 

  • Reflects volume-conducted dendritic field potentials
    • 

  • Measures voltage fluctuations at each electrode over time
    • 



Temporal resolution: Excellent — millisecond basis Exam
Spatial resolution: Poor/coarse — signals "smear" across the scalp Exam


    

  • Inexpensive, fast, and easy recording procedures
    • 



The Inverse Problem Exam


Prof emphasis Prof
This is a critical concept — referenced Hermann von Helmholtz.


    

  • Forward problem (solvable): If we know the generators, we can calculate the scalp distribution
    • 

  • Inverse problem (unsolvable): Given a scalp distribution, there are an infinite number of possible generator configurations that could have produced it Exam
    • 

  • "We can never with certainty say where a signal came from" based on EEG alone
    • 



Prof emphasis Prof
"If we observe a particular distribution of activity over the scalp, there can physically be an infinite number of different generators underlying that particular distribution."


Topographical Maps of ERP Activity


    

  • For every electrode, we can plot whether voltages are more negative or more positive
    • 

  • Creates distributions of activity over the scalp (voltage maps)
    • 

  • Useful for visualisation but does not solve the inverse problem
    • 



3.3 Event-Related Potentials (ERPs) Exam


    

  • Extracted from EEG data via time-locked averaging Exam
    • 

  • Average the EEG signal across many trials locked to a specific stimulus/event
    • 

  • Reveals systematic voltage deflections (peaks) that reflect computational processes
    • 

  • Has been widely used to study human brain activity
    • 



Prof emphasis Prof
The peaks in ERP waveforms are "systematic indications of computational processes" — they show when two stimuli diverge in processing.


3.4 Magnetoencephalography (MEG) Exam


Electrophysiological basis:
- Electrical currents create circular (perpendicular) magnetic fields
- Fields "come out of the head" on one side and "enter the head" at the other
- Field strengths measured over time using a magnetometer
- Fields can be positive (coming out) or negative (going in)


Key claim vs. reality Exam:


Prof emphasis Prof
"Sometimes people mistakenly say that MEG has way better spatial resolution than EEG, but it's not... based on not understanding the physics correctly."


    

  • Magnetic fields are not distorted by neural tissue and scalp (unlike EEG's electrical signals which smear)
    • 

  • HOWEVER: The inverse problem equally applies to MEG Exam
    • 

  • Even without smearing, an observed MEG distribution could still have an infinite number of generator locations
    • 



MEG terminology:
- ERFs (Event-Related Fields) instead of ERPs (Event-Related Potentials)
- Same logic applies — time-locked averaging of magnetic field responses


Practical: MEG systems are very expensive compared to EEG ("EEG system, peanuts compared to MEG")


3.5 Structural Imaging


These image brain structure without activity — no functional information.






Method
Basis






MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)
Magnetic fields




CT (Computerised Tomography)
X-ray imaging






    

  • Important for identifying lesion locations and brain anatomy
    • 

  • The foundation upon which functional methods (fMRI) are built
    • 



3.6 Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI) Exam


The dominant technology for imaging normal and abnormal cognitive function today.


The BOLD Signal — How fMRI Actually Works Exam


Know the full chain:


    

  1. Active brain areas use more oxygen than inactive areas
    2. 

  2. Oxygen is replenished through blood flow (within seconds)
    3. 

  3. MR signals differ for:
    4. 

  4. Oxyhemoglobin (oxygen-rich hemoglobin)
    5. 

  5. Deoxyhemoglobin (oxygen-depleted hemoglobin)
    6. 

  6. The Blood-Oxygen-Level-Dependent (BOLD) signal = MR signal changes due to reduction in deoxyhemoglobin (oxygen influx in active areas) Exam
    7. 



Temporal Properties Exam


    

  • fMRI response begins at about 1–2 seconds, peaks at about 5–6 seconds, returns to baseline at about 12–15 seconds
    • 

  • Whole-brain samples can be acquired every 1–2 seconds
    • 



Prof emphasis Prof
"fMRI happens like six to ten seconds after the fact... that's like 10 seconds in cognitive time. It's like ages."


Experimental Designs Exam






Design
Description






Block design
Subject performs task continuously for ~20–30 seconds; compare blocks (inherited from PET)




Event-related design
Capture fMRI response to individual stimuli/events; allows selective averaging of different event types




Hybrid/Mixed design
Combines block and event-related elements; disentangled by appropriate analysis






    

  • Event-related fMRI was a highly significant advance — enabled cognitive paradigms not possible with block designs
    • 

  • Allows linking trial-by-trial behavioural measures (e.g., reaction time) to fMRI response amplitudes
    • 



Spatial vs. Temporal Resolution Exam


    

  • Spatial resolution: Excellent — millimetres (compared to centimetres for PET)
    • 

  • Temporal resolution: Poor relative to electrophysiology — seconds (compared to milliseconds for EEG)
    • 

  • Better than PET on both dimensions
    • 



Prof emphasis Prof
fMRI gives "beautiful maps" of where stuff happens, but "it's a very indirect signal... happening six to ten seconds after a brain region has been active."


Advanced fMRI Techniques Exam






Technique
Description






Multivoxel Pattern Analysis (MVPA)
Uses pattern classification to find voxels that consistently respond to a particular stimulus type (e.g., train a classifier to distinguish happy vs. angry face responses) Exam




Repetition Suppression
Exploits the fact that brain areas respond less when stimuli are repeated or related (fMRI adaptation)




Functional Connectivity
Examines coactivation patterns between structurally connected brain regions (e.g., resting-state connectivity)




Psychophysiological Interaction (PPI)
Uses time course of one seed region to predict changes in another region, as a function of cognitive task




Structural Equation Modeling
Infers causality from fMRI by incorporating anatomical connectivity and comparing directional influence models




Dynamic Causal Modeling
Models functional connections between regions; evaluates how connectivity changes due to experimental manipulations






Prof emphasis Prof
On MVPA: "We train a classifier... that tries to predict whether the activity pattern in the brain is in response to a happy or angry face. And the idea is that if there's anything systematic in the voxels..."


3.7 Positron Emission Tomography (PET)


    

  • Slightly older functional imaging method Exam
    • 

  • Requires radioactive isotopes as tracers (e.g., 15-O-labeled water)
    • 

  • Block design only: ~1 minute blocks with 10-minute breaks between (for tracer decay)
    • 

  • Essentially no temporal resolution
    • 

  • Has been largely supplanted by fMRI in cognitive research
    • 

  • Spatial resolution: centimetres (worse than fMRI)
    • 



3.8 Optical Brain Imaging Methods


Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS) Exam


    

  • Uses light to detect differences in absorption in active brain areas
    • 

  • Measures the same blood-based (hemodynamic) signal as fMRI Exam
    • 

  • Sometimes called "the portable fMRI"
    • 



Prof emphasis Prof
Clarification on a common confusion: "You can actually measure on a millisecond basis, so it feels like it has way higher temporal resolution than fMRI. But it's still measuring the hemodynamic response... the underlying system you're measuring is not going to be faster for it." Exam


    

  • More measurements does not equal a faster underlying signal
    • 



Event-Related Optical Signals (EROS)


    

  • Another optical imaging method based on light transmission differences
    •

The fundamental rule Exam:
- High temporal resolution methods (EEG, MEG, single-unit) = based on electricity → poor spatial resolution because signals smear across the scalp
- High spatial resolution methods (fMRI, PET) = based on metabolic/blood signals → poor temporal resolution because blood flow is slow


Prof emphasis Prof
"Most high temporal resolution methods are based on electricity... electricity smears over the scalp." "With fMRI we can [localise] but fMRI is slow... six to ten seconds afterwards."




















5. Assembling Evidence: Associations and Dissociations Exam



Association


    

  • Impairment in Task A but not Task B after damage to Region 1 → indicates an association between Region 1 and the neural basis for Task A
    • 

  • But an association alone is not strong evidence (could be a general processing deficit)
    • 



Double Dissociation Exam


The gold standard for establishing brain-function relationships:


    

  • Damage to Region 1 impairs Task A but NOT Task B
    • 

  • Damage to Region 2 impairs Task B but NOT Task A
    • 



This establishes that the two regions are selectively involved in different tasks — rules out general processing explanations.


Prof emphasis Prof
Example from materials: Richardson et al., 2003


Multimethodological Approaches Exam


Combining methods to overcome individual limitations:
- High temporal (EEG) + high spatial (fMRI) resolution methods
- Comparing normal individuals with lesioned individuals
- Relating genetic variations and neuroimaging measures (neuroimaging genomics)




















6. Neuroimaging Genomics Exam



    

  • Polymorphisms = variations in a gene or DNA segment
    • 

  • These genetic variations can manifest in structural and functional neuroimaging data
    • 

  • Example: APOE gene and Alzheimer's risk
    • 

  • Individuals with APOE e4 allele showed greater hippocampal activation during memory tasks
    • 

  • Greater early activation predicted more memory decline over 2 years
    • 

  • Neuroimaging can detect subtle brain variations before clinical symptoms appear Exam
    • 

  • Example: Serotonin transporter gene variations
    • 

  • Certain alleles → greater serotonin in key brain areas → greater anxiety in stressful situations
    • 

  • fMRI shows greater amygdala activation to emotional images in these individuals
    •

8. Key Takeaways



    

  1. 

    Two categories: All methods are either perturbation (disrupt brain → observe behaviour) or neuromonitoring (manipulate cognition → observe brain) Exam
    

    2. 

  2. 

    The spatial-temporal trade-off is the central framework: electrical methods (EEG, MEG) give millisecond timing but poor localisation; metabolic methods (fMRI, PET) give millimetre localisation but are seconds slow Exam
    

    3. 

  3. 

    The inverse problem applies to BOTH EEG and MEG: a scalp distribution can have an infinite number of underlying generator configurations — you can never uniquely determine the source Exam
    

    4. 

  4. 

    fMRI BOLD signal chain: neural activity → oxygen consumption → blood flow → oxyhemoglobin/deoxyhemoglobin ratio change → MR signal change. Response peaks at 5-6 seconds Exam
    

    5. 

  5. 

    EEG measures summed post-synaptic dendritic field potentials of pyramidal cells — NEVER action potentials. Only single-unit recording measures action potentials directly Exam
    

    6. 

  6. 

    TMS creates virtual lesions — temporary, reversible disruption that is the key advantage over studying natural lesions. Limited to superficial cortex (~1.5 cm) Exam
    

    7. 

  7. 

    Convergence and complementarity are why cognitive neuroscience uses multiple methods: convergence strengthens arguments; complementarity provides different information types Exam
    

    8. 

  8. 

    Double dissociation is the gold standard for establishing that two brain regions are selectively involved in different cognitive tasks Exam
