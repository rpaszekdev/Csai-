Cognitive Neuroscience — Exam Study Notes
1. Motor Control Is Hierarchical Exam

The motor system is organized as a hierarchy with multiple levels of control:

Level Structure Function
Upper motor neurons Primary motor cortex, premotor areas Issue motor programs (commands) to lower levels
Basal ganglia Caudate, putamen, globus pallidus Movement initiation/stopping, motor skill learning, goal-directedness, movement sequences
Cerebellum Cerebellar cortex, deep nuclei Movement coordination and error correction
Spinal cord & brainstem Lower motor neurons, local circuits Reflexes and local coordination
  • Key demonstration (Bernstein): When people sign their name with a pen attached to different body parts (hand, wrist, elbow, shoulder, foot, mouth), the signature preserves distinctive features like shape and style — evidence that motor control operates at an abstract, hierarchical level above specific muscle groups Exam
  • Prof The hierarchy means that higher levels provide increasingly abstract movement plans, while lower levels handle specific muscle activation

The Reflex as a Fundamental Unit

  • Local motor neurons form direct circuits from brainstem to muscles
  • Sensory and motor neurons interact directly for reflexive movements (e.g., postural adjustments when lifting a coffee cup)
  • These circuits handle both simple movements (raising a cup) and contribute to complex movements (swimming) — though complex movements require higher-level coordination
2. Selecting Goals for Actions: Graded Sensory-Motor Conversion Exam

Motor planning is not binary — it is driven by accumulated sensory evidence:

  • Experiment: Monkeys fixate while observing motion stimuli (dots moving up/down or not moving)
  • Monkeys judge net direction of dot movement with eye movements to targets (T1 or T2)
  • Strength of evidence manipulated by changing fraction of dots moving coherently (motion coherence)

Key Findings:

  • Premotor neurons in the receptive field (RF) increased firing rate when dots moved into RF direction
  • Rate of increase is directly proportional to motion coherence (weight of evidence)
  • Microstimulation of frontal eye fields evoked eye movements biased by motion direction
  • Deviation of stimulation-induced eye movements depended on motion coherence and viewing duration
  • When monkeys made a decision, neuronal activity reflected eye movement speed

Implications Exam:

  • Premotor neurons specify movements in a graded manner as sensory evidence accumulates
  • The relation between firing rate and motion coherence reflects integration of motion information over time
  • This gradedness also applies to reward value (motivation) — not just sensory evidence
3. Sequential Movements and the Supplementary Motor Area (SMA) Exam

SMA vs. Premotor Cortex — Critical Dissociation

Feature SMA Premotor Cortex
Primary role Self-initiated movements Externally cued movements
Key function Generating movements without explicit sensory cues Responding to sensory triggers
Ablation effect Cannot perform well-learned movements Disrupts cued movements, not learned ones

Tanji's Monkey Experiment Exam Prof

  • Researcher: Jun Tanji and colleagues
  • Monkeys trained to make arm movement sequences based on visual cue (e.g., red light = "push, pull, turn"; green light = "turn, push, pull")
  • Visual cues specify which sequence to make, not individual movements

Key Findings:

  • Many SMA neurons selectively activated for a particular action embedded within a sequence
  • Some neurons responded only for a particular action sequence, irrespective of movement type (e.g., selectively responding to "Pull, Push, Turn")
  • Pharmacological inactivation of SMA → errors in learned sequences but NOT in explicitly cued sequences Exam

Human Neuroimaging Evidence:

  • SMA activation is larger for self-initiated movements than for visually cued movements
  • For movement sequences, SMA is active in both self-initiated and visually cued conditions — because SMA carries out the sequence steps after the initial cue
  • Prof "The initial cue leads to the sequence, and the SMA is carrying out the sequence itself"

Summary:

  • SMA provides relatively abstract motor intentions
  • SMA is crucial for producing movement sequences (requires generating actions without cues for each individual step)
4. Sensory-Motor Coordination and the Parietal Cortex Exam

Role of Parietal Cortex

  • Complex sensory-motor coordination involves translation of sensory spatial and textural information into motor commands
  • Example: Grabbing a cup of coffee requires continuous translation of:
  • Spatial information (where is the cup?)
  • Textural information (how heavy? how much coffee left?)
  • Converting all this into appropriate motor movements

Evidence: Parietal Cortex is Crucial

Optic Ataxia Exam: - Damage to parietal cortex leads to optic ataxia — failure to correctly integrate information about locations of the eye, hand, and target - Patients cannot accurately reach and grasp objects - Disruptions manifest in grasping: compare precision grip of patient R.V. vs. control — patients fail to grasp across center of mass of objects

Experimental Evidence: - Patients with parietal cortex damage vs. healthy controls interfacing with complex vs. simple objects - Healthy controls: fine-grained patterns of hand-eye coordination and finger-grab coordination (precision grip) - Patients: disrupted coordination — cannot perform coordinated hand-eye movements Prof - Confirmed by pharmacological inactivation of parietal cortex in monkeys → same deficits

Goodale and Milner's Dorsal/Ventral Stream Theory Exam:

  • Dorsal stream (parietal) → using vision to guide movement ("where/how" pathway)
  • Ventral stream (temporal) → object identification ("what" pathway)
  • Double dissociation evidence:
  • Temporal lobe damage: difficulty describing orientation of mail slot, but can put envelope in it
  • Parietal damage: no trouble naming objects, but difficulty reaching for and grasping them

Summary:

  • Parietal cortex = critical locus for integrating visual, eye position, and limb position data to produce coordinated eye-hand movements
5. Online Error Correction by the Cerebellum Exam

Brainstem Circuits:

  • Make anticipatory postural adjustments to accommodate movements
  • Correct posture for ongoing changes in balance
  • Coordinate more complex multi-joint movements

Cerebellum — Key Functions:

  • Large, foliated structure sitting atop the pons in the brainstem
  • Cerebellar circuits compute and relay net error between motor commands and actual movements
  • Functions as a comparison system: compares intended motor movement with actual motor movement → performs online error correction Exam

Cerebellar Circuitry — Inputs and Outputs Exam:

Inputs to cerebellum: 1. Frontal motor and parietal cortices → generate intention for movement → commands pass down to pons → cross midline via middle cerebellar peduncle → reach cerebellar cortex 2. Sensory feedback from the body: - Inferior olive - Spinal cord - Vestibular nucleus

Processing: Cerebellum compares intended vs. actual movement and computes error signals

Three functional divisions:

Division Function
Medial cerebellar (vermis) General coordination; damage → general ataxia
Lateral cerebellum Movement correction
Vestibulocerebellum Balance and eye movements

Cerebellar Disorders Exam:

General Ataxia: - Results from lesions to the medial cerebellar vermis - Characterized by uncoordinated, disorganized movements - Presents as wide-based, unsteady gait (looks like intoxication) Prof

Intention Tremor Exam: - Shows up in patients with ipsilateral cerebellar damage (note: ipsilateral, not contralateral!) - Only occurs during voluntary movements — NOT during reflexes - Erratic, halting movement when trying to reach a target - Classic test: patient asked to point rapidly from clinician's finger to their own nose → produces irregular, jerky movements instead of smooth pointing

Cerebellum and Motor Learning Exam:

  • Cerebellum is most active during initial learning of motor skills
  • Damage prevents learning new motor skills entirely
  • Neuroimaging: cerebellar activation peaks during initial learning, then declines as performance improves
  • Example: tracking a target on screen — motor error is initially large but improves with practice; cerebellar activation parallels this change
6. Cerebellar Contributions to Cognition Exam

Beyond Motor Control Prof

  • Traditionally viewed as purely motor structure
  • Modern neuroimaging has changed this view

Evidence for Cognitive Role:

  • Cerebellar activity during non-motor tasks: attention, timing, verbal working memory
  • Patients with cerebellar damage show deficits in speech, learning, timing, and working memory
  • Autism research: attentional problems linked to cerebellar abnormalities

The Comparator Model Extended to Cognition Exam:

  • Motor function: compares intended motor movement vs. actual motor movement → error correction
  • Cognitive extension: compares intended cognitive act vs. cognitive outcome → same comparison mechanism
  • The cerebellum may have evolved as a motor coordination/error correction system but has been co-opted for cognitive functions
7. Basal Ganglia Exam

Structure and Motor Loop:

  • The basal ganglia act as a gating mechanism — inhibiting potential movements until they are fully appropriate
  • The motor loop shows how different basal ganglia regions interact with cortex and thalamus
  • Connections are either excitatory (+) or inhibitory (−)
  • The entire motor loop ensures that actions from the cortex that want to be executed are properly selected and released

Basal Ganglia and Cognition Exam:

  • Anatomical studies reveal non-motor pathways through basal ganglia:
  • Limbic/emotional channel
  • Associative/cognitive channel
  • Principles governing movement disinhibition in the motor pathway also apply to emotional and cognitive processing
  • Prof Like the cerebellum, the basal ganglia may have evolved for inhibition/release of motor commands but have been adapted for other cognitive functions (emotion, goal-directed behavior)

Basal Ganglia and Learning Exam:

  • Strongest putamen activation when subjects had to learn joystick-cursor movement (with 90° offset)
  • Compared to: moving without feedback (P), watching cursor move (V), or resting (R)
  • Motor sequence learning (SRT tasks) is:
  • Preserved in amnesia patients (medial temporal lobe damage)
  • Impaired in patients with basal ganglia disorders (Parkinson's, Huntington's) Exam
8. Apraxia Exam

Definition:

  • Apraxia (Greek: "without act or deed") = loss of ability to execute learned voluntary movements (especially complex sequences) despite motivation and physical capacity
  • Key characteristic: muscles work normally, person understands the assignment, but cannot execute the movement correctly
  • The problem lies in motor planning processes in the brain

Types of Apraxia Exam:

Type Description Examples
Ideomotor apraxia Cannot voluntarily perform a learned action with an object, imitate actions, or follow verbal instructions Cannot wave goodbye on command, but waves naturally when someone leaves; cannot pantomime combing hair with a comb, but can comb when wanting to
Ideational apraxia Cannot carry out sequential tasks involving tools/objects in proper order Puts shoes on before socks; strikes candle against matchbox instead of striking match
Verbal apraxia Difficulty producing speech Affects both adults and children; devastating for learning and socialization

Key Distinction Exam:

  • Ideomotor: person knows what to do but cannot perform the actions
  • Ideational: person cannot plan the sequence of actions properly

Neural Basis:

  • Ideational and ideomotor apraxia linked to damage of parietal and premotor cortex (typically language-dominant side)
  • Can result from stroke, ischemia (ideomotor), or diffuse degeneration like Alzheimer's or Parkinson's disease
  • Example: Actor Daniel Radcliffe has mild apraxia (dyspraxia) — struggles to tie shoes

Behavioral Examples Prof:

  • Unable to demonstrate how to use a toothbrush
  • Difficulty dressing or buttoning clothes
  • Problems making gestures
  • Different types of apraxia → different behavioral difficulties
9. Mirror Neurons Exam

Discovery and Location:

  • Found in ventral premotor cortex (area F5) in monkeys
  • Also found in rostral inferior parietal cortex → projects to F5
  • Together form an interconnected fronto-parietal mirror system

Key Properties:

  • Fire both when performing an action AND when observing the same action performed by another
  • Some neurons show context specificity: selectively respond to grasping actions leading to eating vs. grasping to move an object (related to end goal)
  • Superior temporal sulcus (STS) relays perceptual information about biological motion to the parietal component but does NOT exhibit mirroring itself
  • Activity in this premotor circuit is inhibited to prevent obligatory copying behavior

Human Mirror System:

  • Putative human mirror system identified via fMRI:
  • Ventral premotor cortex (vPM)
  • Dorsal premotor cortex (dPM)
  • Motor/somatosensory cortex
  • Middle temporal gyrus (MTG)
  • Human activation is more widely distributed throughout motor system and STS than in monkeys
  • Similar activity occurs when people mime actions

Theoretical Implications:

  • Speculated roles in: understanding intentions of others, language acquisition, empathy
  • Possible relevance to autism (behavioral disorders)

Important Caveats Exam:

  • Adult monkeys do not learn much by direct imitation — undermines simplest interpretation
  • Human evidence is suggestive but indirect — fMRI cannot determine if same neurons (vs. adjacent ones) respond to both execution and observation
  • Debate: do mirror neurons reflect a unique social cognitive function, or are they simply established by associative learning?
10. Motor Skill Learning — Two Phases Exam

Early Learning Phase:

  • Performance improves rapidly within a single training session
  • Activates broad network: premotor cortex, pre-SMA, SMA, basal ganglia (caudate), cerebellum, parietal cortex
  • Duration depends on task complexity (minutes for simple sequences, months for complex tasks like piano)

Advanced Learning Phase:

  • Performance improves slowly over multiple practice sessions
  • Reduced global activity in most regions (less error correction needed)
  • Increased activity in specific subregions of primary motor cortex and cerebellum
  • These increases correlated with synchrony of motor responses

Serial Reaction Time (SRT) Task Exam:

  • Four screen locations, four spatially mapped response keys
  • Stimulus order follows a repeated (hidden) sequence (10-12 units long)
  • Even when unaware of the sequence, participants show faster reaction times over time (implicit learning)
  • Inserting a random block → reaction times significantly slower → proves sequence-specific learning
  • SRT learning is preserved in amnesia but impaired in Parkinson's/Huntington's → depends on basal ganglia, not medial temporal lobe

Key Takeaways

  1. Motor control is hierarchical — from abstract motor plans (cortex) down to specific muscle commands (spinal cord), with the basal ganglia and cerebellum providing modulatory control. Bernstein's signature experiment proves the abstract level of motor programs.

  2. SMA vs. Premotor cortex dissociation — SMA handles self-initiated and sequential movements; premotor cortex handles externally cued movements. Tanji's monkey experiment and pharmacological inactivation provide key evidence. [EXAM CRITICAL]

  3. Parietal cortex is essential for sensory-motor coordination — damage causes optic ataxia and disrupted precision grip. Supports Goodale & Milner's dorsal ("how") vs. ventral ("what") stream theory. [EXAM CRITICAL]

  4. The cerebellum is a comparator system — compares intended vs. actual movements for online error correction. Damage causes ataxia and intention tremor. Most active during initial motor learning. Now also implicated in cognition (attention, timing, working memory). [EXAM CRITICAL]

  5. Basal ganglia gate movement through disinhibition — also involved in motor learning (SRT tasks impaired in Parkinson's/Huntington's) and have expanded into emotional and cognitive channels. [EXAM CRITICAL]

  6. Apraxia demonstrates the cognitive nature of motor control — patients have intact muscles and comprehension but cannot plan/execute learned movements. Three types (ideomotor, ideational, verbal) with distinct behavioral profiles.

  7. Motor planning is graded, not all-or-nothing — premotor neurons accumulate sensory evidence proportional to motion coherence and reward value before committing to a movement.

  8. Motor skill learning has two phases — early (rapid improvement, broad network activation) and advanced (slow improvement, focused activation in primary motor cortex and cerebellum). The SRT task is the classic paradigm for studying implicit motor sequence learning.
