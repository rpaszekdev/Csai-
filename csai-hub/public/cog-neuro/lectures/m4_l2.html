1. Classification of Emotions: Categorical, Dimensional, and Component-Process Theories



The flipped session opened with a survey of how emotions are taxonomically organised. Three main theoretical families were contrasted, each with very different implications for how a neuroscientist would design an experiment. Exam


Categorical theories


Categorical theories treat emotions as distinct, discrete categories, splitting a small set of basic emotions from a larger pool of complex emotions.






Property
Basic emotions
Complex emotions






Origin
Innate, pan-cultural
Learned, socially/culturally shaped

The core set of basic emotions that researchers (mostly) agree on: anger, sadness, happiness, fear, disgust, surprise Exam. Note: most basic emotions are negative in valence — a clue about evolutionary priority on threat detection.


Prof emphasis Prof
Complex emotions like pride are difficult to study because expression is more subdued, occurs in varied contexts, and is highly culture-dependent — some East Asian cultures view overt pride negatively, so the postural "shoulders back, chin up" expression is suppressed.


Dimensional theories


Each emotion is a point in a multi-dimensional space, defined by two continuous axes:
- Arousal — the intensity of the emotion (low to high)
- Valence — the pleasantness of the emotion (negative to positive)


Two competing geometries:






Model
Shape
Description






Vector model
Boomerang
Two arms (positive/negative valence) meeting at a neutral endpoint; arousal = distance from neutral




Circumplex model (Russell, 1980)
Circle
Emotions ordered around circumference; orthogonal axes of arousal × valence; similar emotions cluster






Worked example from the presenters: relaxed vs excited — both share positive valence, but relaxed has low arousal and excited has high arousal, so they sit far apart on the circle. Boredom/gloominess sit in low-arousal/low-valence quadrant; frustration/distress in high-arousal/low-valence.


Component-process theories


Emotions are fluid, dynamic states arising from interactions of multiple cognitive appraisal processes — not fixed categories.


Key appraisal dimensions: goal relevance, conduciveness vs obstructiveness, urgency, coping potential, control, power. Different appraisal patterns yield different emotional experiences — the same situation can produce different emotions depending on how it is appraised.


Prof emphasis Prof
Component-process theories are rarely tested with neuroscience methods because of their complexity — a recurring critique throughout the course.



















2. Fear Acquisition: The Amygdala and the Two-Pathway Model



Fear is the most well-characterised emotion in cognitive neuroscience because it can be elicited rapidly, conditioned reliably, and studied across species. Exam


Measuring fear output


    

  • Skin conductance response (SCR) — sympathetic-only, measured at the palms (eccrine sweat glands), excellent index of arousal
    • 

  • Fear-potentiated startle — eyeblink (orbicularis oculi) reflex amplified by fear context, measured via EMG
    • 

  • Pupil dilation
    • 



Core circuit for fear acquisition


    

  • Amygdala — central hub; basolateral nuclei integrate sensory input, central nucleus drives output
    • 

  • Thalamus — first relay; provides sensory input both directly (fast) and via cortex (slow)
    • 

  • Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) — also activated during acquisition
    • 



The two pathways Exam Prof


The presenters used the classic "bear on a forest trail" scenario to illustrate LeDoux's dual-pathway model:






Pathway
Route
Speed
Function






Rapid subcortical ("low road")
Thalamus → amygdala (bypasses cortex)
Few milliseconds
Crude sensory discrimination; triggers immediate fight/flight/freeze/fawn response




Slow cortical ("high road")
Thalamus → neocortex → amygdala
Slower
Detailed perceptual analysis; refines or suppresses the initial response






The fast pathway essentially primes the amygdala so the slower, more detailed cortical analysis can be processed more effectively. Within the amygdala, intercalated cells (ITC) act as a gating mechanism — if it's not a real threat, ITCs inhibit signal flow to the central amygdala and break the chain. If it is a threat, the central amygdala forwards signals to the hypothalamus (heart rate, breathing) and midbrain/striatum (action selection).


Prof emphasis Prof
A student asked whether the rapid subcortical pathway is conscious. Answer: not really — it's fast and pre-conscious, even though the response feels integrated. The cortical pathway is what brings detail and conscious awareness.

3. Fear Modification, Extinction, and the vmPFC Brake



Emotional perseveration


A prolonged fear response that persists even after the danger is gone. Hallmark of clinical disorders: anxiety, phobias, PTSD. Exam


Fear extinction


Repeatedly presenting the conditioned stimulus (CS) without the unconditioned stimulus (US, e.g. shock) reduces the conditioned fear response.


Prof emphasis Prof
Critical conceptual point: extinction does NOT erase the original CS-US association. New, competing connections are formed that override the old ones. The original association can resurface (relapse) under stress or context change.


Brain regions of extinction


    

  • Ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) — applies a "braking" function on the amygdala via inhibitory projections; suppresses fear when no longer adaptive (e.g., the zoo-tiger example: you don't fear the tiger because vmPFC contextualises the safety)
    • 

  • Rats with vmPFC damage acquire fear normally but take much longer to extinguish it
    • 

  • Human fMRI: vmPFC activity during extinction correlates with extinction retention
    • 



Contextual fear conditioning


Fear can attach not only to the cue but to the environment in which conditioning occurred. Requires hippocampus (spatial/declarative memory). Human amnesics with hippocampal damage show SCR to a tone but cannot recall the conditioning episode — proving hippocampus is not purely emotional (contra limbic system theory) but binds emotion to context.



















4. Somatic Marker Hypothesis & the Iowa Gambling Task



Antonio Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis explains the role of emotion in decision making. Exam


Core claim


The vmPFC is a "library" linking factual knowledge ↔ bioregulatory states for past events. When a similar situation recurs, prior knowledge calls up the relevant somatic markers — autonomic, endocrine, and musculoskeletal traces of the original emotional state — which steer decisions.


Prof emphasis Prof
Presenter's anecdote: "When I was a kid, a bird flew close and pooped on me. Now every time a bird comes near, I get a bad feeling and duck away." That's a somatic marker — your gut tells you before your reasoning does.


Direct vs indirect activation


    

  • Direct ("body loop") — vmPFC triggers the actual visceral response via amygdala
    • 

  • Indirect ("as-if loop") — vmPFC simulates the somatosensory pattern in the insula and somatosensory cortex without needing the body to physically respond Exam
    • 



Why this matters for rationality


Somatic markers act as a heuristic — they prune the decision tree by flagging options as good/bad before logical weighing begins. Reasoning becomes more efficient because fewer options need explicit evaluation.


Prof emphasis Prof
Counterintuitive but exam-critical: you need gut feelings to make rational decisions. Pure logic without somatic markers leads to paralysis, as in patient Elliot (Damasio's case).


Patient Elliot


Tumor compressed both frontal lobes upward. After successful surgery his IQ and logic remained perfect, but he could not make daily decisions: lost jobs, divorced twice, partnered with a "disreputable character" leading to bankruptcy. Used as the prototype example of vmPFC damage → poor decision making despite intact intellect.


Iowa Gambling Task Exam


4 decks (A, B, C, D) — participants try to maximise winnings.
- Decks A, B: high reward (+$100) but occasional very large punishments → disadvantageous long-term
- Decks C, D: smaller rewards (+$50) and smaller punishments → advantageous long-term

The healthy participants' bodies flag risk before they consciously know — direct evidence that "the body feels before the consequence is present." vmPFC patients lack this and so make persistently bad choices.


The insula is critical for interoception — monitoring the physiological state of the organism and storing visceral/skeletomotor representations of emotional states.



















5. Emotion's Influence on Perception and Attention



Emotion is not just generated by sensory input — it actively shapes what we see and what we attend to. Exam


Prioritisation and attentional bias


    

  • Sensory input with emotional significance is given priority through automatic detection of salient features
    • 

  • Triggers attentional bias — your attention is automatically pulled toward emotional stimuli (threats, joy, fear)
    • 

  • All input is screened by the amygdala, which flags salience and triggers preferential processing
    • 



Perceptual awareness: RSVP and the attentional blink


Rapid serial visual presentation (RSVP) = a fast stream of stimuli (words/images) one after another. The brain misses the second target because it's still processing the first → attentional blink.


Key finding Exam: if the second target is emotionally significant, it bypasses the attentional blink — shorter lag times relative to neutral words. Emotion gets a "skip the line" pass.


Fast unconscious detection


With high-threat stimuli (loud bang, snake), the amygdala and autonomic nervous system fire even before conscious awareness. Pathological version: PTSD patients show exaggerated startle — the system is hyper-tuned.


The amygdala feedback loop on visual processing


Prof emphasis Prof
Crucial point — the amygdala doesn't just receive visual input, it feeds back to all stages of the ventral visual stream, including primary visual cortex (V1).


    

  • Amygdala receives input from late visual stages (inferior temporal cortex)
    • 

  • Amygdala sends feedback to early visual areas, including V1 and the fusiform gyrus
    • 

  • Evidence: fearful eye-whites elicit a much larger amygdala response than happy eye-whites; fusiform gyrus shows enhanced activation for fearful faces; in patients with amygdala damage, the fusiform enhancement is absent, proving causation
    • 



This creates tunnel vision for emotional content — the brain over-allocates resources to threat-relevant features.


Indirect routes of amygdala influence on attention


Mostly indirect — the amygdala doesn't directly control attention but biases the network through:
1. Amygdala → nucleus basalis of Meynert → acetylcholine release in cortex → enhanced attention
2. Amygdala → ACC, insula, mPFC, OFC (interface regions) — these then target:
   - dlPFC (what to focus on)
   - Posterior parietal cortex (where to focus)
   - PCC (sustained attention)
3. Direct route: amygdala → sensory cortices for the visual feedback loop above


Insula = salience detection; OFC = emotion evaluation; ACC = attention allocation.



















6. Emotion's Influence on Memory Consolidation



Flashbulb memory


Flashbulb memory = vivid, detailed recollection of an emotionally fraught episode "registered graphically in the mind's eye" (term coined in the 1970s). Both positive and negative arousing events leave more lasting impressions than neutral ones. Exam


Prof emphasis Prof
You remember the embarrassing thing you said when you were 12, but not five years of high-school biology — because the embarrassing event had a much higher emotional weight at encoding.


Memory modulation hypothesis (James McGaugh)


Emotionally arousing events enhance memory by activating systems that regulate storage of newly acquired information. Exam
- Centrepiece: the amygdala
- Amygdala enhances consolidation in the medial temporal lobe (MTL), dlPFC, vlPFC
- Two routes:
  - Direct — axonal projections
  - Indirect — release of stress hormones into the bloodstream
- Arousal is the primary force behind this neuromodulation


Stress hormones and the HPA axis


Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis mediates the principal stress response.
- CRF released by hypothalamus → ACTH from anterior pituitary → glucocorticoids (cortisol) from adrenal cortex
- Two receptor types: mineralocorticoid receptors (occupied at rest) vs glucocorticoid receptors (engaged during stress)
- Bruce McEwen's concept of allostatic overload — cumulative wear-and-tear from chronic stress; allostasis = "stability through change"


Three key hormones


    

  • Cortisol — crosses blood-brain barrier, acts on central receptors
    • 

  • Epinephrine — acts on β-adrenergic receptors on visceral sensory endings (peripheral)
    • 

  • Norepinephrine — same β-adrenergic targets
    • 



Why hormones operate during consolidation, not encoding


Hormones are slow — they arrive after the event. That's why after a test or public speech, you sometimes can't remember exactly what you said — consolidation is happening post-hoc. Exam


Propranolol experiment Exam


Propranolol = β-adrenergic blocker. Subjects shown an audio-visual narrative with an emotionally arousing middle (a car crash) flanked by neutral framing.

The propranolol effect mimics amygdala lesion → confirms β-adrenergic action mediates the amygdala's memory-enhancing role.


Emotional version of subsequent memory paradigm


Participants scanned while viewing emotional vs neutral words/pictures; later memory tested. Items later remembered (vs forgotten) showed stronger encoding activity in amygdala, hippocampus, entorhinal cortex, and PFC — and amygdala-entorhinal cortex activity was correlated only for emotional items, not neutral. Consistent with the memory modulation hypothesis.



















7. Emotion Regulation: Reappraisal and the Top-Down Brake



Emotion regulation = the process by which people influence which emotions they have, when, how strongly, and how they express them. Exam


Prof emphasis Prof
Transient emotional episodes are healthy — they help us adapt. Regulation only becomes critical when emotion is maladaptive: wrong social context, misdirected target, too long, too strong — leading to distress or psychopathology.


Strategies — James Gross's model (timing × target)


    

  1. Situation selection — change behaviour to avoid the elicitor entirely (e.g., fear of flying → take the train)
    2. 

  2. Cognitive reappraisal — change the meaning of the elicitor to alter its impact
    3. 



Two types of reappraisal:
- Situation-focused — re-imagine the situation improving (wounded soldier eventually goes home, recovers)
- Self-focused — view the scene from a third-person perspective, detaching emotionally


Ochsner et al. (2004) experiment Exam


Negative images shown under two conditions: look (passive) vs decrease (apply reappraisal).

The top-down model


    

  1. Dorsal frontoparietal attentional network (frontal eye fields, intraparietal sulcus — the task-positive network) selects and applies the cognitive strategy
    2. 

  2. Dorsal ACC, vlPFC, dlPFC translate this into regulatory control signals
    3. 

  3. These signals target and dampen the amygdala and insula (the emotion-processing regions)
    4. 

  4. Result: reduced subjective negative affect
    5. 



Prof emphasis Prof
Take-home: emotion regulation is the interaction between dorsal executive control and ventral emotion-processing regions. The frontoparietal network was active during both increase and decrease conditions — so it's a general control mechanism, not strategy-specific.


The insula is doubly relevant here: it appeared earlier in somatic markers (interoception) and is also linked to the emotion of disgust.



















8. Mirror Neurons and the Premotor System



Discovery — Rizzolatti (1996)


Macaque experiments: mirror neurons are cells (mainly in premotor cortex, area F5) that fire both when the monkey performs an action AND when it sees someone else perform that action. Exam


Anatomy


    

  • F5 (premotor cortex, frontal lobe) — handles hand/mouth movements
    • 

  • PFG / area IP (rostral inferior parietal cortex) — projects to F5; spatial/body-related processing
    • 

  • These two regions form an interconnected fronto-parietal mirror system
    • 

  • Superior temporal sulcus (STS) — relays perceptual info about biological motion to the parietal node, but STS itself does NOT mirror — it just watches and reports Exam
    • 



Goal vs movement


Critical finding from the macaque graphs: mirror neurons fire denser action potentials AFTER the food is placed on the tray — meaning they encode the goal of the action, not the raw movement itself.


Why don't we copy everything?


Prof emphasis Prof
If mirror neurons fired and you simply mimicked, you'd copy every action you observed. The brain inhibits motor output so you can understand the action without performing it.


What might mirror neurons do?


    

  • Provide a substrate for understanding social actions and intentions
    • 

  • Link perception and motor experience (possibly just learned by ordinary association)
    • 

  • Language acquisition (linking heard speech to mouth movements)
    • 

  • Empathy
    • 

  • Implicated in autism (the "broken mirror" hypothesis — disputed)
    • 



Challenges to the theory


    

  • Monkeys don't really learn by imitation, which is awkward if mirror neurons supposedly support imitation
    • 

  • Human fMRI can't tell whether the same neuron fires for doing and watching — could be different but adjacent neurons
    • 

  • Some scientists argue mirror neurons aren't special — the brain just learns these associations through normal experience like everything else
    •

Why perspective taking matters


Mirror neuron activity is time-locked to someone else's action — you need an agent performing right now. Mentalizing / Theory of Mind is broader: understanding beliefs, desires, feelings even when no action is occurring.

10. False-Belief Tasks, Theory of Mind in Apes, and Empathy



Do apes have Theory of Mind?


Prof emphasis Prof
"Short answer: yes... no, I'm joking." Apes engage in deception in the wild — implying they track where others are attending and what others might falsely believe.


Subordinate vs dominant chimp study — subordinate chimps were more likely to go for food the dominant chimp did not see or held a false belief about, because the location was changed only when the subordinate was watching.


False-belief tasks Exam


The "acid test" for Theory of Mind = predicting others' behaviour based on false beliefs.






Task
Setup

Humans develop these abilities around age 4. Exam


Implicit vs explicit Theory of Mind


Most researchers believe kids and apes have implicit Theory of Mind — they don't form explicit thoughts like "I know that he believes X but actually Y is true." That kind of explicit reasoning is meta-representation (representing someone else's mental representations).

Key Takeaways



    

  • Three theories of emotion classification — categorical (basic vs complex), dimensional (arousal × valence; vector vs circumplex), and component-process (appraisal-based). Categorical and dimensional theories dominate neuroscience because they are testable.
    • 

  • Fear has two pathways: a fast subcortical thalamus → amygdala route (crude, pre-conscious, drives fight/flight) and a slow cortical route (detailed, refines response). Memorise this for the exam.
    • 

  • Amygdala vs hippocampus double dissociation (S.M. and W.C.): amygdala = fear response; hippocampus = contextual binding. The hippocampus is not dedicated to emotion, contradicting the limbic-system theory.
    • 

  • vmPFC is the brake on the amygdala — critical for fear extinction, somatic markers, and rational decision-making. Damage (e.g., Elliot, Iowa Gambling Task patients) leaves intellect intact but destroys real-world judgement.
    • 

  • Somatic markers = bodily flags that prune the decision tree before logical reasoning — you need gut feelings to be rational. Insula is the interoception hub.
    • 

  • Emotion biases perception and attention through amygdala feedback to V1 and fusiform, plus indirect routes via nucleus basalis (acetylcholine) and ACC/insula → dlPFC/parietal/PCC.
    • 

  • McGaugh's memory modulation hypothesis: amygdala enhances MTL/PFC consolidation via direct projections AND slow stress hormones (cortisol, epinephrine acting on β-adrenergic receptors). Propranolol blocks the emotional memory boost.
    • 

  • Emotion regulation uses dorsal frontoparietal executive control (Gross's reappraisal) to dampen amygdala/insula activity — the Ochsner experiment is the canonical study.
    • 

  • Mirror neurons (Rizzolatti, F5/PFG) encode goals not movements, and form a fronto-parietal loop with STS feeding it. Implicated in action understanding, language, and possibly empathy/autism — but the theory has serious open challenges.
    • 

  • Theory of Mind emerges around age 4 (false-belief tasks), recruits a network of mPFC, TPJ, STS, temporal poles, and precuneus/cingulate. Empathy (Decety's 4-component model) builds on this with emotion sharing, self-awareness, mental flexibility, and emotion regulation.
