1. What is Cognitive Neuroscience?

Cognitive neuroscience is defined as work at the intersection of cognitive science and neuroscience. Exam

  • Cognitive science seeks to understand the information processing associated with functions like perception, memory, and decision making
  • Neuroscience seeks to characterize the structure and function of the nervous system
  • Cognitive neuroscience applies research methods from neuroscience to the functions and behaviors studied by cognitive science

Key Distinction Exam

Cognitive neuroscience is NOT simply mapping brain regions activated during a task (finding "neural correlates"). It aims to create neurobiologically grounded models of cognition — understanding how and why, not just where.

Prof emphasis Prof

The professor distinguished three tracks: cognitive/computational neuroscience, behavioural neuroscience (animal experiments), and molecular neuroscience (ion channel dynamics). Cognitive neuroscience tries to get at the neural basis of cognition using every tool available: brain scanners, lesions, brain perturbations, and computational modelling.

Tools at Our Disposal

  • Brain scanners (EEG, fMRI, MEG)
  • Lesion studies
  • Brain perturbation methods (TMS, tDCS)
  • Computational modelling — "using explicit, mathematically precise models to explicate how neurones might actually produce cognitive behaviour"
2. Historical Development — The Four Eras Exam

Era 1: Natural Philosophy & Early Psychology (19th century)

Key figures: | Person | Dates | Contribution | |--------|-------|-------------| | Hermann von Helmholtz | 1821–1894 | Early psychophysics; models of mental processes through behavioral observation | | Wilhelm Wundt | 1832–1920 | Founded first psychology laboratory; experimental manipulation of cognition | | William James | 1842–1910 | Early psychology; Principles of Psychology |

  • These pioneers built models of mental processes through behavioral observation and experimental manipulation
  • Used methods like psychophysical judgment and memorization for later testing to quantify cognitive processes
  • Some of these methods are still used today Exam
Prof emphasis Prof

Helmholtz described as "a bit of a hero of mine" by the professor

Era 2: Behaviourism (Early 20th century)

Key figure: B.F. Skinner (1904–1990)

  • Behaviourism did away with any subjective work on mental functions Exam
  • Core argument: We can't study mental representations or mental processes directly, so the only thing we should focus on is observable behaviour
  • Constructed highly controlled experiments matching objective external stimuli (light intensity, sounds) with measurable behaviour
  • Operant conditioning (Skinner): Behavior occurring before a reward becomes more likely to be repeated; studied using the Skinner box Exam

Critical limitation: Behaviourism treated the mind as a "black box" — input and output only, no internal processes
