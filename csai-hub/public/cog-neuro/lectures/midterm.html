3. Mock Exam Walkthrough -- Key Concepts



3.1 Event-Related Potentials (ERPs) Exam


    

  • Mock exam question: What are ERPs?
    • 

  • Correct answer: ERPs are raw EEG signals averaged across multiple trials Exam
    • 

  • Key distinctions:
    • 

  • ERPs = raw EEG averaged across trials (time-domain averaging)
    • 

  • NOT EEG spectra (that would be frequency-domain analysis)
    • 

  • NOT averaged across recording sites (that would lose spatial information)
    • 

  • Why this works: The signal of interest is time-locked to the event, while background noise is random. Averaging cancels out noise and reveals the consistent neural response
    • 



3.2 Causation vs. Correlation in Neuroscience Methods Exam


This was the most extensively discussed mock exam question and represents a core exam theme.


Mock exam scenario: A researcher hypothesises that the lateral prefrontal cortex is causally involved in self-control during food choices.


Question: Which method is most appropriate?






Option
Method
Verdict






A (CORRECT)
TMS -- testing participants twice, once with real TMS and once with sham TMS of the lateral prefrontal cortex
Establishes a causal link by actively manipulating brain activity




B
fMRI -- testing participants before and after depleting self-control
Only shows correlation, not causation




C
fMRI -- comparing brain-damaged vs. healthy participants
Lesion studies have interpretation problems; not a controlled experimental manipulation

Critical distinction Prof:
- fMRI can show a correlation between self-control and lateral prefrontal cortex activity -- not necessarily a causal link
- TMS establishes causation by actively up-regulating or down-regulating activity in that particular area


Key terms from this question:


    

  • TMS (Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation): Non-invasive extracranial brain stimulation that can temporarily up-regulate or down-regulate activity in targeted brain areas Exam
    • 

  • Sham TMS: Placebo condition -- "Like a placebo drug." Exam
    • 

  • Lateral prefrontal cortex: Associated with self-control and executive functions Prof

The Inverse Problem Exam


Applies to both EEG and MEG: A distribution of scalp-recorded electrical (or magnetic) activity could have been produced by any configuration of generator(s). You cannot uniquely determine the source from the surface recording alone.












5. Lesion Studies -- Strengths and Limitations Exam



Strengths:
- Provide extremely valuable information about what happens when the system is "actually broken" Prof
- Can reveal which brain regions are necessary for specific functions
- Remain "highly relevant" despite their limitations


Limitations Prof:
- Individual cases can be misleading -- need consistency across patients
- Brain damage is rarely clean -- typically affects multiple areas and connections
- Cannot establish what a region does, only what goes wrong without it

The Pivotal Transition: Chomsky's Critique Exam


    

  • Chomsky's 1959 review of Skinner's Verbal Behavior
    • 

  • Argued that behaviourism could never explain the structural and generative properties of mental phenomena like language
    • 

  • This revived the legitimacy of studying internal mental processes
    • 

  • Marked the critical shift from behaviourism to cognitive science
    • 

  • The professor presented this as the key turning point in the history of the field
    • 













7. Convergence and Complementarity Exam



    

  • Convergence: Using multiple different methods to study the same cognitive function. When results from different approaches point to the same conclusion, confidence increases significantly
    • 

  • Complementarity: Different methods have different strengths and weaknesses (e.g., EEG has excellent temporal but poor spatial resolution, while fMRI has the reverse). Using them together provides a more complete picture
    • 

  • Meta-analysis: A formal statistical technique for combining results across multiple studies
    •
