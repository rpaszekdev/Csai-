1. Prefrontal Cortex: A Key Contributor to Executive Function Exam



Executive functions modulate the activity of other cognitive functions in a flexible and goal-directed manner. Central to nearly all definitions are two concepts: rules (abstract and flexible behavioural prescriptions, e.g., "drive on the right") and control (engaging the rule appropriate to the current context). Exam


1.1 Historical Anchors


    

  • 19th century: belief that frontal lobes underlie higher cognition was based on their relatively large size in humans/great apes
    • 

  • Franz Gall proposed phrenology: mental functions are localised, and skull shape reflects them. Wrong, but it encouraged research into brain localisation Exam
    • 

  • Hitzig & Fritsch (late 1860s): stimulating the posterior frontal lobe in dogs caused contralateral movement; damage abolished voluntary movement but spared reflexes. Anterior frontal stimulation produced no motor/sensory effect → speculation that it supports higher cognitive functions Exam
    • 

  • David Ferrier & Leonardo Bianchi: bilateral prefrontal lesions in monkeys caused failure to recognise objects, inability to use past experience, loss of initiative, blunted affect, and disorganised behaviour. Unilateral damage rarely produced these effects Prof
    • 



1.2 Brain Size, Prefrontal Cortex, and Intelligence


    

  • Brain size alone does not predict intelligence (the elephant has a larger brain than humans) Prof
    • 

  • Brodmann (1912): PFC = ~7% of dog cortex, ~10% in monkeys, ~20% in great apes, ~30% in humans
    • 

  • Semendeferi et al. (1997, 2002) revised this using structural MRI: the proportional size of the frontal lobes is roughly equal in humans and great apes (~35%) — humans are not distinctive because of an oversized PFC, but because of overall brain scaling Exam
    • 

  • Take-home: PFC size scales with neocortex area but is not disproportionately larger in humans relative to hemispheric cortex volume
    • 



Prof emphasis Prof
The prof reinforced the take-home: executive function does not arise from one region (no "homunculus"). It emerges from interactions among prefrontal sub-regions and their network partners (parietal, basal ganglia, ACC, thalamus).

3.2 Connectivity (Figure 13.3)


    

  • PFC has broad, diverse, bidirectional connections — more than any other cortical region Exam
    • 

  • General rule: PFC connects with secondary, not primary, sensory cortices (e.g., inferior temporal cortex → lateral PFC, but V1 does not project there) Exam
    • 

  • Exception: OFC receives input from primary taste, olfactory, and somatosensory cortices — reflecting its role in evaluating reward and sensory value Exam
    • 

  • Lateral PFC: strong reciprocal links with posterior parietal cortex, supplementary motor area, frontal eye fields → fronto-parietal attention/WM network
    • 

  • vmPFC / OFC: connect to amygdala, MTL, ventral tegmental area → emotion + memory-guided decisions
    • 

  • Shared: mediodorsal nucleus of thalamus, basal ganglia, ACC, insula
    • 

  • Important asymmetry: PFC projects directly to basal ganglia, but information returns only indirectly via thalamus and substantia nigra Exam
    •

3.4 Famous Patient Cases Exam


    

  • Phineas Gage (1848): tamping iron through ventromedial/orbitofrontal cortex. Historically a textbook disinhibition case; modern reconstruction (Damasio et al. 1994) confirms OFC + vmPFC damage. Recent work (Macmillan, Wilgus) suggests the personality-change narrative is exaggerated — Gage stayed employed and travelled to South America. Exam
    • 

  • Acquired sociopathy (Damasio): vmPFC damage → blunted affect with emotional outbursts, poor social decisions; patients know the rules but act impulsively
    • 

  • Early-life vmPFC damage: similar to congenital sociopathy — patients cannot even articulate moral rules
    • 

  • Environmental dependency syndrome (Lhermitte 1986): anterior/medial frontal damage → imitation behaviour (mimics interviewer) and utilization behaviour (uses any object presented — pours water, hangs picture with hammer when seen). Behaviour driven by environment, not goals.
    • 



















4. Establishing Rules: How a "Rule" Is Encoded Exam



4.1 The Switch-Operator Metaphor — Miller & Cohen (2001)


Earl Miller & Jonathan Cohen likened PFC to a railroad switch operator: it sets up patterns of information flow, strengthening some pathways and inhibiting others, to bring about context-appropriate behaviour. Example: a phone rings → answer at home, ignore at the neighbour's. Exam


4.2 Distributed Rule Coding — Wallis, Anderson & Miller (2001) Exam


This was one of the most-discussed papers in the flipped session.


    

  • Monkeys trained to follow either a matching rule or a non-matching rule, signalled by a cue (e.g., juice reward → matching, high tone → non-matching)
    • 

  • Procedure: sample image → cue → delay → test 1 (respond if cued rule says "match") → delay → test 2
    • 

  • Single-unit recordings in lateral PFC: individual neurons fired selectively for a particular rule, independent of the cue (juice or tone) and independent of the stimulus (Object A or B) Exam
    • 

  • Conclusion: rules are encoded in distributed populations of neurons; the same neuron can participate in different rules depending on context
    • 

  • Rule-selective neurons cluster along the principal sulcus of lateral PFC
    •

  • Lateral PFC patients show disinhibited early-latency ERPs in sensory cortex — sensory filtering fails
    •

  • Ventral PFC damage → failure to match behaviour to context (the disinhibition syndrome)
    • 

  • Antonio Damasio's acquired sociopathy: blunted affect + emotional outbursts; patients can verbalise rules but still act impulsively; remorse without behavioural change Exam
    •

    •

6. Shifting and Relating Rules Exam



6.1 Shifting — Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (WCST)


    

  • Brenda Milner (1963): first reported that PFC patients show perseveration on the WCST Exam
    • 

  • Sort cards by colour, shape, or number; rule changes after a streak of correct sorts; healthy people switch quickly using feedback
    • 

  • Perseveration: continuing to use a previously-valid rule after repeated negative feedback → hallmark of PFC dysfunction Exam
    • 

  • Crucial nuance from the prof: perseveration is NOT a learning deficit. PFC patients can learn new rules just fine when the old rule is unavailable. The deficit is specifically the inability to disengage from the old, still-applicable rule. Prof
    • 

  • Brain regions: lateral PFC for rule switching; OFC for processing feedback / reversal learning
    • 



6.2 Reversal Learning and the OFC


    

  • OFC lesions → impaired reversal learning (slow to update when stimulus-reward contingencies flip)
    • 

  • Lateral PFC lesions can also impair reversal, but only because of failure to stay engaged (consistent with dysexecutive syndrome) — not because of reward-learning deficit per se
    • 



6.3 Relating Rules — Higher-Order Models of the World


    

  • Kurt Goldstein & Martin Scheerer (1941): frontal damage destroys the abstract attitude — patients cannot say "the snow is black" because it is untrue; they interpret proverbs literally ("People in glass houses…" → "otherwise they will break the walls")
    • 

  • Raven's Progressive Matrices: requires identifying and integrating multiple rules simultaneously across rows and columns. Frontopolar cortex (FPC) damage produces selective deficits on complex matrices Exam
    • 

  • Smith, Keramatian & Christoff (2007): FPC activates specifically for relational integration trials vs. simple comparison
    • 

  • Tower of London / Tower of Hanoi: planning task. Shallice (1982): left anterior frontal damage → poor performance, especially on complex (4-5 move) puzzles Exam
    •

A central organising principle the prof flagged repeatedly: posterior PFC handles concrete, motor-near rules; anterior PFC handles abstract, goal-distant rules.

8. Conflict Monitoring and the ACC Exam



8.1 The Conflict-Monitoring Model


    

  • Posner & Petersen (1990) proposed three control systems: alertness, orienting, detection of events requiring extra resources — the last (later called conflict monitoring) was tied to the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) Exam
    • 

  • Conflict = information indicating an incorrect response is available earlier than or simultaneously with information indicating the correct response
    • 

  • The Stroop task is the canonical demonstration: incongruent trials (word "YELLOW" in blue ink) > congruent trials in ACC activation Exam
    • 

  • ACC does not resolve the conflict — it signals lateral PFC, which adjusts behaviour. Kerns et al. (2004): high ACC activation on trial N predicts higher dlPFC activation on trial N+1 Exam
    • 

  • ACC subdivisions: dorsal ACC → cognitive control; ventral / rostral ACC → emotional processing (Bush, Luu & Posner 2000)
    • 



8.2 Error-Related Negativity (ERN) Exam


    

  • ERN: negative-polarity scalp potential following a mistake
    • 

  • Two variants:
    • 

  • Response ERN: after the participant realises an incorrect motor movement
    • 

  • Feedback ERN: after feedback indicates an undesired outcome (e.g., gambling loss)
    • 

  • ERN amplitude correlates with slower, more cautious responding on the next trial — link to behavioural adjustment
    • 



8.3 Challenges to the Conflict-Monitoring Model Exam


The student presenter ran through three problems — the prof endorsed this as exam-likely material:


    

  1. Specificity problem: ACC activates not only for conflict but also for correct feedback and rewarding outcomes — and may track conflict at earlier processing stages, not just response selection
    2. 

  2. "Avoidance of mistakes" reframing: in a difficult visual search where subjects could guess or skip, dmPFC activation was highest when they chose NOT to guess, with no difference between correct and error trials → ACC supports avoiding mistakes, not just detecting errors
    3. 

  3. Inconsistent causal evidence:
    4. 

  4. Monkey stop-signal and eye-movement tasks: cingulate neurons did not increase activity under high response conflict
    5. 

  5. Fellows & Farah (2005): patients with dorsal ACC lesions perform normally on Stroop and go/no-go (Stroop effect identical to controls in Figure 13.16) Exam
    6. 

  6. Macaque cingulate lesions: error detection intact; deficit is in learning which actions lead to better rewards
    7. 



8.4 The New Account: Mismatch / Volatility Tracking


    

  • Recent work (e.g., Behrens et al. 2007) recasts dmPFC activity as encoding the mismatch between expectations and outcomes — a learning signal rather than a conflict signal
    • 

  • dmPFC activation tracks environmental volatility (how unstable reward contingencies are over time) — see Figure 13.17 Exam
    • 

  • This fits both the lesion data and the reward-related activations that broke the original model
    •

10. Working Memory: Maintaining Rules and Information Over Time Exam



10.1 Definition and Two Models


    

  • Working memory = the maintenance and manipulation of information that is no longer directly available to the senses Exam
    • 

  • Mental arithmetic (multiply 23 × 7) is the textbook example: hold the rule, run sequential operations, update intermediate values
    • 







Model
Architecture
Status






Baddeley (1970s, 2003)
central executive + phonological loop + visuospatial sketchpad + episodic buffer; each buffer has a store + rehearsal mechanism
classic, conceptual backdrop




Cowan (1998)
one long-term memory store; activated representations decay unless rehearsed; only ~4 items fall within the focus of executive control
better supported by current neuroscience Exam






The Cowan model predicts that WM should activate the same sensory regions that process the original stimulus — a prediction now confirmed (see §10.4).


10.2 The Funahashi, Bruce & Goldman-Rakic (1989) Study Exam


The single most influential WM neurophysiology study — flagged by the presenters and prof as must-know for the exam.


    

  • Oculomotor delayed response task: monkey sees a target flash at one of 8 spatial locations → 3-second delay (no stimulus, no movement) → saccade to remembered location
    • 

  • dlPFC neurons fire continuously through the delay ("delay-period activity") — sustained without external input Exam
    • 

  • Activity is content-specific: each neuron has a memory field, firing maximally for one preferred location (e.g., 135°) and weakly for others (e.g., 270°)
    • 

  • Behavioural link: when delay-period firing broke down, the monkey made errors — sustained activity predicts correct behaviour, not just correlates with it
    • 



10.3 Petrides — Maintenance vs. Manipulation Exam


    

  • Michael Petrides proposed a functional dissociation within lateral PFC:
    • 

  • vlPFC → maintenance (delayed match-to-sample, simple "hold this letter"); activation roughly constant with load
    • 

  • dlPFC → manipulation (alphabetise, reorder, n-back); activation scales with cognitive demand (D'Esposito et al. 2000) Exam
    • 

  • Caveat from the presenters (and prof endorsement): the dissociation is real in broad strokes, but the boundary is fuzzier than a clean split — most real-world tasks engage both Prof
    • 



10.4 Distributed Network View Exam


    

  • WM is not localised — it emerges from a network of PFC + posterior parietal + sensory cortex
    • 

  • Content lives where it was originally processed: holding a face → fusiform activity persists; holding a spatial location → parietal spatial maps stay active
    • 

  • Harrison & Tong (2009): multivoxel pattern analysis decoded the orientation of a remembered grating from V1-V4 activity, even when behavioural performance was at chance Exam
    • 

  • PFC role: orchestrator — sends top-down signals back to sensory cortex to keep representations online when the stimulus is gone, but does not store the content itself Exam
    • 

  • Clinical dissociation: PFC damage impairs maintenance/control across the board; posterior damage produces content-specific deficits (e.g., spatial WM but not face WM)
    • 



Prof emphasis Prof
The prof commented this group "gave a masterclass on working through a graph" and singled out the take-away triplet: (1) WM = storage + operation; (2) Funahashi 1989 = sustained content-specific firing; (3) WM is a distributed network, not a single region. Memorise these three.






















Key Takeaways Exam



    

  1. Executive function = rules + control + working memory, all working together. No single region is the "executive"; PFC interacts with parietal cortex, basal ganglia, ACC, and thalamus to produce goal-directed behaviour.
    2. 

  2. PFC damage → two contrasting syndromes: lateral damage → dysexecutive (apathy, no planning, no initiation); ventromedial damage → disinhibition (impulsive, socially inappropriate, acquired sociopathy in severe cases). Phineas Gage = classic disinhibition; environmental dependency syndrome (Lhermitte) shows imitation + utilization behaviour.
    3. 

  3. Rules are encoded in distributed neuron populations in lateral PFC (Wallis, Anderson & Miller 2001) — rule-selective neurons fire for the rule itself, independent of cue or stimulus.
    4. 

  4. Inhibition has multiple forms tested by oddball (P300), go/no-go, and stop-signal tasks. Right vlPFC damage and ADHD both impair stop-signal performance; schizophrenia patients are normal on go but impaired on no-go.
    5. 

  5. Wisconsin Card Sorting tests shifting; PFC damage causes perseveration (Milner 1963) — not a learning deficit, but a failure to disengage from the old rule. OFC handles reversal learning. Frontopolar cortex is required for relating rules (Raven's matrices, Tower of London, relational integration).
    6. 

  6. Rostral-caudal gradient: posterior PFC = concrete, motor-near rules; anterior PFC = abstract, goal-distant rules. Two theories: Koechlin (temporal abstraction cascade) and Badre & D'Esposito (policy abstraction hierarchy). Same gradient is found in dmPFC (response → decision → strategy control; Venkatraman et al. 2009) and the medial-lateral surfaces are topographically connected (Taren et al. 2011).
    7. 

  7. ACC and conflict monitoring is the classic story (Stroop, Kerns et al. 2004; ERN), but challenged: ACC also responds to rewards/correct feedback, Fellows & Farah (2005) showed dorsal-ACC lesion patients have a normal Stroop effect, and Behrens et al. (2007) recasts dmPFC activity as tracking expectation mismatch / volatility — a learning signal more than a conflict detector.
    8. 

  8. Working memory = maintenance + manipulation of information no longer in the senses. Funahashi, Bruce & Goldman-Rakic (1989): dlPFC neurons show content-specific delay-period firing — the canonical neural mechanism. Petrides: vlPFC = maintenance, dlPFC = manipulation.
    9. 

  9. The Cowan model beats Baddeley at the neural level: WM content lives in the same sensory regions that processed it (decoded by Harrison & Tong 2009 from V1-V4). PFC orchestrates via top-down signals; it does not store content.
    10. 

  10. Researchers to know cold for the exam: Brodmann, Semendeferi, Hitzig & Fritsch, Bianchi, Phineas Gage / Damasio, Lhermitte, Miller & Cohen, Wallis, Milner, Goldstein & Scheerer, Koechlin, Badre & D'Esposito, Posner & Petersen, Bush, Kerns, Fellows & Farah, Behrens, Pochon, Venkatraman, Taren, Baddeley, Cowan, Funahashi & Goldman-Rakic, Petrides, D'Esposito, Harrison & Tong.
