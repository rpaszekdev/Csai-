Lesson Seven · slides 1–2

Every token, looking at every other token, all at once.

Last lesson the encoder squeezed an entire sentence into one final hidden state. The decoder then read from that single vector. It was a bottleneck. Self-attention removes the bottleneck — every token gets to look at every other token, in parallel, with no recurrence.

Slides by Dr. Juan Sebastián Olier
Department of Cognitive Sciences and AI · Tilburg University
Section 1 · slides 3–4

Where we left off

Recurrent networks process tokens one at a time. The hidden state at step t is computed from the input xt and the previous hidden state ht-1. To compute step 50 you must first compute steps 1 through 49. Sequential. Slow. Not parallelizable.

The encoder-decoder pattern made things worse: the entire input sequence had to fit inside the final encoder hidden state, a single fixed-size vector. The decoder then had to reconstruct the output from that one bottleneck vector.

Two problems with pure recurrence. First, training is slow because each step waits for the previous one. Second, long sequences lose information — by the time the encoder reaches token 50, traces of token 1 have been overwritten and squashed many times.

The animation shows the bottleneck: information flows left to right, gets crushed into a single state, then the decoder tries to recover it. Watch how the decoder gets only one cable from the encoder.

Quick check

Why can't we compute hidden states h1, h2, h3 in parallel in a vanilla RNN?

Each hidden state is a function of the previous one. That is a strict data dependency. No matter how many cores you have, you must wait.

Section 2 · slides 5–8

Attention inside an RNN

The first patch was to keep the recurrence but let each step peek at all the earlier ones. Instead of relying on a single hidden state, every step computes a weighted sum over all previous hidden states. The weights say which past steps matter most for the current one.

This was a real improvement, but it kept two limits. All the information is still routed through hidden states built one after another. And intermediate steps cannot pay attention to future inputs — they have not been computed yet.

The question on slide 7. Why can step 3 not pay attention to step 5? Because in a recurrent encoder, step 5 is computed after step 3. So step 3 only sees the past. That is a real handicap when the meaning of an early word depends on a later one.

The heatmap on the right shows attention weights inside an RNN encoder. Each row is a query timestep; each column is a key timestep. The lower-left triangle lights up — earlier steps cannot see future ones.

Quick check

If we drop the recurrent connections entirely, what becomes possible?

Slide 8 makes this point exactly: remove the recurrent connections, process every input in parallel. That is the seed of the Transformer.

Section 3 · slides 14–18

Self-attention — the core idea

Self-attention treats representation as a retrieval task. For each token, you ask: given my current state, which other tokens should I read from, and how strongly?

Every token plays three roles at once:

  • It produces a query q — what it is looking for.
  • It produces a key k — a label saying what it offers.
  • It produces a value v — the content it would hand over if asked.

Take token i's query, dot-product it with every other token's key. The bigger the dot product, the better the match. Run those scores through a softmax so they form a probability distribution. Then take the weighted sum of all the values. That is the new representation of token i.

Slide 18 walks the example. The two words Deep and Learning become embeddings e1, e2. Each gets a q, k, v. Score q1·k1 and q1·k2. Softmax gives weights s1, s2. Output for token 1 is s1·v1 + s2·v2.

Click any token on the right. Watch its query light up, its dot-products with every key compute, the softmax normalize them, and the weighted sum of values produce the output.

Section 4 · slides 15, 19–20

Q, K, V are three learned projections

Where do the queries, keys, and values come from? They are not three separate inputs. They are three different views of the same input, produced by three weight matrices.

Take an input vector x (the token embedding). Multiply it by:

  • WQ to get the query q = WQ x
  • WK to get the key k = WK x
  • WV to get the value v = WV x

The matrices are learned during training. Nothing tells the network what a query should look like — it figures out, by minimising the loss, what kind of query and key are useful for this task.

Why three matrices? If we used the input itself as both query and key, every token would always match itself most strongly and the attention would collapse into the identity. The separate projections let the network learn a different similarity geometry for searching versus offering content.
In matrix form for the whole sequence: stack the inputs into a matrix X, then Q = X WQ, K = X WK, V = X WV. Three matrix multiplies and you have everything you need.

Section 5 · slides 21–24

The full attention formula

Put the pieces together. For one whole sequence, scaled dot-product attention is:

// scaled dot-product attention Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( Q · KT / √dk ) · V

Read it left to right. Q · KT is a matrix of raw similarity scores: row i, column j is how much token i's query matches token j's key. Divide by √dk, where dk is the dimension of each key. Apply softmax along each row so the weights sum to one. Multiply by V to take the weighted average of the values.

Why divide by √dk? If q and k have dimension dk and their entries are roughly unit-variance and independent, then the dot product q · k has variance proportional to dk. Without the scaling, large dk would push the softmax into a region where one entry dominates and the gradient vanishes. Dividing by √dk keeps the variance roughly constant.

Drag the slider. At low temperature (large scaling) the softmax is sharp — almost one-hot. At high temperature (no scaling, large dk) the distribution either flattens out or collapses onto a single token. Both extremes hurt training.

Quick check

If dk = 64, what does the formula divide the scores by?

The scaling factor is the square root of the key dimension. √64 = 8.

Section 6 · slides 25–27

Multi-head attention

One attention layer learns one notion of relevance. But language has many: subject–verb agreement, coreference, syntactic role, semantic similarity. Multi-head attention runs several attention computations in parallel, each with its own WQ, WK, WV.

Each head projects the input into a smaller subspace, computes attention there, and produces its own output. The outputs of all heads are concatenated and multiplied by one final matrix WO to mix them back together.

// h heads, each with its own Q, K, V projection headi = Attention(X · WQi, X · WKi, X · WVi) MultiHead(X) = Concat(head1, …, headh) · WO
Different heads, different relationships. When you visualise attention from a trained Transformer you find heads that lock onto syntactic patterns, heads that track repeated tokens, heads that look at the previous word, heads that scan the whole sentence. The model picks up these specialisations during training — none of them are designed in.

The four heatmaps on the right are four different attention heads on the same six-token sequence. Each has a different bias: head 1 is local, head 2 looks at the first token, head 3 is uniform, head 4 has a few sharp spikes. None of these are hand-designed — they are the kind of patterns a trained model produces.

Section 7 · slides 28–30

The Transformer block

One encoder block wraps multi-head self-attention in a small recipe that is the same in every Transformer:

  1. Take the embedded inputs (with position information added).
  2. Run multi-head self-attention.
  3. Add the input back (a residual connection) and apply LayerNorm.
  4. Run a small feed-forward network, applied to each position independently.
  5. Add another residual and another LayerNorm.

That is one block. You stack N of them. The original paper used N = 6 blocks for the encoder.

LayerNorm, not BatchNorm. Slide 30 spells this out: statistics are taken over the last dimension. Normalising over features within a single token (rather than across the batch) makes Transformers robust to varying sequence length and small batch sizes — exactly the regime they live in.
Why the residual? It gives the gradient an unobstructed path back through the stack. The block then only has to learn the change it wants to apply, not the whole representation. The same trick that makes ResNets trainable makes deep Transformers trainable.

Section 8 · slides 35–43

Masked self-attention — the decoder's rule

An encoder is allowed to look at the whole input. A decoder generates one token at a time and must predict the next word from the words it has already produced. If it could peek at future tokens during training, the task would be trivial — just copy the next position. So we forbid it.

The fix is a causal mask. Compute the score matrix Q · KT as usual. Before the softmax, add a mask that puts −∞ on every entry above the diagonal — every position that points to the future. After e−∞ = 0, those entries contribute nothing to the softmax sum.

// causal mask: zeros below and on the diagonal, −∞ above scores = Q · KT / √dk masked = scores + M // M[i,j] = −∞ if j > i else 0 weights = softmax(masked) // rows still sum to 1 output = weights · V
Slide 35 spells out the training pair. To learn the sentence "The cat sat on the mat.", the model sees input <SOS> The cat sat on the and is asked to predict the target The cat sat on the mat <EOS>. The mask makes sure each prediction position only sees the tokens up to and including itself.

Click Apply mask. The upper triangle of the score grid floods with deep red −∞ entries. After softmax, those squares go to zero. Row 1 ends up with all its weight on token 1, row 2 splits between tokens 1–2, and so on — exactly slide 43.

Section 9 · slides 30, 32

Positional encoding

Self-attention is permutation-invariant. Shuffle the tokens of the input and the attention output simply shuffles the same way. That is wrong for language: "the cat ate the fish" is not "the fish ate the cat".

The fix is to add positional information directly to the embedding. Each position p gets its own vector PEp, and the input to the first block becomes x = embedding(token) + PEp.

The original Transformer used a fixed sinusoidal encoding:

PE(p, 2i) = sin( p / 100002i/d ) PE(p, 2i+1) = cos( p / 100002i/d )

Different dimensions oscillate at different frequencies. Low dimensions change slowly across positions; high dimensions change quickly. The model can read absolute and relative position from this fingerprint. Modern Transformers often replace the formula with learned positional embeddings or relative-position schemes — but the principle is the same: position must be injected.

Why this works. Sums of sines and cosines at multiple frequencies form a kind of multi-resolution clock. The dot product between PEp and PEq depends only on p − q — so the model can in principle learn relative offsets directly.

The heatmap on the right plots PE for positions 0–63 (rows) across 32 dimensions (columns). Notice how the leftmost columns sweep slowly while the rightmost columns oscillate quickly.

Check yourself

Q1. What is the main reason recurrent encoders are hard to parallelize?

The recurrent step ht = f(xt, ht-1) creates a strict data dependency chain. Self-attention drops it.

Q2. In self-attention, the query, key, and value for a token come from:

The same vector x is multiplied by WQ, WK, WV to produce three different views.

Q3. The attention scores are computed as:

Score(i, j) = qi · kj. The result is then scaled by √dk and softmaxed.

Q4. Why divide the scores by √dk before the softmax?

Without scaling, large dk produces huge logits, the softmax saturates, and the gradient vanishes.

Q5. What does multi-head attention give you that single-head does not?

Each head has its own Q, K, V projection and learns a different relationship — syntactic, positional, semantic.

Q6. Inside a Transformer block, what comes immediately after multi-head self-attention?

Add & Norm: residual to keep the gradient path open, LayerNorm to stabilise statistics. Then the position-wise feed-forward network.

Q7. What does the causal mask in masked self-attention contain?

Adding −∞ to future positions before softmax forces them to e−∞ = 0 after softmax, so they contribute nothing.

Q8. Why do Transformers need positional encoding?

Without recurrence or convolution, attention treats the sequence as a set. Positional encoding injects order back in.

Q9. In sinusoidal positional encoding, low dimensions oscillate __ across positions and high dimensions oscillate __.

The frequency depends on 100002i/d. Small i gives a long period (slow), large i gives a short period (fast).

Q10. A decoder cannot attend to future tokens during training because:

Future tokens are known during teacher-forced training. The mask ensures the model still predicts each position from only the past — that's what it must do at inference time.
