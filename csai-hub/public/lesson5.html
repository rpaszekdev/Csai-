Section 1 · slides 2, 5

The model that knew too much

Give a flexible model enough freedom, and it will fit your training data perfectly. Every point, every wiggle, every bit of noise.

Then you hand it a new example. It fails. Badly.

The network did not learn the underlying pattern. It learned the specific training set, quirks and all. This is overfitting.

Regularization is any technique that closes the gap between training error and test error. It trades a small increase in bias for a larger drop in variance — and the result is a model that generalizes.

Section 2 · slides 3–5

The bias–variance tradeoff

Two failure modes live at opposite ends of model complexity.

High bias (underfitting) — the model is too simple. Even if you gave it more data, it could not fit the true pattern.

High variance (overfitting) — the model is too flexible. Change the training set slightly and the learned function changes a lot.

You want the sweet spot in the middle: a model flexible enough to capture the truth, constrained enough to ignore noise.

Each frame on the right trains a fresh model on a newly sampled training set. Watch how the three capacity regimes behave differently: simple models cluster around the wrong answer (bias); very flexible models scatter widely (variance).

In practice: you detect this by splitting your data into train, validation, and test. High train accuracy plus low validation accuracy is the fingerprint of overfitting.

Section 3 · slide 6

Penalize the weights directly

The simplest regularizer: add a term to the loss that punishes large weights.

Ltotal = Ldata + λ · R(W)

Two common choices:

  • L2 penalty — R(W) = Σ wi². Also called weight decay. Shrinks every weight smoothly toward zero. In PyTorch: optim.SGD(..., weight_decay=1e-4).
  • L1 penalty — R(W) = Σ |wi|. Has a corner at the axes, so the optimum often snaps exactly to zero for some weights. Produces sparsity — automatic feature selection.
Why the difference? On the right: the blue contours are the data loss. The grey shape is the budget — how far the weights are allowed to roam. The solution sits where the contour first touches the budget. L1's diamond has sharp corners sitting on the axes, so the touch happens there. L2's round disk has no corners — it touches somewhere general.

Slide the λ control to expand the budget. Larger λ = more regularization = smaller weights.

Section 4 · slides 7–8

Early stopping

Train a model for many epochs and you will see two curves:

  • Training loss keeps falling — the model keeps memorizing.
  • Validation loss falls for a while, then starts rising — the model has begun to overfit.

The fix is obvious once you see it: stop training at the moment validation loss bottoms out. Keep the weights from that epoch, throw away the rest.

Patience. Real training curves are noisy. You cannot stop the first time validation nudges up — it might dip again. A patience of say 10 epochs means: stop only if validation has not improved for 10 straight epochs. That is the hyperparameter you tune.

Press Train — the dashed vertical line shows the best-validation epoch. After that the gap opens up: pure memorization.

Section 5 · slides 10–12

Data augmentation

The best regularizer is more data. When you cannot get more, you fabricate it — by applying label-preserving transformations.

For images: flip, crop, rotate, scale, shift colors, add noise. A flipped cat is still a cat. A slightly rotated cat is still a cat. The network sees new examples without you collecting any.

Not every transform is safe. Flip a 6 and it becomes a 9. Flip a medical scan horizontally and the left lung ends up on the right. The transformations must preserve the label — they depend on what you are classifying.

Augmentation is usually done on the fly, not by making copies on disk. Each time a sample loads, a fresh random transform is applied — so the network sees virtually infinite variations.

Adversarial training is a relative: deliberately add tiny, carefully chosen noise that fools the current model, then train on those examples. The result is a model robust to small perturbations.

Section 6 · slides 14–18

Dropout

At each training step, randomly turn off a fraction of the neurons. Set their activation to zero. The rest of the network has to solve the task without them.

Next step: a different random set gets turned off. And again. And again.

Why it works. The network cannot rely on any single neuron being present — so it learns redundant, distributed features. You can also read it as training an ensemble of exponentially many sub-networks, all sharing weights.

Implementation is a one-liner: for each neuron, sample a Bernoulli variable with keep-probability 1 − p. Multiply the activation by it.

Train vs. test. At test time you want all neurons active. But then each neuron receives more signal than during training. To match, either scale down at test time by (1 − p), or — more common — scale up during training by 1 / (1 − p). This is called inverted dropout, and it is why model.eval() just turns the dropout layer off.

Dropout is typically used in the fully-connected part of a network, not directly on convolutional feature maps (their spatial correlations make random pixel drops less useful).

Section 7 · slides 20–23

Capacity and the double descent surprise

A model's capacity is the range of functions it can represent. More neurons, more layers, more parameters → higher capacity.

Classical statistics says: too much capacity means overfitting, so test error should climb forever after the sweet spot.

The surprise. Very large neural networks — the kind with more parameters than training examples — break the rule. After the classical overfitting spike, test error drops again. This is double descent (Belkin et al., 2019).

The peak sits at the interpolation threshold — the capacity that just barely fits every training point. Go bigger, and the network finds simpler, smoother solutions among the many that interpolate the data.

Takeaway. In deep learning, the safest move when overfitting is often not to shrink the model — it is to make the model larger and regularize it. This is how modern giant language models work.

Section 8 · slides 26–35

Batch normalization

We already know it helps to normalize the input to a network — zero mean, unit variance. But as weights change during training, the activations of every hidden layer drift too. Each layer sees a distribution that keeps shifting under its feet.

This is internal covariate shift, and it slows learning down.

The fix. For every mini-batch, at every layer, compute the mean μ and standard deviation σ of the pre-activations. Subtract μ, divide by σ. The distribution is now centered and scaled.
ẑ = (z − μ) / σ    →    y = γ · ẑ + β

But forcing every layer to zero-mean unit-variance may be too rigid. So batch norm also learns two parameters per feature: γ (scale) and β (shift). The layer can choose any mean and variance it wants — but it must learn them, instead of drifting passively.

At inference there is no batch. So during training the layer also tracks a running average of μ and σ, and uses that frozen pair when you run model.eval().

Other normalizers exist: LayerNorm (normalize across features within one sample — used in Transformers), InstanceNorm (style transfer), GroupNorm (small batches).

Check yourself

Q1. A model has 99% train accuracy and 62% test accuracy. The most likely cause is:

Near-perfect train but poor test is the textbook signature of overfitting. The model memorized the training set instead of learning the underlying pattern.

Q2. The key difference between L1 and L2 regularization is:

The L1 "budget" has corners on the axes, so the optimum often sits exactly at an axis — meaning one weight becomes zero. L2's round ball has no corners, so it just shrinks weights toward zero smoothly.

Q3. In early stopping, we stop training when:

Training loss basically always keeps dropping. It is the validation curve that tells you when you are starting to overfit. Patience protects you from stopping too early on a noisy dip.

Q4. Why is flipping digit images horizontally a bad idea?

Augmentations must be label-preserving. A flipped cat is still a cat, so it is safe. A flipped "6" is a "9", so the model would learn the wrong mapping.

Q5. During training with dropout at rate p = 0.5, inverted dropout:

Half the neurons are off, so the expected total input is halved. To compensate, kept activations are scaled up by 1/(1−p) during training — then no scaling is needed at test time.

Q6. Double descent means:

Classic theory predicts test error should keep rising with capacity. In deep networks it rises, peaks where the model just barely fits all training data, then falls again. Over-parameterization plus implicit regularization is the current best explanation.

Q7. Batch norm's learnable γ and β exist because:

Pure standardization is too rigid. γ and β give the network the flexibility to rescale and shift back — while still keeping the activations under learned control.

Q8. Which normalizer is preferred in Transformers?

Transformers process sequences of varying length with often small effective batches, where BN's batch statistics are unreliable. LayerNorm normalizes across features within a single sample and is batch-size independent.
